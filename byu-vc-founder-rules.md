# BYU Rules on VC Money and Founder Involvement

What BYU's official policies actually say about faculty/staff interacting with venture-backed companies — at arm's length, as a named co-founder without monetary ties, or as an equity-holding founder. Compiled from the primary policy documents (quoted verbatim where it matters) to complement the [survey of enterprise/academic precedents](enterprise-academic-precedents.md). See [PR #156 discussion](https://github.com/vertical-cloud-lab/byu-vcl/pull/156).

> **Not legal advice.** Policies quoted were current as of July 2026 (see [access notes](#access-caveats)). For a real situation, the deciding offices are the department chair/dean, the Research Administration Office (RAO), the Technology Transfer Office (TTO), and the Associate Academic Vice President – Research and Graduate Studies (AAVP–R).

## The three postures, side by side

### 1. Arm's length (no founder role, no equity)

A VC-backed company sponsoring research, paying for lab access, or engaging a faculty member as a consultant — with no ownership or officer role on the BYU side.

- **Sponsored research:** faculty may not negotiate the deal. The Sponsored Projects Handbook: "only those authorized by the university can and should conduct negotiations. At BYU only RAO, Technology Transfer/Creative Works, and General Counsel have such responsibility." Proposals require chair + dean approval and are signed by the AAVP–R or designee. Indirect cost rate is 51.5% MTDC (DHHS-negotiated, effective Jan 2022), collected in full absent a central-administration exemption.
- **Publication:** "Externally funded research should not be undertaken that constitutes work for hire, is classified, or presents permanent restrictions on faculty publication of the research." Short sponsor-driven delays are permitted; permanent restrictions are not. Student publication rights are explicitly protected.
- **Consulting for the company:** allowed, but it is "Secondary Employment" requiring **prior written chair approval**, capped at "up to four days per month... while under contract," with scope communicated to chair and dean in writing before starting. If approved activities happen during the workweek, "there is an inherent expectation that the faculty member... will devote equivalent time on adjacent weekends and/or in the evenings to compensate." The BYU name "shall not be used in the consulting services, in any advertising, or in any other way," and a faculty member "may either act as a paid consultant to or participate in sponsored research for the same company but may not perform both at the same time" (rare exceptions require an approved management plan + AAVP–R written approval).
- **Money into the lab:** VC or corporate money funding research is a sponsored project through RAO; philanthropic money is a gift through the Development Office/LDS Philanthropies (no indirect costs, no deliverables). These channels are deliberately separate, and the University Fund Raising Policy hardens the gift side: "In general, only the BYU Development Office and deans should solicit funds, goods, or services from individuals or companies on behalf of BYU"; all corporate/foundation solicitation contacts run through Development's Directors of Foundation and Corporate Relations, with prospective donors cleared through a Church-wide Name Clearance Committee; individuals "may not solicit or accept gifts not on the approved fund-raising list"; "the University will not accept research funds or donated funds that will be administered by the donor"; and conditional gifts, "where the donor expects to receive some special service or research in exchange for the gift, are contrary to University and tax regulations" absent Advancement/Academic VP approval. In short: the gift channel cannot carry strings, and researchers cannot work it themselves.
- **Pay above base salary:** the Supplemental Compensation for Faculty Policy closes the side door — full-time faculty "may not receive additional income from university or external sources for any work performed during the time period covered by the full-time appointment" except as policy specifically allows. Sponsored-project summer salary is capped at 12.5% of the eight-month base per month from external sources (11.1% on NIH/NSF grants), within an 11-months-of-effort/year ceiling; paid private instruction and similar stipends count against the same 4-days/month outside-activity cap.
- **Supplemental research compensation is deliberately narrow.** The RAO's Supplemental Compensation Procedures (Nov 2011) allow extra pay from a sponsored project during the appointment only when the work is "across departmental lines" — the faculty member "is neither principal investigator, co-principal investigator, co-investigator, or listed as a participant in the proposal," and must be from a *different department* than the project team. In other words, **a PI cannot top up their own salary from their own grant during the academic year**; a sponsor wanting to pay a PI more can only do so through capped summer salary. The mechanism further requires the sponsor to have specified the compensation in the budget/scope (or authorize it in writing), prior written approval from five parties (project PI, chair, dean, RAO, and AAVP–R) via the "Approval for Professional Activities in Addition to Regular University Assignment" form, a maximum of one day per week, no work during buy-out periods or outside the contract appointment, pay capped at the regular daily rate (eight-month base ÷ 1,386 hours × 8), payment in arrears within 90 days, and auditable time records.
- **Perks from sponsors/vendors:** the Business Gifts and Entertainment Policy allows only gifts of "nominal value" given "without the intent of influencing the recipient's objectivity" — no cash or cash equivalents, no extravagant gifts, and "University personnel (or members of their families) may not solicit gifts." Supplier-paid event tickets are acceptable only for a legitimate business purpose.
- **FCOI:** no equity or significant payments → no Significant Financial Interest → nothing beyond routine annual disclosure.

**Bottom line:** this is the frictionless posture. Everything runs through standing university channels; the individual researcher's only obligations are disclosure and the consulting approval/time limits.

### 2. Listed co-founder with no monetary ties (no equity, no compensation)

Being named a co-founder, officer, or director — even unpaid — is **not** exempt from the rules; it's the role, not the money, that triggers them.

- **Prior written authorization required.** A full-time employee must get written authorization from chair/dean/supervisor to "operate a business, serve as an officer (in title or in fact) of a business, or maintain employment with a business that requires attention during the employee's assigned work hours" (Conflict of Time or Interest Policy). "In title or in fact" is doing work here: a nominal co-founder title counts. The definition of Secondary Employment is equally broad — "any employment or paid work, including self-employment... operating a business in any capacity."
- **Disclosure comes before the activity, then annually.** Outside Activities (which include service on for-profit boards) must be disclosed *before* engaging in them, then re-disclosed every year via the Faculty Outside Activities Form in the Annual Review section of the Faculty Profile System. If a conflict is identified, the faculty member and chair/dean write a management plan; the dean approves and submits it through the same system, and chairs/deans "do not approve activities they believe would unduly limit a faculty member's availability to students or compromise... teaching, scholarship, or citizenship performance."
- **No line-officer roles while full-time.** The RAO Sponsored Projects Handbook is blunt: "Faculty members not on leave are not to be line officers in outside companies." The legacy Expectations of a Faculty Appointment policy classifies "maintaining a private business" as an Area 3 (conflicting) activity, and its worked example resolves a faculty-founder-president case by **leave of absence until the company can be handed to a new president, after which the faculty member returns with an advisory-only board role**.
- **"Chief Science Officer" is presumptively a line-officer title.** Neither BYU document defines "line officer," but in ordinary management usage a *line* officer sits in the chain of command with operational authority over people and functions (CEO, COO, president, a CSO who runs the R&D organization — hiring, supervising, owning deliverables), whereas *staff* roles advise without commanding. A founder-CSO in the executive suite is squarely a line officer; only a purely honorific CSO title with advisory substance would arguably fall outside the term. At BYU, though, the distinction barely matters, because the Conflict of Time or Interest Policy triggers on the title alone: authorization is required to serve "as an officer **(in title or in fact)** of a business" — so any C-suite designation needs prior written chair/dean approval even if the duties are advisory. Peer policies draw the same line explicitly: [Penn State AC80](https://policy.psu.edu/policies/ac80) lists "assuming an executive or management position for a third party entity (e.g., as President, Chief Scientific Officer, etc.)" among activities needing prior approval, and [Stanford's COI policy](https://doresearch.stanford.edu/policies/research-policy-handbook/conflicts-commitment-and-interest/faculty-policy-conflict-commitment-and-interest) bars full-time faculty from "line management or significant managerial responsibilities, or titles that suggest or connote such responsibilities" ("roles or titles such as CEO, COO, CIO, 'director', 'officer' or 'vice-president'") while expressly permitting "'advisor' or 'consultant,' e.g., 'Chief Scientific Advisor,' or 'Chief Technical Consultant.'" The BYU-compatible shape for a faculty founder who provides scientific direction is therefore the advisor title (the Coreform "senior advisor" precedent); NeuroID shows officer titles are survivable in practice — Jenkins was co-founder, CTO, later Chief Innovation Officer, and a board member while remaining a professor — but that path runs through disclosure, written authorization, and (with equity) a management plan, not around them. The largest live demonstration of the Stanford shape is [Physical Intelligence](enterprise-academic-precedents.md#physical-intelligence-professors-co-founding-a-vc-backed-startup-while-staying-in-post): Stanford's Chelsea Finn and Berkeley's Sergey Levine co-founded a robotics-AI company that has raised $1.1B+ at a $5.6B valuation while both remain active professors with labs and PhD students — and the CEO title went to a full-time, non-tenure-line co-founder from day one.
- **Board seats at BYU-IP licensees are tightly capped.** The IP Policy: voting board membership is allowed only "on rare occasions," and only if "(i) the company has not raised more than $3,000,000 and (ii) non-accredited investors are not involved" — with prior written approval from the dean, the AAVP–R, **and** the TTO. Note the first condition: a company that has raised a meaningful VC round is already over the $3M line.
- **Research disclosures still apply.** Even with zero equity/pay, the FCOI policy requires investigators to disclose any "management position with a Sponsor, vendor, or subcontractor of the program activity," defined to include "board member, director, officer, partner, trustee, employee, or consultant." Conducting research where a participant has a "managerial or consulting role" in the sponsor is on the policy's list of potential conflicts regardless of financial stake.

**Bottom line:** "co-founder without monetary ties" is permitted only as an *approved, disclosed, non-executive* role — advisor or (rarely, small-company) board member. A line-officer title (CEO/CTO/president), even unpaid, is what the policies push to a leave of absence.

### 3. Co-founder with monetary ties / equity

Everything in posture 2, plus the financial-interest machinery:

- **Any startup equity is a disclosable SFI.** For non-publicly-traded entities, an SFI exists when remuneration exceeds $5,000/year **"or when the Investigator['s or] Immediate Family Members hold any equity interest (e.g., stock, stock option, or other ownership interest)"** — no dollar floor, options count, spouse/dependent holdings count. Disclosure is due before submitting a proposal to an external sponsor and within 30 days of acquiring a new interest. (This mirrors the PHS rule; NSF's own floor is $10,000 or 5% ownership.)
- **The AAVP–R decides whether it's a conflict and can order divestiture.** Management-plan tools explicitly include public disclosure in publications, an independent monitor, modification of the research plan, personnel disqualification, "reduction or elimination of the financial interest (e.g., sale of an equity interest)," and "severance of relationships that create financial conflicts."
- **Above 10% ownership the company becomes a "Related Party."** Related Party covers "corporations and other business entities in which an employee has an ownership interest that exceeds 10 percent" (as well as the employee's immediate and extended family). The employee is then prohibited from "negotiating, influencing, or attempting to influence the negotiations of contracts or agreements between the university and a third party for the benefit of the employee or a Related Party" — i.e., a ≥10% founder cannot be on either side of the company's dealings with BYU.
- **At ≥10% ownership of a BYU-IP licensee, the founder forfeits their royalty share.** IP Policy: "Occasionally a Developer may become a principal equity owner [≥10%] of a company that licenses university Intellectual Property. In such cases, the developer will forfeit his or her distribution" — the inventor keeps the equity upside but gives up their 45% of BYU's licensing income. This is BYU's distinctive anti-double-dipping rule; it also implicitly confirms that licensing BYU IP to a founder-owned startup is a contemplated, allowed path.
- **BYU itself can take equity in the licensee.** "If the university receives equity in a company as a condition for a license or an assignment of Intellectual Property, then BYU will be the holder of such equity," with any proceeds run through the standard 45/27.5/27.5 split; "such equity will not be distributed to the Developer(s)."
- **Hard resource wall.** "University Personnel may not use university resources, facilities, or work time to engage in commercial activities, businesses, or 'start-up' companies." Using students/staff "to perform services for an outside entity in which the Investigator or the Investigator's Immediate Family has an equity ownership, managerial or consulting role, or SFI" is a listed potential conflict; work involving students or substantial facilities must be converted to a sponsored research agreement with cost reimbursement (this is where SBIR/STTR subawards fit).
- **Time and role limits unchanged:** still ≤4 days/month secondary employment with approval, still no line-officer role while full-time. The sanctioned full-commitment path is a personal leave or salary reduction, "a temporary solution" requiring AAVP–Faculty Development approval — the mechanics of having the company pay the salary instead are detailed in [the leave/buyout section below](#what-if-the-company-pays-the-salary-instead-of-byu).

**Bottom line:** an equity-holding faculty founder is allowed — and BYU's licensing pipeline assumes it — but inside a defined box: disclose everything, don't negotiate with BYU, don't touch university resources or students without a sponsored agreement, stay out of line-officer roles (or take leave), and accept the royalty forfeiture at ≥10%.

## When BYU itself holds the equity

What changes if the *university*, not just the founder, is on the cap table? Under the IP Policy this is already a contemplated outcome — equity is a form of license consideration, not a separate program — and it changes the economics and adds an institutional dimension **without relaxing any founder-side rule**.

- **The only sanctioned way BYU acquires equity is as licensing consideration.** "If the university receives equity in a company as a condition for a license or an assignment of Intellectual Property, then BYU will be the holder of such equity." There is no BYU venture fund or policy channel for investing university cash into a spinout ([Cougar Capital](https://marriott.byu.edu/mba/career/accelerated-learning/cougar-capital/) is a student-run, donation-based teaching fund, not a balance-sheet investor in BYU spinouts), so in practice "BYU holds equity" means the TTO negotiated shares in lieu of — or alongside — up-front fees and royalties, the standard cash-poor-startup trade and consistent with BYU's "service to faculty rather than a profit center" philosophy. Peer benchmarks for scale: [Stanford's OTL asks 1–5% for a license, Columbia takes 5% with anti-dilution through Series A](https://globalventuring.com/university/us-and-canada/us-university-spinouts-investing/), US license equity often carries anti-dilution until a funding threshold, and [practitioner guidance](https://www.zerocarbon.vc/news/spinouts-and-success-a-vc-view-on-spinout-equity) is that a university should stay under ~10% (lower if it also charges royalties) — VCs tolerate small passive university positions and treat large ones as ["dead equity"](https://www.ifm.eng.cam.ac.uk/research/uci-policy-unit/uci-news/uci-report-on-university-approaches-to-spinout-equity/) that blocks later rounds. The BYU policy sets no percentage and grants BYU no board seat.
- **Inventors get cash later, not shares now.** Income from BYU-held equity runs through the standard split — "Any income BYU receives from such equity will be distributed pursuant to the income distribution described above" — but "such equity will not be distributed to the Developer(s)." So the Developer's 45% arrives only if and when BYU realizes proceeds (exit, buyback, dividends), as cash through the royalty waterfall, on BYU's timing. Contrast [Stanford, which distributes one-third of net equity to inventors as shares](https://doresearch.stanford.edu/policies/research-policy-handbook/conflicts-commitment-and-interest/equity-acquisition-technology-licensing-and-distance-learning-agreements) that "it shall be the sole responsibility of the Inventor(s) to manage." And the forfeiture rule stacks: a founder who personally holds ≥10% of the company has already forfeited their distribution — **including their 45% of BYU's eventual equity proceeds**. A ≥10% founder's upside comes solely from their own shares; a sub-10% inventor, by contrast, gets both their own equity and a 45% share of BYU's.
- **Nothing on the founder side is relaxed — if anything the recusal wall gets taller.** The license that grants BYU its equity is a contract "between the university and a third party," so a >10% founder (Related Party) may not negotiate or influence it from either side: the TTO sits across the table from the company, and the founder steps out of the room. Disclosure, management plans, the resource wall, the 4-days/month cap, the officer-title rules, and the $3M board-seat cap all apply unchanged — the board-seat cap is in fact written specifically for "a company that licenses university Intellectual Property," i.e., exactly the company BYU may hold equity in.
- **What it adds is an *institutional* conflict of interest — and BYU has no written policy for that.** Once BYU is a shareholder, the university itself has a financial stake in research outcomes on its own campus. BYU's FCOI policy governs only individual "Investigators" and contains no provisions for the university's own interests; the [federal FCOI regulations](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/42/50.603) likewise regulate individuals, leaving institutional COI to self-governance ([COGR's principles document](https://www.cogr.edu/sites/default/files/COGR%20COI%20Principles%20document%20for%20formatting%20march%2030%202023%20formatted.pdf) is the sector framework). The national reference points: the [AAMC/AAU "rebuttable presumption"](https://www.aamc.org/media/24266/download) against conducting *human-subjects* research at an institution holding a financial interest in the sponsor absent compelling circumstances ([background](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK22934/)), and structural separation of equity management from research oversight — at Stanford, "all equity received by the University will be managed by Stanford Management Company" (not the licensing office or the host school), and when Stanford invests, ["no Stanford officer is to be a member of the board, or be an officer of the company, or have a personal equity position in the company"](https://doresearch.stanford.edu/policies/research-policy-handbook/conflicts-commitment-and-interest/university-investments-start-companies-involving-stanford-faculty). VCL work is unlikely to be human-subjects, so the hard AAMC presumption won't usually bite; the soft expectations will: disclose BYU's stake where relevant (publications, proposals), and expect heightened scrutiny of the closed loop where the company BYU part-owns also sponsors research, places equipment, or funds students in the founder's own lab — the maximal conflict stack (sponsor + licensee + university-shareholder + founder-PI). With no institutional-COI mechanism on the books, the AAVP–R would have to manage that stack through the individual-side management plan.
- **Whether BYU actually exercises the clause is not public.** License terms are confidential, and no public record shows whether BYU took equity in NeuroID, Coreform, or the other precedents. "How often does BYU take equity, and at what percentage and share class?" is already effectively on the [question list for Mike Alder (TTO)](byu-founder-contacts.md#what-to-ask-them) (Q4).

**Bottom line:** BYU-held equity mostly helps the company — it substitutes for cash a startup doesn't have and aligns BYU's incentives with the company's success rather than with maximal royalties — at the cost of one more passive line on the cap table and an institutional-conflict dimension BYU's policies don't yet regulate. It changes who benefits and when (BYU holds the shares; inventors see cash only at exit, and only if they hold <10% of the company themselves), but it loosens none of the founder rules: the §2–§3 box is identical whether or not BYU is on the cap table.

## Company-owned equipment in a PI's lab

Can a company's equipment reside in a PI's BYU lab and be used by their students? **Yes — this is an established, routine practice, but only under a written agreement signed by an authorized university office, and the choice of channel is forced by whether the company keeps title.** There are three sanctioned shapes:

1. **Gift-in-kind (title transfers to BYU).** The company donates the equipment outright. The BYU [Asset Management Policy Manual](https://spo.byu.edu/00000190-3675-df15-a1f9-7675d38f0001/asset-management-policy-manual) (Asset Management Office/ORCA): equipment gifts over $5,000 are assigned an asset number, and "the Asset Management Office will record no gift until a 'Gift Receipt' is completed by the LDS Philanthropies. All gifts must be coordinated through the LDS Philanthropies." The Fund Raising Policy's rules then bite: no conditional gifts, no donor-administered funds — so a "donation" where the company retains ownership, retains pull-back rights, or expects data/services back **is not a gift and cannot be papered as one**. Once donated, it's university equipment, and students use it like any other lab instrument.
2. **Equipment loan / bailment (company keeps title).** This is the standard mechanism when the company wants the equipment back — universally used for vendor demo/beta placements and industry collaborations. Peer offices describe the shape precisely: at [Stanford](https://ico.stanford.edu/stanford-researchers/agreement-types/equipment-loans), "Companies may offer to lend specialized equipment to Stanford laboratories for research purposes at no cost," the Industrial Contracts Office "negotiates and signs agreements with the lending company," and "Investigators should not sign the agreement"; whether "other researchers [may] use equipment loaned to my lab" and whether research funded by another company may run on it "depends on the terms of the ELA." [Boston University](https://www.bu.edu/research/collaboration-partnership/industry-collaboration/equipment-loan-agreements/) likewise requires "an authorized institutional signature" and flags export-control/ITAR review as a standard ELA term. At BYU the signing-authority rule already quoted above does the same work — only RAO/Technology Transfer/General Counsel negotiate and sign agreements with outside entities, so **the PI cannot sign the loan agreement**. The Asset Management Policy Manual's framework for non-owned equipment: BYU has custodial responsibility for equipment it doesn't own only "to the extent that it has explicitly agreed to accept responsibility thereof"; equipment "title to which is not vested in the University but which is in the care and custody of the University, may not be subsequently loaned without the specific written approval of the titleholder"; and (by analogy to the personally-owned-equipment rule) non-owned equipment on campus should be reported to the custodial department and "identified as the property of the owner," with BYU "not responsible for loss of or damage" absent agreement. [Risk Management and Safety](https://risk.byu.edu/insurance) can insure "owned, leased, borrowed, or rented property or equipment" and issue evidence of coverage — who insures and maintains the machine is a negotiated term of the agreement, not an assumption.
3. **Sponsor-furnished equipment under a sponsored research agreement.** If the placement is part of a funded project, it's handled inside the SRA: the Asset Manual records "agency-furnished and University-purchased equipment acquired under a contract" in the inventory, identified with the contract number, with ORCA notifying Asset Management of any title transfers and their conditions; the Sponsored Projects Handbook tells PIs "to know sponsor's feelings about ownership and retention of equipment after projects are completed."

**Student use is the easy part — *whose work* the students do on it is the hard part.** Once the equipment is under a gift or a university-signed loan/SRA, students using it for their BYU research is exactly the intended purpose. The traps are all on the flip side:

- **Direction of benefit.** Students running their own university research on a company's loaned machine is fine; students operating that machine to produce data, services, or product development *for the company* runs into the rules already quoted above — using students "to perform services for an outside entity" in which the PI has an interest is a listed FCOI conflict, and the resource wall requires converting such work into a sponsored research agreement with cost reimbursement. A loaned instrument does not buy the company student labor.
- **If the PI has equity or a role in the company** (postures 2–3 above), the placement is part of a relationship that must be disclosed and may need a management plan — and a >10% owner is a Related Party who cannot negotiate or influence the loan agreement between BYU and their own company.
- **Federal disclosure.** On NIH awards, in-kind resources from an outside organization — explicitly including "office/laboratory space, equipment, supplies" — must be reported: in Facilities & Other Resources if used on the proposed project, or in the PI's Other Support otherwise ([NIH Other Support rules, NOT-OD-21-073](https://sites.wustl.edu/engineeringresearchtoolkit/funding/proposal-development/proposal-elements/nih-other-support/)). A quietly placed company instrument is a disclosure item, not a private arrangement.
- **Export control and access.** Equipment embodying controlled technology, or company-imposed use/access restrictions, triggers export-control review (a standard ELA checklist item at peer institutions; at BYU, export-controlled work is screened through RAO, with the [Office of Research Computing](https://rc.byu.edu/wiki/?id=Protected+Data) as the campus entity for controlled data). Restrictions on who may use the machine or see its outputs can also erode the lab's fundamental-research posture, so they belong in the negotiated agreement, not in side understandings. Likewise, ongoing company access to the lab (keys, remote monitoring, on-site personnel) should be written into the agreement.
- **The gift-vs-loan line cannot be blurred.** A company offering "free" equipment to the PI personally is capped by the Business Gifts and Entertainment Policy (nominal value only); equipment of real value goes to the *university* through one of the three channels above. And a gift with retained strings violates the Fund Raising Policy — if the company wants title, access, or deliverables, the honest instrument is a loan agreement or an SRA.

**For VCL this is a load-bearing pattern:** vendor-placed instruments are precisely how a cloud lab could bootstrap capacity (the CMU–Emerald Cloud Lab arrangement in the [precedents survey](enterprise-academic-precedents.md) is a scaled-up version), and the policy path is well-worn — route the placement through RAO as a loan agreement or SRA, decide insurance/maintenance/access up front, and keep any company-directed use of the equipment (and of students' time) on the sponsored-project side of the wall.

## What if the company pays the salary instead of BYU?

There are exactly four sanctioned shapes, ordered by how much of the BYU salary the company replaces — and one shape that does not exist: **a company cannot simply pay a full-time BYU faculty member's salary directly while they remain full-time faculty.** The Supplemental Compensation policy (§1 above) bars extra income "from university or external sources for any work performed during the time period covered by the full-time appointment," so during the 8-month contract, company money can reach a faculty paycheck only *through* BYU. The real options:

1. **Company funds the salary, BYU still pays it (buyout through a sponsored agreement).** The company's money enters as a sponsored project via RAO, and a slice of the faculty member's effort — and the salary behind it — is charged to it. The Sponsored Projects Handbook describes the mechanism and then all but closes it for the academic year: faculty at many universities "may 'buy out' some of their regular teaching assignments in favor of doing sponsored research. This practice substitutes sponsor funds for normal university funds... **At BYU, this practice is limited to 6 academic year Full Time Equivalents (FTEs) for faculty** and 40 FTEs (calendar year) for other employees," because "it is intended that all regular faculty be appointed to a full-time contract." Six FTEs is a *campus-wide* annual allocation, not a per-person one — academic-year buyout at BYU is a scarce exception requiring RAO consultation, not a routine budget line. By contrast, "there are no FTE limits during Spring/Summer terms": summer is the open season, which is why the standard shape is company-sponsored summer salary (capped at 12.5% of the 8-month base per month, 11.1% on NIH/NSF, total effort ≤ 11 months/year). Either way the pay rate is BYU's, not the company's — the daily rate is the 8-month base ÷ 1,386 hours × 8, no premium — the money passes through the 51.5% MTDC indirect rate, and if the faculty member holds equity in the sponsoring company, the FCOI machinery of §3 (disclosure, management plan, possible disqualification from the project) sits on top.
2. **Partial leave / salary reduction (fractional company employment).** The Conflict of Time or Interest Policy names "personal leave or salary reduction" as the sanctioned "temporary solution" for outside commitments beyond the 4-days/month cap, with AAVP–Faculty Development approval. The Faculty Leaves Policy's implementing shape is the **partial personal leave**: "a proportional reduction in salary and faculty assignments" with the same approval chain as a full leave — but it is deliberately narrow: granted "only... in extraordinary circumstances," it "should not generally exceed twenty-five percent time off, in order to maintain full benefits," and "should not generally exceed one year." So a 75% BYU / 25% company split is roughly the policy ceiling for staying benefits-whole, and even that is exceptional.
3. **Full personal leave (unpaid) — the founder-executive path.** This is the template the Expectations of a Faculty Appointment case law already pointed to, and the Faculty Leaves Policy confirms it is a contemplated purpose, not a workaround: the listed examples of personal-leave purposes include "**employment outside the university, including starting a business** or consulting that does not qualify as professional development." During the leave the company employs and pays the founder directly at whatever salary it likes — none of BYU's compensation caps apply, because BYU isn't paying — and the line-officer bar lifts by its own terms ("Faculty members **not on leave** are not to be line officers in outside companies"): this is the posture in which a CEO/CSO title is actually permitted. The fine print that shapes the deal:
   - **Duration:** personal leaves "vary in length, but generally are not longer than one year" — short against venture timelines (compare Stanford's two-year maximum and Georgia's three-year innovator leave, below).
   - **Approval:** chair → dean → AAVP–Faculty Development, under the same final-approval process as professional development leaves.
   - **Benefits:** for the first three months the faculty member pays their normal premium share and BYU pays its share; "after the third month, the faculty member will be responsible for both his or her normal monthly premium contribution to benefits, **and** the university's monthly premium contribution." There are "other significant benefits implications, for example, under the Master Retirement Plan" — the policy directs faculty to Benefits Services before deciding. (A company-paid founder would normally move onto the company's benefits instead.)
   - **Return is negotiated, not automatic.** "The department chair and the dean should specify **in writing** whether the faculty member will be guaranteed employment after the leave" — so a founder should secure a written return guarantee *before* leaving. The CFS (tenure-track) clock may stop for one year, and the post-leave salary "may, at [the university's] discretion" include the missed increases.
   - **Still a BYU employee.** "A faculty member on personal leave remains an employee of BYU subject to university policy, including policy governing conflict of interest" (and, per the leave application, the Honor Code). Accepting tenure or a continuing relationship with *another university* while on leave without written permission voids CFS. The resource wall also still stands — being on leave does not license using one's BYU lab or students for the company.
4. **Resignation.** The clean break, and a real precedent: David Arnold left the BYU ECE faculty to run Wavetronix full-time while co-founder Michael Jensen stayed (see [contacts list](byu-founder-contacts.md)). The company then deals with BYU as an ordinary third party.

**BYU has no named "entrepreneurial leave" program — personal leave is its functional equivalent.** Peer institutions have formalized the same move: Stanford's [Best Practices for Faculty Start-Ups](https://web.stanford.edu/group/OTL/documents/bp_faculty_sus.pdf) instructs founders to "serve only in advisory or consultative roles" and "take a leave of absence if engaging in a management role," with leaves capped at two years; [Minnesota's entrepreneurial leave](https://research.umn.edu/inquiry-blog/post/new-leave-program-puts-faculty-entrepreneurial-path) is 12 months (+6 extension) with benefits retained; [Nevada-Reno](https://www.unr.edu/administrative-manual/2000-2999-personnel/2684-faculty-entrepreneurial-leave) and [Michigan Tech](https://www.mtu.edu/faculty-handbook/faculty/chapter4/s4-2/421/) allow up to 12 months unpaid (Michigan Tech full- or part-time); the [University System of Georgia's Regents' Innovator leave](https://rpt.coe.gatech.edu/academic-faculty-nomination-to-regents-innovator-leave-of-absence) runs up to three years, full or partial. BYU's one-year norm with negotiated return rights is at the short, discretionary end of that spectrum.

**Could one still teach while on leave?** On a *full* personal leave, no — and the mechanism that would allow it is sized the wrong way around:

- **A full personal leave releases the faculty assignments; teaching is what you are on leave *from*.** "Personal leaves are not funded by BYU and generally are not compensated," so there is no pay mechanism behind a retained course, and the university's model for a leave-taker's courses is coverage, not retention — the parental-leave section shows the machinery: "the department chair may consider hiring additional temporary faculty to teach one or more courses, or teaching loads may be reassigned among department faculty."
- **The policy's instrument for keeping some teaching is the partial personal leave — but its envelope is inverted from what a founder wants.** A partial leave is "a proportional reduction in salary and faculty assignments," so the retained fraction can certainly include a course. The catch: it "should not generally exceed twenty-five percent time off" — the policy contemplates someone who stays ~75% BYU and gives ~25% to the outside activity. The founder shape — *teach one course (~25% retained), spend 75% at the company* — is the mirror image, outside the stated norm, and partial leaves are "only... granted in extraordinary circumstances" to begin with. So "mostly at the company but still teaching my course" has no sanctioned lane; it would take an extraordinary, negotiated exception.
- **Paying an on-leave founder as an adjunct for one course is a theoretical crack, not a channel.** "Generally are not compensated" is not "never," and once off the full-time contract the Supplemental Compensation bar (which applies to "work performed during the time period covered by the full-time appointment") no longer bites by its own terms — but no BYU document describes such an arrangement, and it cuts against the leave's premise. Anyone wanting it should raise it explicitly in the written leave agreement rather than assume it.
- **Uncompensated academic citizenship is the part that plausibly continues.** The personal-leave text is silent on graduate committees, but the leave-taker "remains an employee of BYU subject to university policy," and the McKay School's informal-PDL rule shows the university's instinct about which duties are separable: faculty are "excused from teaching courses but not from thesis/dissertation committee work." Continuing to sit on thesis committees or informally advise students during a founder leave is the kind of arrangement to specify in the written leave terms (and the resource wall still applies — advising students is fine; directing their work toward the company is not).
- **Teaching at *another* institution while on leave is the sharpest edge:** accepting "tenure... or a continuing employment relationship with another academic institution" without written permission from the chair, dean, *and* academic vice president "voids the faculty member's continuing faculty status."
- **Peers make the part-time shape explicit where BYU doesn't:** Michigan Tech's entrepreneurial leave "may be full or part-time," and Georgia's Regents' Innovator leave runs full or partial for up to three years — a part-time entrepreneurial leave at those schools is precisely the keep-a-course arrangement. BYU's partial personal leave is the same tool with a tighter (25%/1-year) norm. And the Stanford/Physical Intelligence shape solves the problem by not using leave at all: Finn and Levine remain full-time professors who teach and run labs, with company time fit into the consulting-day allowance — at BYU, that's the stay-full-time-plus-4-days/month posture of §2.

**Could one keep mentoring (and paying) students, running the lab, and submitting proposals while on leave?** The same logic as teaching, applied to the rest of the faculty assignment — and here the sponsors, not just BYU, hold vetoes:

- **The presumption on a full leave is disengagement from the research, not continuation.** Research, like teaching, is among the "faculty assignments" a full personal leave releases. BYU publishes no procedure for an on-leave PI, but [Columbia's procedure for faculty taking unpaid leaves](https://research.columbia.edu/news/new-procedures-faculty-and-officers-research-who-take-unpaid-leaves) states the sector norm plainly: when faculty on unpaid leave "typically will receive full salary from another entity... the presumption is that they must disengage from their Columbia research projects," the university must "seek prior approval for an interim principal investigator," and "the Provost's Office will not approve unpaid leaves for individuals with sponsored research unless" a written project-continuity plan is completed. The federal triggers that force this (NIH/NSF prior approval for a 3-month absence or ≥25% effort reduction) are covered in the practical-limitations block below.
- **"Running the lab" means holding PI status, and PI status rides on the appointment.** The Sponsored Projects Handbook: "At BYU, only faculty on regular appointments and certain research faculty may be principal investigators." Whether a year of unpaid leave keeps one "on regular appointments" for PI purposes is not stated anywhere — it's a question for RAO, resolved in the written leave agreement. The federal side is harder to negotiate around: committed effort on an award must be backed by institutional salary, and an unpaid-leave PI has none to charge, so each active award realistically moves to a **sponsor-approved interim PI** for the year. The founder can remain scientifically involved in a defined, disclosed advisory capacity — but informally *directing* federally funded work while formally disengaged is precisely what the sponsor-notification rules exist to prevent. The honest structure is interim PI de jure, founder-as-advisor de facto, with the advisory role written into the leave agreement and the sponsors told.
- **Mentoring splits into two different things.** Uncompensated academic citizenship — thesis committees, informal advising — plausibly continues (see the teaching block above; specify it in the leave terms). Day-to-day supervision of funded students is different: it transfers with the interim-PI plan, because the students' RA-ships hang off the awards. [Kentucky's entrepreneurial-leave application](http://web.archive.org/web/20230501113403/https://administration.ca.uky.edu/files/leave_entrepreneurial_leave_application.pdf) shows what a formalized version looks like — the leave is conditioned on terms including that "the faculty employee's status as an investigator on all extant grants and contracts during the leave period shall be maintained or reassigned to another investigator, as deemed appropriate," that leave duties "will not jeopardize the academic progress of any graduate students under the supervision of the faculty person," and that the faculty member keep "a monthly time sheet to document the performance of continuing University assignment during the leave period." BYU has no such form, which means a BYU leave agreement should write those same terms in by hand.
- **"Paying students" is never done personally, in any posture.** Students are paid either by BYU (RA/hourly appointments on grants or department funds) or by the company as its own employees. During a leave, grant-funded students keep getting paid by BYU through the awards — under the interim PI, which is why keeping the awards alive *is* keeping the students paid. The company-side channel exists but is the most conflict-laden move on the board: [Stanford's best-practices list](https://web.stanford.edu/group/OTL/documents/bp_faculty_sus.pdf) is categorical that faculty founders must not "involve current students in company activities" (a student who wants to join the company should be referred to the dean "for review of the request and independent advice"), and BYU's FCOI policy lists using students "to perform services for an outside entity" in which the investigator has an interest as a conflict requiring a management plan. The same person controlling a student's degree progress and their paycheck is the textbook dual relationship. The sanctioned shapes: company money paying students *through* BYU via a sponsored research agreement (the §3 resource-wall conversion, SBIR/STTR subaward shape), or company internships for students the founder does not academically supervise.
- **Submitting proposals is possible in principle — but the proposal commits the very time the leave gave away.** Proposals are the university's, not the individual's: chair and dean approve (committing "facilities and faculty time"), and "before proposals leave the university, they must be approved by the Associate Academic Vice President for Research and Graduate Studies." Nothing in NSF/NIH rules bars a PI who happens to be on leave from being named on a new proposal; the gate is institutional. A proposal submitted mid-leave asks the chair to certify future effort from someone currently promised full-time to a company, and its academic-year budget can't charge effort while the salary is zero — so the realistic pattern is proposals with start dates after the return (or with co-PIs carrying the early effort), signed off by a chair who knows the leave status. A founder planning a one-year leave should expect the proposal pipeline to thin accordingly — one more cost of the full-leave branch.

**Bottom line:** a full personal leave converts "running the lab" into "the lab runs under a sponsor-approved interim PI while the founder advises within written limits" — mentoring continues only in its uncompensated forms, student funding continues only if the awards are handed off cleanly, and new proposals effectively date from the return. If continuous lab operation, student mentoring, and proposal flow are the priority, that is the strongest argument yet for the §2 stay-full-time advisory posture or the partial-leave shape, both of which keep PI status and the whole mentoring relationship intact.

**What else changes in practice during a year of unpaid leave — office, software, grants, students?** The Faculty Leaves Policy answers almost none of this expressly, which is itself the finding: the operational terms live in the *written leave agreement*, not in policy. What the documents and standard practice do establish:

- **Employee status keeps the keys; the resource wall limits what they may be used for.** Because a personal-leave-taker "remains an employee of BYU," the credentials tied to employee status — netid, BYU email, ID card, [library privileges](https://lib.byu.edu/about/faculty-guide/) (faculty borrow up to 100 items for 26 weeks on a current BYU ID) — persist by default rather than being cut off the way they are at separation (compare [BYU Box](https://box.byu.edu/), which deactivates accounts 14 days after someone *leaves* the university, and eligibility rules generally keyed to "active" faculty/staff status). But everything retained is a *university resource*, and the rule quoted in §3 applies on leave exactly as before: "University Personnel may not use university resources, facilities, or work time to engage in commercial activities, businesses, or 'start-up' companies." Practically, the BYU office, email address, file storage, and lab do not become the company's field office — company work needs company email, company hardware, company premises. Campus software adds an independent *contractual* layer on top of policy: university site licenses (MATLAB-, Microsoft 365-, Adobe-type campus agreements) are licensed for university education and research use, not commercial use, so running company work on BYU-licensed seats would breach the vendor license terms even where access still works. The clean pattern is a hard cutover: everything company-related on company accounts and machines from day one of the leave.
- **Office and lab space are discretionary, not guaranteed.** Nothing in the Faculty Leaves Policy promises the office or lab will be waiting; space is a chair/dean allocation, and the policy's coverage model — hiring "additional temporary faculty" or reassigning loads — is exactly the mechanism that can consume a vacant office or idle lab for the year. The same instinct as the written return guarantee applies: the leave agreement should specify in writing what happens to the office, lab space, equipment, startup funds, and any research accounts during the year, rather than leaving them to goodwill.
- **Active federal grants are the sharpest operational edge.** Sponsors treat a PI's leave as a reportable change requiring *their* consent, not just BYU's: NIH requires awarding-institute prior written approval whenever a PD/PI will be "absent from the project during any continuous period of 3 months or more" or reduces effort by 25% or more from the approved level ([NIH GPS §12.13.1](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/policy/nihgps/html5/section_12/12.13.1_leave.htm), [NIH prior-approval reminder](https://nexus.od.nih.gov/all/2018/05/29/changing-the-status-of-a-pd-pi-reminder-of-the-nih-prior-approval-policy); requests go in ~30 days ahead, and the usual resolution is a sponsor-approved interim/substitute PI — [Dartmouth's summary](https://www.dartmouth.edu/osp/award-management/changes_requiring_approval/leaves_absences.php) of the mechanics is typical). NSF's PAPPG (already in the sources) has the same withdrawal/3-month-absence/25%-reduction trigger. So a founding PI with active federal awards cannot simply go dark for a year: each award either moves to an interim PI the sponsor approves in writing, or the leave is structured to preserve ≥75% of committed effort — which is hard to square with running a company full-time. And the grad students funded on those awards still need day-to-day supervision, so the interim-PI plan and the committee-work arrangements above are really one plan.
- **The family tuition benefit is keyed to full-time status — get its leave treatment in writing.** The [Employee Tuition Benefit Policy](https://policy.byu.edu/view/employee-tuition-benefit-policy) ties spouse/dependent tuition benefits to full-time-employee status and does not say expressly what an unpaid personal leave does to them (one clause — survivor eligibility when an employee dies "in active employment or on approved university leave" — implies approved leave preserves status, but that's an inference). A founder whose spouse or children use the benefit should get a written answer from Benefits Services alongside the premium arrangements above.
- **Visa-holding faculty largely cannot use this path.** H-1B status depends on continued paid employment by the petitioning employer; extended unpaid leave outside legally protected categories (FMLA and similar) jeopardizes status — peer universities state they grant H-1B employees unpaid leave ["only for situations required by law"](https://ap.washington.edu/ahr/visas/scholar-resources/h1b/maintaining-h1b-status/). A non-citizen founding PI would need the company to file its own H-1B petition (with the attendant timing risk) — a structural constraint no BYU leave agreement can waive.
- **The IP boundary needs a bright line drawn on day one.** Still an employee, the leave-taker remains under the IP Policy, whose ownership triggers are work "within the scope of employment," "on the university's time," or "with the aid, assistance, or substantial use of" university property or resources. Company inventions made on company time without BYU resources fall outside those triggers — and inside the company's own employment/assignment agreement — but during leave one person carries *both* assignment obligations, so the practical protections are the same as the resource cutover above: clean records of where and with what each invention was made, no BYU accounts or equipment in company work, and (if the company licenses BYU IP) attention to how the license allocates improvements. Naming the boundary explicitly in the leave agreement is cheap insurance against the ugliest dispute a founder can have.
- **Liability and insurance follow the employer.** University coverage attaches to university activities; work done for the company during the leave rides on the company's insurance — one more reason not to blur premises, equipment, or personnel between the two.

**For a VCL founding PI, the decision tree is:** company-paid *and* full-time faculty → doesn't exist (only capped summer salary and the scarce 6-FTE buyout channel, both through BYU payroll at BYU rates); company-paid fractionally → partial personal leave, realistically capped near 25% and one year; company-paid fully while keeping the faculty seat warm → unpaid personal leave with a written return guarantee, the Expectations Case 3 handoff template (run the company until a successor president is in place, then return to an advisory role); company-paid permanently → resign. The leave route is also the only one that unlocks officer titles — which makes "one year, then hand off to a professional CEO and come back as Chief Scientific Advisor" the shape BYU's documents collectively draw. Note the teaching trade-off across the branches: only the stay-full-time and partial-leave branches keep a course in the classroom — the full-leave branch hands the course off (see above). The same split governs the lab itself: staying full-time or on partial leave keeps PI status, student supervision, and the proposal pipeline intact, while the full-leave branch hands active awards to an interim PI, reduces mentoring to its uncompensated forms, and defers new proposals to the return (see the mentoring/lab/proposals block above). And on the full-leave branch, the written leave agreement should settle the practical terms too — office/lab space, grant handoffs, the resource cutover, and the tuition-benefit question (see the practical-limitations block above).

## What this means for VCL specifically

1. **VC money can reach the lab only as a sponsored project or a gift** — negotiated by RAO/TTO/General Counsel, never by the researchers, and the gift route cannot carry strings (conditional gifts and donor-administered funds are expressly disallowed, and only Development/deans may solicit companies). A VC firm wanting influence over the research agenda has no direct policy channel; the consortium/sponsorship structures in the [precedents survey](enterprise-academic-precedents.md) are the compliant shapes.
2. **Spinouts are the supported VC interface.** BYU's TTO licenses to inventor-founded startups as a matter of course (7 startups in 2020; Milken Institute ranks BYU #4 nationally for tech transfer), BYU can hold equity in them (see [When BYU itself holds the equity](#when-byu-itself-holds-the-equity)), and the ≥10% forfeiture rule prices the founder path explicitly.
3. **The "co-founder" title is the trap to avoid casually.** Advisory roles are cheap to approve; officer titles are not. The clean precedent shapes: Coreform (BYU News describes Prof. Michael Scott as "senior advisor" to the company built on his licensed research) and NeuroID (Prof. Jeff Jenkins co-founded the licensee of his BYU patents and remained a professor through its acquisition by Experian). The historical template for a founder who must run the company is a leave of absence (Expectations of a Faculty Appointment, Case 3).
4. **A meaningful VC round forecloses the board seat.** The IP Policy's $3M-raised cap means faculty at a venture-scale licensee can realistically hold only advisory (non-voting) roles absent extraordinary approval.
5. **Non-employee students are freer than faculty.** Students who independently develop IP own it (subject to a shop-right license to BYU when faculty-supervised or using substantial resources), which is why Sandbox companies raise venture money without BYU taking equity. Student founders working *within* VCL projects, though, are inside the employee/shop-right regime.
