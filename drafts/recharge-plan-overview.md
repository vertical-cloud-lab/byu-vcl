# Proposed Recharge Center for Ultrasonic Metal Atomization — Overview

*Draft for discussion, July 2026. Prepared for department and college
leadership. Quotations and page numbers refer to BYU's* Recharge Center Policy
& Procedures *(Regulatory Accounting).*

---

## 1. The instrument

The lab operates an **ultrasonic metal atomizer** (an AMAZEMET rePowder-class
system). It melts a small amount of metal — initially aluminum alloys used in
metal 3D printing — on a vibrating plate; the vibration throws off droplets
that freeze in flight into fine, spherical powder. That powder is the feedstock
for metal 3D printers, and producing it in small custom batches is something
few facilities anywhere can do. A run takes about six hours and is performed
entirely by trained lab personnel; customers never touch the machine, and
remote customers can observe their runs through the lab's web interface.

The atomizer was purchased in 2026 for approximately **$250,000**, paid
entirely from faculty startup funds. No federal money was involved in the
purchase — a fact that matters below, because it is what allows the machine's
cost to be recovered through user fees.

## 2. What we are proposing

We propose to establish a **recharge center**: BYU's official mechanism for a
lab to charge users — inside and outside the university — for a service. The
governing document is the *Recharge Center Policy & Procedures*, administered
by Regulatory Accounting, and its core rule is one line of arithmetic:

> "Annual Rate = Annual Costs / Total Annual Usage" (p. 4)

Add up what the service costs for a year, divide by the number of runs, and
that quotient is the internal price. The center may not profit from BYU users,
and every BYU user pays the same rate (p. 4).

Three reasons to do this:

1. **It is the only clean way for outside labs and companies to pay us** for
   powder runs. Demand is real: external academic groups (mostly federally
   funded) and some industry.
2. **It recovers the $250,000 purchase price** through depreciation built into
   the rates, and lets the startup account that bought the machine be repaid
   (Policy §8, pp. 7–8).
3. **It keeps us audit-safe.** The moment anyone pays for a run with federal
   grant money, federal cost-accounting rules apply. A recharge center is the
   structure built to satisfy them.

## 3. Proposed rates

Per run (~6 machine-hours), effective January 1, 2027:

| Who is paying | Rate |
|---|---|
| BYU users (any BYU account — one uniform rate) | **$918 / run** |
| External users paying with documented federal grant funds | **$1,391 / run** |
| External users paying with non-federal money | **$1,650 / run** |
| Minimum fee, any job that gets started | **$150** |

**Data-embargo options** (available only to non-federal external users; see §7):

| Delay of public data release | Added fee |
|---|---|
| 6 months | +$1,000 |
| 12 months | +$2,500 |
| 24 months | +$5,000 |
| 48 months | +$10,000 |

**Where the $918 comes from.** The cost pool is dominated by people, not by
the machine. Running a customer-facing facility means scheduling, quoting,
material intake, safety paperwork, setup, monitoring, powder recovery,
cleaning, packaging, documentation, maintenance, training, and billing —
budgeted here as two full-time-equivalent operator positions (staffed in year
one by a bench of trained student employees, with a staff technician added as
billed volume grows). Adding depreciation ($50,000/year — see §6),
maintenance, supplies, and per-run consumables (argon, sonotrode and crucible
wear) gives an annual pool of about $280,000 against a forecast of 305
runs/year: **$918 per run**. For reference, Northwestern University operates
the same instrument and publicly charges **$1,075 internal / $1,720 external
academic / $2,150 commercial** per run — our schedule sits below theirs at
every tier.

**Why external prices are higher.** External users pay the same underlying
cost **plus BYU's institutional overhead** (51.5%, which goes to the
university, not to the lab): $918 × 1.515 ≈ $1,391. For external users *not*
spending federal money, the policy also permits a **surcharge** (p. 5) —
here $259/run — which is the only money the center may keep above cost, and
the only internal source of funds for future equipment purchases and upgrades.

## 4. The ground rules

The policy rules that shape everything, in plain terms:

- **Everyone at BYU pays the same rate** for the same service (p. 4).
- **Break even, don't profit** — internal rates must wash out over a five-year
  window (p. 3); if the center over-collects one year, the next year's rate
  comes down (p. 6).
- **Every run is logged, billed or not** (p. 4). Unbilled runs still count in
  the denominator, so they lower everyone's rate rather than hiding.
- **No advance billing** (p. 2) — invoices go out at or after delivery.
- **Building costs stay out** of the rates — rent, renovation, and building
  utilities are not chargeable (p. 3); the university recovers those through
  its overhead rate instead.
- **Internal rates cannot be padded to save for new equipment** (p. 5) —
  growth money comes only from the external surcharge, sale of old equipment,
  or new investment.

## 5. Our own group's use

Our group will be the largest single user (~200 runs/year). Every one of those
runs is logged and valued at the full $918 internal rate — nothing is "free"
on paper. They are settled through **Policy §8 (pp. 7–8)**, which allows the
startup account that bought the machine to be repaid — up to the $250,000 it
invested — by "receiving services without charge." Each of our runs becomes an
installment on that repayment, recorded in a ledger that Regulatory Accounting
approves in advance. The establishment proposal asks Regulatory Accounting to
approve this arrangement and to set the per-run credit value.

Any of our runs charged to a federal award is simply billed at the uniform
internal rate like anyone else's.

## 6. Recovering the $250,000

The purchase price comes back through **depreciation**: $250,000 on a
**five-year straight-line schedule** puts **$50,000 per year** into the rates.
The depreciation share of every billed run — **$164** — is deposited in an
**equipment reserve account** (p. 7), which funds equipment purchases of
$5,000 or more and the §8 repayment described above.

Three facts about how this works:

1. **The clock is already running.** Rates "cannot include depreciation from
   prior years" (p. 6). The machine went into service in mid-2026; with rates
   effective January 1, 2027 (BYU budgets on the calendar year), the roughly
   $29,000 of 2026 depreciation is simply not recovered. This is why the
   timeline in §9 targets January 1 and not later.
2. **Volume cannot speed it up.** Busy years do not recover the machine
   faster — over-collection just lowers the next year's rate. Capital comes
   back on the depreciation schedule, period.
3. **Overhead is not ours.** The 51.5% that external users pay on top goes to
   central administration. The center recovers the machine's capitalized cost,
   not cost-plus-overhead.

The capitalized cost that depreciates includes not just the invoice but also
duty, freight, and installation as booked by Fixed Assets; the timeline in §9
includes confirming that asset record — including machine-specific
installation items from the associated renovation — before the schedule is
approved.

## 7. Data policy: open by default

The facility is being built as an open, remote-access "cloud lab," and its
data policy matches:

- **Run data becomes public by default.** Data from every job is deposited in
  a public repository under a permissive license after a **3-month grace
  period** from delivery. The customer owns their data and may shorten or
  waive the grace period; the facility claims no rights beyond the license.
- **Customers who need more time can buy it — if they are non-federal.** The
  embargo menu in §3 delays the public release by 6 to 48 months. Because an
  embargo fee is a surcharge, it is available only to non-federal external
  users (p. 5); federally funded users receive the standard grace period.
  This mirrors the model at U.S. Department of Energy user facilities, where
  publication is the default and confidentiality is the priced exception.
- **Every embargo expires.** There is no permanent-secrecy tier; work that
  requires it belongs in a sponsored-research agreement through the Sponsored
  Programs Office, not on the rate sheet.
- **Aggregate operational statistics** — throughput, uptime, reliability,
  queue behavior — are publishable by the facility immediately in all cases,
  with no customer-identifying information. This is the material our students'
  research on remote-laboratory operation depends on.

The open default strengthens the facility's educational-mission position and
keeps student publication timelines independent of any customer's embargo.

## 8. Payments

- **BYU users:** monthly journal entries between university accounts.
- **External users:** invoice with payment by check/ACH/wire; smaller jobs may
  pay by credit card through BYU Treasury Services' **eMarkets** storefront
  (the university's hosted, PCI-compliant payment page — no third-party
  processors). Card fees (~2.9%) are absorbed within the non-federal
  surcharge. Utah sales tax on shipped powder is collected on top via the
  university's tax software.
- Engagements requiring negotiated intellectual property, student intellectual
  contribution, or co-authorship are routed to sponsored-research agreements
  through the Sponsored Programs Office rather than the rate sheet.
