## 1. The machine, in one paragraph

The lab owns an **ultrasonic metal atomizer** (an AMAZEMET rePowder-class
system). It melts a small amount of metal — initially aluminum alloys used in
3D printing — on a vibrating plate; the vibration throws off droplets that
freeze in flight into fine, spherical powder. That powder is the feedstock for
metal 3D printers, and making it in small custom batches is something few labs
can do. One run takes about **6 hours** and is operated by trained personnel;
customers never touch the machine. It was purchased around **May 2026** for
roughly **$250,000**, paid entirely from startup funds — no grant money.

## 2. What we are setting up, and why

A **recharge center** is BYU's official mechanism for a lab to charge other
people for using its equipment. The rules come from BYU's *Recharge Center
Policy & Procedures*, administered by **Regulatory Accounting**. The core rule
is one line of arithmetic:

> "Annual Rate = Annual Costs / Total Annual Usage" (p. 4)

You add up what the service really costs for a year, divide by how many runs
you expect, and that quotient is the price. You are not allowed to profit from
BYU users, and you must charge every BYU user the same rate (p. 4).

Why do it at all: (a) it is the only clean way for outside labs and companies
to pay us for powder runs; (b) it lets us recover the $250,000 purchase price
over the machine's life and repay the startup account that bought it (Policy §8,
pp. 7–8); and (c) it keeps us safe in an audit — the moment anyone pays for a
run with federal grant money, federal cost rules apply.

## 3. The suggested numbers

These are the planning rates we suggest, per ~6-hour run:

| Who is paying | Suggested planning rate |
|---|---|
| BYU users (any BYU account — one uniform rate) | **~$840 / run** |
| External users paying with a documented federal grant | **~$1,270 / run** |
| External users paying with non-federal money — standard | **~$1,500 / run** |
| External users paying with non-federal money — proprietary (nothing published) | **~$1,900 / run** |
| Minimum fee, any job that gets started | **$150** |

**Why these numbers.** Our first draft computed **$306/run** — but it counted
only 2 hours of student labor per run and no dedicated staff. The outside
review flagged that as the single biggest problem: a real facility's cost is
mostly **people** — scheduling, quoting, material intake, safety paperwork,
setup, monitoring, powder recovery, cleaning, packaging, documentation, failed
runs, maintenance, training, billing. Northwestern runs the **same instrument**
and publicly charges **$1,075 internal / $1,720 external academic / $2,150
commercial** per run. Rebuilding our cost pool with roughly two full-time
staff-equivalents gives **~$836/run internal**; that is the basis of the table
above. The honest number will come from the time study in §9 — the review's
advice, which we adopt, is: **do not quote $306 to anyone, even informally.**

**Why external prices are higher.** External users pay the same underlying cost
**plus BYU's institutional overhead** (~51.5%, which goes to the university, not
to the lab). If they are *not* spending federal money, BYU also allows a
**surcharge** on top (p. 5) — that surcharge is the only money the center may
keep above cost, and it is what can eventually help buy the next machine.

## 4. The ground rules

The handful of policy rules that shape everything, in plain terms:

- **Everyone at BYU pays the same rate** for the same service (p. 4). No
  discounts for friends, no premium for strangers.
- **Break even, don't profit** — internal rates must wash out to zero over a
  5-year window (p. 3); if we over-collect one year, next year's rate drops (p. 6).
- **Log every run, even unbilled ones** (p. 4). Free runs still count in the
  denominator, so they lower everyone's rate rather than hiding.
- **No advance billing** (p. 2) — we invoice at or after delivery, never before.
- **Building costs stay out** — rent, renovation, building utilities are not
  chargeable (p. 3); those are recovered through the university's overhead rate
  instead.
- **Internal rates can't be padded to save for new equipment** (p. 5) — growth
  money comes only from the external surcharge, from selling old equipment, or
  from new investment.

## 5. How our own lab's runs are handled

Our group will be the biggest user (~200 runs/year). The key framing, per the
review: our runs are **never "free" on paper**. Every run is logged and valued
at the full internal rate. What varies is who covers it:

- **Preferred: repayment in kind.** Policy §8 (pp. 7–8) lets the startup account
  that bought the machine be paid back — up to the $250,000 it invested — by
  receiving "services without charge." Our runs become installments on the
  repayment.
- **Fallback:** pay the internal rate from startup funds, or have the center
  cover our runs from non-federal money as a documented subsidy (pp. 3–5).

One honest unknown: **how much repayment credit one free run is worth**. The
full internal rate? Only the ~$82 depreciation slice of it? Something in
between? The answer changes the repayment timeline from under 2 years to longer
than the machine's life, so we will get it **in writing from Regulatory
Accounting** before relying on any projection.

## 6. Getting the $250,000 back

The purchase price comes back through **depreciation**: $250,000 spread over an
assumed 10-year life puts **$25,000/year** into the rates, and the depreciation
slice of every *billed* run is set aside in an **equipment reserve account**
(p. 7). Three timing facts:

1. **The clock is already running.** Rates "cannot include depreciation from
   prior years" (p. 6), so months before rates take effect are lost — about
   **$14.6k forfeited** if rates start January 1, 2027 (BYU budgets on the
   calendar year). That is why the timeline in §9 targets that date.
2. **Volume cannot speed it up.** Busy years don't recover the machine faster —
   over-collection just lowers next year's rate. Capital comes back on the
   depreciation schedule, period.
3. **Overhead is not ours.** The ~51.5% external users pay on top goes to
   central administration. Plan on recovering the machine's capitalized cost,
   not cost-plus-overhead.

Whether the **tariff, freight, installation, and machine-specific renovation
work** (dedicated electrical, argon plumbing, exhaust) count inside that
recoverable base depends entirely on how Fixed Assets booked them. That is a
question of BYU's regular accounting practice — not something we can assert —
and it must be settled **before** the depreciation schedule is approved.

## 7. External users, publication, and payment

- **Two products in year one.** *Standard:* the customer owns their run data;
  we may publish only aggregate, anonymized operational statistics (throughput,
  uptime, reliability — the material our students' cloud-lab papers need), and
  anything customer-specific only with written consent. *Proprietary:* nothing
  published, priced at the top tier. The fancier menu of timed data embargoes
  (6/12/24 months) is **deferred** — the review found no precedent for it at
  any university facility and recommended launching simple.
- **Federal vs. non-federal is about the money, not the badge.** A university
  lab paying with its NSF grant is "federal" (no surcharge allowed); the same
  lab paying with department funds, or any company, is "non-federal"
  (surcharge allowed). Customers certify their funding in the service agreement.
- **Credit cards: yes, through BYU's own storefront only** (Treasury Services'
  eMarkets — a hosted, PCI-compliant payment page). No lab-run Stripe or PayPal,
  ever. Card processing costs ~2.9%, so large jobs go by invoice/ACH instead.

## 8. Safety and the other sign-offs we added

The review's biggest gap finding: our plan routed through the finance offices
but skipped everyone else. Added to the approval path:

- **Environmental Health & Safety** — aluminum powder is a **combustible dust**
  (the relevant standard is NFPA 484), and the argon supply is an asphyxiation
  hazard in an enclosed space. EHS review of SOPs, ventilation, oxygen
  monitoring, powder storage/shipping, and emergency procedures happens
  **before** any customer work, not after.
- **Export control** — metal-AM powders and process parameters can be
  export-sensitive. Every non-US customer (and remote observer) gets screened
  first; no non-US sales are requested in year one.
- **Customer data** — a cloud lab receives customer files and recipes; we need
  a stated policy on classification, retention, and access before accepting
  proprietary work.
- **Legal/insurance** — the Office of General Counsel reviews the service
  agreement, including liability for powder used in someone else's printer.

## 9. The plan between now and January 2027

| When | What |
|---|---|
| **Now** | Freeze all price quotes (say "rates pending approval"). Start the usage log — every run, billed or not (p. 4). |
| **July–Aug 2026** | Run a 2–4 week **time-and-cost study**: log every minute of staff time per run, by task. This one activity determines the real rate. |
| **July–Aug 2026** | One **cross-functional kickoff meeting** — Regulatory Accounting, Fixed Assets, department finance, EHS, General Counsel, Tax, Treasury, Export Control, Sponsored Programs — instead of visiting offices one at a time through December. |
| **Aug 2026** | Get the **asset decision** from Fixed Assets: capitalized cost, in-service date, useful life, and whether tariff/installation/renovation items are on the equipment record. Everything else keys off this. |
| **Aug–Sep 2026** | Settle the own-lab structure (§5) with a **written** ruling on repayment valuation. |
| **Sep 2026** | Submit the establishment proposal and a **conservative rate proposal** showing two columns: bare-minimum documented cost, and the staffed/realistic rate the time study supports. |
| **Oct–Dec 2026** | Approvals; open the accounts; EHS sign-off; service agreement through General Counsel; eMarkets setup; operational readiness checklist. |
| **Jan 1, 2027** | Go live. |
| **CY2027** | **Quarterly** check-ins (not just the annual one): if labor per run or utilization drifts more than ~20–25% from forecast, revise the rate mid-year. |

## 10. What's still open

1. **The real staffing cost per run** — the time study answers this; it is the
   difference between $306 and $840+.
2. **What's in the depreciable base** — tariff, freight, installation,
   renovation line items; Fixed Assets decides.
3. **The repayment-credit valuation** for our own runs (§5) — written ruling.
4. **The external overhead rate and base** for service sales — confirm with
   Sponsored Programs / Regulatory Accounting.
5. Whether the June-2005 policy is still current, and whether our
   **mostly-external paying customer base** changes how the center should be
   scoped (raised explicitly in the establishment proposal).
