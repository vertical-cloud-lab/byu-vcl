## Detailed Analysis by Item

### 1. Ultrasonic Cleaner — Medium Priority

**Market context:** Used lab ultrasonic cleaners typically go for $200–$800 at auction for standard benchtop models. Large or multi-frequency units can reach $1,000–$4,000.

**Strategic fit:** Useful for cleaning LPBF build plate residue, atomization nozzle components, and general lab parts. Not a primary research tool but a solid utility item for the alloy workflow.

**Recommendation:** Bid up to ~$800 for a standard model. Walk away above $1,000 unless it is an unusually large or high-specification unit.

---

### 2. Vacuum Oven — Medium Priority

**Market context:** Used lab vacuum ovens sell at auction for $350–$1,200 depending on brand, size, and condition. Thermo Scientific units are in the $400–$1,000 range.

**Strategic fit:** Directly relevant to the **autonomous electrochemistry** thrust (battery slurry drying, electrode preparation). Also useful for drying metal powders post-atomization and for degassing samples. A vacuum oven complements the lab's move toward electrochemical cell fabrication ([Issue #27](https://github.com/vertical-cloud-lab/byu-vcl/issues/27)).

**Recommendation:** Solid utility buy at $500–$800. Worth up to $1,200 if in good condition.

---

### 3 & 4. Ductless Fumehood (P20, no floor) × 2 — **High Priority**

**Market context:** Used Air Science Purair P20 ductless hoods sell for $1,000–$3,000 on resale platforms. At auction from a battery manufacturer, expect $800–$2,000 each.

**Dimensions & OT-2 fit:**
| | P20 Internal | OT-2 Footprint | Clearance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Width | 49 in (1245 mm) | 25 in (630 mm) | **24 in surplus** ✅ |
| Depth | 27.4 in (695 mm) | 22.5 in (570 mm) | **4.9 in surplus** ✅ |
| Height | 38 in (965 mm) | 26 in (660 mm) | **12 in surplus** ✅ |

The OT-2 fits comfortably inside the P20 with room for cable routing and the recommended 8 in (20 cm) ventilation clearance on the sides. However, "no floor" means a separate work surface must be provided or fabricated.

**Strategic fit:** Fumehoods are a critical safety need for powder handling (metal powders for atomization and LPBF are potentially pyrophoric/hazardous). This directly addresses [Issue #29](https://github.com/vertical-cloud-lab/byu-vcl/issues/29). Having two P20 units would allow one for powder dispensing (with OT-2) and one for manual chemistry/electrochemistry work.

**Recommendation:** Bid aggressively on at least one. Two would be ideal for parallel workflows. Budget $800–$2,000 each. Factor in ~$50–$200 for replacement filters.

**Recommendation:** This could be the best-value fumehood in the auction. Bid $600–$1,500. If it goes cheap, this is an excellent acquisition even as a secondary hood for manual operations.

### 6. Ductless Fumehood (Larger) — Low Priority

**Market context:** Larger or specialty ductless hoods sell for $1,000–$5,000 used, but bulky models are harder to place and attract fewer bidders, so $500–$1,500 at auction is plausible.

**Strategic fit:** Bigger footprint and unwieldy form factor are negatives for the lab's vertical/compact design philosophy. Random ports add complexity without benefit. Only worth acquiring if priced very low as a general-purpose backup.

**Recommendation:** Only bid if the price is significantly below the P20/P15 options. Max $1,000–$1,500 and only if the other fumehoods are lost.

### 7. Instron 5966 — **High Priority**

**Market context:** The Instron 5966 is a 10 kN dual-column universal testing machine. Although recently discontinued, it is a modern, well-regarded platform. Used/auction prices for comparable Instron models range $15,000–$40,000 depending on accessories, software, and condition. Battery company usage (film testing, adhesion peels) may mean lighter wear on the load cell and frame compared to metal testing labs.

**Strategic fit:** This is one of the highest-value items in the auction for BYU VCL. Tensile testing of 3D-printed alloy specimens is a core requirement of the **autonomous alloy discovery** workflow ([Issue #46](https://github.com/vertical-cloud-lab/byu-vcl/issues/46)). The 10 kN (≈2,250 lbf) capacity is suitable for sub-size ASTM E8 metal tensile specimens. The Instron 5966 is compatible with Bluehill Universal software and can be integrated programmatically (critical for the "cloud experimentation" model).

**Key considerations:**
- Verify if Bluehill software license and load cells/grips are included in the lot
- A battery company likely used this for peel and adhesion testing, so the grips may not be suitable for metal specimens — budget ~$2,000–$5,000 for appropriate grips and fixtures
- Verify calibration status and service history

**Recommendation:** Bid $10,000–$25,000 depending on included accessories. This could save $40,000–$80,000 vs. buying new. Max bid $30,000 if well-equipped with software and load cell.

### 8. 100 N Test Frame — Low Priority

**Market context:** A 100 N (~22 lbf) test frame or load cell is very low capacity. Used units sell for $500–$2,000. If it's just a load cell, $150–$250.

**Strategic fit:** 100 N is insufficient for metal tensile testing (ASTM E8 specimens typically require 5–50 kN). This would only be useful for testing thin films, adhesives, or very soft materials. Could have niche value for electrochemistry electrode peel tests, but not a primary need.

**Recommendation:** Only bid if very cheap ($200–$500). Not aligned with core alloy testing needs.

### 9. Particle Sizer — **High Priority**

**Market context:** Used particle size analyzers have a wide price range: $400–$2,000 for basic/older models, $7,000–$20,000 for mainstream lab analyzers (Malvern, Horiba, Beckman Coulter), and $20,000–$60,000 for high-end units. At a battery manufacturer liquidation, the unit is likely a laser diffraction analyzer (e.g., Malvern Mastersizer) optimized for powder characterization.

**Strategic fit:** Particle size distribution is a critical quality metric for metal powders used in LPBF. The alloy workflow (ultrasonic atomizer → LPBF) requires characterization of atomized powder to ensure it meets LPBF feedstock specifications (typically 15–45 µm or 20–63 µm). This directly supports the autonomous alloy discovery thrust. A battery manufacturer likely used this for electrode particle characterization, and the same instrument can characterize metal powders.

**Key considerations:**
- Identify the specific model and measurement range — verify it covers 1–100 µm at minimum
- Check if a wet dispersion unit is included (essential for metal powder characterization)
- Verify software license and computer are included

**Recommendation:** Bid $2,000–$10,000 depending on model and completeness. A Malvern Mastersizer or Horiba LA-series would be an excellent acquisition. Max $15,000 for a recent, complete system.

### 10. pH Meter — Low Priority

**Market context:** Used lab pH meters sell for $100–$900 at auction, with most standard benchtop models going for $150–$400.

**Strategic fit:** Useful for the **autonomous electrochemistry** thrust (electrolyte preparation, battery cell fabrication). However, pH meters are commodity lab instruments that can be purchased new for $200–$500. Not a high-value auction target.

**Recommendation:** Opportunistic buy. Bid $100–$300 if convenient. Don't prioritize over other items.

### 11. Glovebox — **High Priority**

**Market context:** Used gloveboxes sell for $1,500–$15,000 at auction depending on brand, features, and condition. Basic gloveboxes: $1,500–$5,000. Inert-atmosphere gloveboxes (MBraun, Vacuum Atmospheres): $5,000–$15,000.

**OT-2 fit:** The OT-2 footprint is 25 × 22.5 × 26 in (63 × 57 × 66 cm). Gloveboxes that could accommodate this need an internal working area of at least **28 × 25 × 28 in** (with minimal clearance) or ideally **32 × 28 × 30 in** for comfortable access and cable routing. Dimensions of this specific unit are unknown and **must be verified** before bidding.

**Strategic fit:** A glovebox directly addresses [Issue #30](https://github.com/vertical-cloud-lab/byu-vcl/issues/30) and enables:
- Inert-atmosphere powder handling for pyrophoric metal powders (aluminum, titanium)
- Potential automated powder dispensing with OT-2 inside the glovebox
- Electrochemical cell assembly in controlled atmosphere
- Integration into the vertical lift module (VLM) workflow

A glovebox from a battery manufacturer is likely set up for inert atmosphere (argon/nitrogen) work, which is exactly what metal powder handling requires.

**Key considerations:**
- **Must verify internal dimensions** — if the OT-2 doesn't fit, the glovebox is still useful for manual powder handling but loses the automation integration value
- Check gas purification system condition (O₂ and H₂O removal)
- Verify glove and port condition (replaceable but adds cost)
- Check if it includes a vacuum antechamber for material transfer

**Recommendation:** Bid $1,500–$5,000 for a basic model, up to $7,500 if it has an inert gas purification system and antechamber. Verify dimensions before bidding — this is the swing factor for the lab's automation strategy.
