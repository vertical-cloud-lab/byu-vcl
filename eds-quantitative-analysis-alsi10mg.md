# Quantitative Compositional Analysis of AlSi10Mg Additive Manufacturing Powder by SEM-EDS and Complementary Techniques

## 1. Best Practices for Quantitative EDS Compositional Analysis of Al-Si-Mg Alloy Powders

Rigorous quantitative EDS analysis of AlSi10Mg powder requires careful attention to specimen preparation, instrument calibration, and data processing. The most critical requirement is achieving a flat, highly polished specimen surface—metallographic polishing to a mirror finish without chemical etching is essential, as surface roughness exceeding ~50 nm RMS can bias low-energy X-ray measurements (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 11-13). Measurement conditions must be tightly controlled and documented: beam energy, dose (beam current × live time), beam incidence angle, detector take-off angle, and detector geometry must all be consistent between standards and unknowns (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 2-4). Operating at a detector dead time of approximately 10% minimizes pulse coincidence (sum peak) artifacts, as dead times exceeding ~15% introduce significant coincidence effects (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 11-13).

For powder-specific analysis, EDS works best on flat surfaces, and the spherical geometry of gas-atomized particles degrades accuracy significantly (groarke2021powdercharacterization—methodsstandards pages 14-18). Standards should be co-mounted or measured under identical conditions; appropriate primary standards include pure elements and stoichiometric compounds such as Al₂O₃, SiO₂, and MgO, measured at multiple accelerating voltages (10, 15, 20 kV) to validate matrix corrections (jones2021knowyourstandards pages 1-3). Following ASTM E1508 for quantitative EDS procedures and ASTM E1078 for sample mounting is recommended (groarke2021powdercharacterization—methodsstandards pages 14-18). Multiple spectra (N ≈ 5–10) should be collected for both standards and unknowns to enable outlier rejection and homogeneity assessment (ritchie2018exploringthelimits pages 4-5).

The typical nominal composition of AlSi10Mg powder is: Si 9.0–11.0 wt%, Mg 0.20–0.45 wt%, Fe < 0.55 wt%, with Al as balance. Published measurements report values such as Si 10.1–10.4 wt%, Mg 0.31–0.40 wt%, and Fe 0.11–0.16 wt% (riener2021influenceofstorage pages 3-3, javidani2017additivemanufacturingof pages 1-2, raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 2-3).

## 2. Challenges in Detecting Trace Mg (0.2–0.45 wt%) in an Al Matrix Using SEM-EDS

The detection of Mg at 0.2–0.45 wt% in an Al-rich matrix presents several interrelated challenges. The Mg Kα line at 1.253 keV and the Al Kα line at 1.486 keV are separated by only ~233 eV. While modern silicon drift detectors (SDDs) achieve energy resolutions of approximately 120–135 eV at Mn Kα (5.9 keV), the resolution degrades at lower energies, making the Mg–Al separation marginal and requiring robust peak deconvolution (ritchie2018exploringthelimits pages 1-4). The challenge is compounded by the fact that Mg is a minor constituent sitting on the tail of the dominant Al peak, creating a classic "small peak on large peak" interference scenario (spasevski2026challengesinmeasuring pages 2-5, spasevski2026challengesinmeasuring pages 1-2).

Detection limits for EDS are fundamentally governed by the peak-to-background ratio and counting statistics, following the relationship C_DL = [3N_B^(1/2)/(N_S − N_B)] × C_S (newbury2019electronexcitedxraymicroanalysis pages 13-15). With modern SDD-EDS under optimized high-count-rate conditions, detection limits of approximately 0.1 wt% (1000 ppm) are achievable, and mapping of compositional segregation at the ~0.2 wt% level has been demonstrated (spasevski2026challengesinmeasuring pages 1-2, spasevski2026challengesinmeasuring pages 11-11). However, achieving these limits requires careful pulse pile-up correction, background filtering, and peak deconvolution at each measurement point (spasevski2026challengesinmeasuring pages 8-11). Simple count-time scaling predictions can be misleading because peak deconvolution introduces additional error not captured by standard detection limit formulae (spasevski2026challengesinmeasuring pages 2-5).

At the 0.2–0.45 wt% Mg level in AlSi10Mg, Mg is technically above the detection limit of modern EDS systems but sits in a challenging regime where quantitative accuracy is sensitive to spectral processing quality. Automatic peak identification becomes increasingly error-prone at low concentrations, and analysts should manually verify peak assignments using KLM markers and consider measurements at multiple beam energies to increase confidence (newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 12-14). WDS retains superior peak-to-background performance for the lowest trace levels and is unlikely to be matched by EDS at the most extreme detection limits (ritchie2018exploringthelimits pages 1-4).

An example of an SDD-EDS spectrum showing the low-energy region where Mg and Al peaks appear is illustrated in work by Newbury and Ritchie on NIST Glass K523, demonstrating both Mg Kα and Al Kα peak identification in a multi-element matrix (newbury2019electronexcitedxraymicroanalysis media e2991e80).

**ICP-OES** is the gold standard for bulk composition verification of AlSi10Mg powder. It requires acid dissolution of the powder into solution, after which the solution is injected into an inductively coupled plasma. ## 4. Standards-Based vs. Standardless Quantification Accuracy for Light Elements

The accuracy difference between standards-based and standardless EDS quantification is substantial and well-documented. Standards-based k-ratio/ZAF protocols on well-prepared flat specimens achieve relative errors of approximately ±1–2% for major elements. For example, a standards-based DTSA-II analysis of SRM K411 yielded means within +1.8% (Fe) and −1.0% (Mg) relative, with most results clustering at ~1% relative deviation (newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 18-19).

In contrast, traditional standardless EDS quantification exhibits much broader error distributions. Historical evaluations showed that ±25–30% relative deviation was needed to encompass 95% of standardless results, and specific examples on sulfide minerals produced relative errors exceeding 20–30% for individual elements (newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 18-19, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 4-5, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 16-18). For light elements specifically, standardless analyses of Al in AlN showed approximately ±4.3% relative deviation, and Si in Si₃N₄ showed approximately ±2.5–3.3% (newbury2019electronexcitedxraymicroanalysis pages 16-18).

Modern standardless approaches have significantly improved. Pinard et al. demonstrated a factory-standards-based standardless method achieving unnormalized accuracy better than 5% overall, improving to approximately 2% relative error when very light elements (B, C, O, N, F) are excluded (pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 1-3). The P/B-based standardless method (PeBaZAF) can achieve ±10% relative deviation for 95% of results using modern SDDs (eggert2020effectofthe pages 1-1).

A critical limitation of standardless methods is the forced normalization of concentrations to 100 wt%, which masks absolute scale errors and prevents detection of missing elements or systematic biases (newbury2019electronexcitedxraymicroanalysis pages 18-20, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 4-5). Vendor implementations are often undocumented and evolving, limiting user confidence in the underlying algorithms (newbury2019electronexcitedxraymicroanalysis pages 18-20, newbury2019electronexcitedxraymicroanalysis pages 16-18).

**For AlSi10Mg analysis, standards-based quantification is strongly recommended** when accurate Mg values are needed, particularly given that Mg at 0.2–0.45 wt% represents a minor constituent where standardless errors could easily exceed the specification range.

## 5. Sample Preparation Effects on Quantitative Accuracy for Powder Samples

Sample preparation is one of the most critical factors affecting quantitative EDS accuracy for powder materials. Quantitative EDS fundamentally requires a flat, polished sample surface and a region that is homogeneous throughout the X-ray interaction volume (typically ~1–5 µm depth) (tong2026measurementuncertaintiesof pages 15-18).

**Carbon tape mounting** of loose spherical powder particles produces severe topographic artifacts that make spectra unsuitable for quantitative analysis (tong2026measurementuncertaintiesof pages 15-18). The curved particle surfaces cause geometric effects including variable X-ray take-off angles, shadowing, and altered electron-sample interactions that violate the assumptions of ZAF/φ(ρz) matrix corrections. Carbon tape mounting is appropriate only for qualitative elemental identification or semi-quantitative screening (kennedy2019additivemanufacturingassessing pages 3-4, groarke2021powdercharacterization—methodsstandards pages 14-18). Kennedy et al. described automated CCSEM workflows on carbon tape for rapid particle-by-particle compositional screening of thousands of particles, but noted that results are "not fully quantitative" (kennedy2019additivemanufacturingassessing pages 3-4).

**Cross-section epoxy mounting** with metallographic polishing to a mirror finish is the required preparation for quantitative EDS. Particles are mounted in conductive epoxy or Bakelite resin, sectioned, ground, and polished to expose flat cross-sections through the particle interiors (balart2022automatedsemedsanalysis pages 6-6, ritchie2018exploringthelimits pages 4-5). This approach eliminates topographic effects, provides access to the bulk interior composition (avoiding surface oxide bias), and satisfies the flat-specimen assumption of matrix correction algorithms. Polishing to 0.05 µm with non-crystallizing colloidal silica suspension is a common final step (balart2022automatedsemedsanalysis pages 6-6). Additionally, cross-sectioned powder allows detection of internal contaminants that would be invisible on surface-mounted particles (balart2022automatedsemedsanalysis pages 6-6).

Carbon coating (~10 nm) of non-conductive or poorly grounded mounts ensures conductivity and prevents charging artifacts. Coating thickness must be consistent between standards and unknowns, as differences at low beam energies (≤5 kV) introduce significant systematic errors in low-energy peak intensities (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 11-13, ritchie2018exploringthelimits pages 4-5).

## 6. ZAF/φ(ρz) Matrix Corrections Relevant to the Al-Si-Mg System

Quantitative EDS analysis employs matrix corrections to convert measured X-ray intensities (k-ratios) into concentrations. The classical ZAF correction decomposes into three components: Z (atomic number correction, accounting for electron backscattering and stopping power), A (absorption correction), and F (secondary fluorescence correction) (eggert2020effectofthe pages 3-3, eggert2020effectofthe pages 2-3). The modern φ(ρz) approach models the depth distribution of X-ray production more rigorously and is now the standard implementation in most commercial software (pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 5-7).

For the Al-Si-Mg system, several considerations apply:

- **Absorption correction (A)** is the dominant correction for light elements. Low-energy Kα X-rays from Mg (1.253 keV), Al (1.486 keV), and Si (1.740 keV) are strongly absorbed within the specimen, and the absorption correction is highly sensitive to mass absorption coefficient (MAC) values used in the calculation (eggert2020effectofthe pages 3-3, pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 5-7). The Sewell/Love/Scott "Quadrilateral" depth-distribution model with modern MAC databases (e.g., Elam et al.) is commonly employed (eggert2020effectofthe pages 3-3).

- **Matrix correction magnitudes** for light elements in oxide or silicate matrices can be substantial. Batanova et al. reported ZAF correction coefficients of approximately 1.6–1.9 for Al and P in olivine matrices, meaning the detection limit after correction can be nearly double the raw counting-statistics limit (batanova2018traceelementanalysis pages 3-6). In the Al-Si-Mg metallic alloy system, corrections are somewhat smaller since the matrix is primarily low-Z metal, but absorption remains significant for the lowest-energy lines.

- **Carbon coating effects**: The carbon conductive coating adds an absorbing layer that preferentially attenuates low-energy X-rays. At low accelerating voltages (≤5 kV), variations in coating thickness between samples and standards can introduce systematic errors in Mg and Al peak intensities (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 11-13, pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 5-7).

- **Beam energy optimization**: Accelerating voltage must be chosen to provide adequate overvoltage for exciting all relevant K-lines while limiting the interaction volume. Detection limits for Al and related elements can improve between 15–20 kV but may degrade at 25 kV due to the competing effects of excitation efficiency and absorption (batanova2018traceelementanalysis pages 3-6). For AlSi10Mg, 15–20 kV is a typical operating range.

- **Peak extraction for EDS**: Because EDS peaks are broader than WDS peaks, multiple X-ray transitions sum into single peaks, requiring per-transition corrections and weighted matrix correction factors. Accurate peak profiles incorporating detector impulse response, fundamental atomic data (relative line weights, Coster-Kronig transitions), and mass absorption coefficients are necessary for reliable deconvolution (pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 5-7). Background removal via convolution/top-hat filtering matched to detector resolution is preferred over simple linear interpolation (pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 5-7).

- **Standardless ZAF**: When no physical standards are measured, k-ratios are model-calculated from pure-element theoretical yields, which introduces additional uncertainty from poorly known ionization cross-sections and fluorescence yields, particularly for L- and M-shell families (eggert2020effectofthe pages 2-3, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 4-5).

**Native oxide on virgin powder**: Virgin AlSi10Mg powder carries a thin (~4 nm) native Al₂O₃ oxide layer formed during gas atomization. This oxide is predominantly aluminum-based; silicon is essentially absent from the outermost surface (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 4-6, krajnikov2002surfacechemistryof pages 5-9).

**Oxide growth with aging**: The oxide thickness increases substantially with powder aging and reuse in laser powder bed fusion, growing from ~4 nm on virgin powder to ~18 nm after 6 months and ~38 nm after 30 months of reuse (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 8-10). The oxide becomes more heterogeneous over time. This progression is illustrated in XPS depth profiling data showing normalized oxygen intensity versus etch depth for powders of different ages (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg media 2ed30846).

**Mg surface segregation**: Magnesium is the only alloying element consistently enriched at the powder surface. XPS studies show that the Mg 1s peak is not observed on virgin AlSi10Mg powder surfaces but increases significantly with powder aging, indicating time-dependent Mg accumulation at the surface (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 4-6). Surface enrichment factors of 4–8× relative to bulk composition have been reported for Al-Mg alloy powders (krajnikov2002surfacechemistryof pages 9-11, davis1995degassingandsurface pages 6-7). The surface Mg is present in oxidized form (as MgAl₂O₄ spinel and possibly Mg(OH)₂), not as metallic Mg (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 8-10, davis1995degassingandsurface pages 1-2). STEM-EDX line scans across spatter particle oxide nodules directly demonstrate the Al, O, and Mg enrichment in the oxide scale relative to the underlying alloy (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg media 23d8ecdd).

**Si surface depletion**: Silicon is absent from as-atomized powder surfaces and appears only after sputtering into subsurface layers, where it exists in elemental (non-oxidized) form (krajnikov2002surfacechemistryof pages 5-9, krajnikov2002surfacechemistryof pages 9-11). This means surface EDS measurements can under-report Si relative to bulk chemistry.

**Spatter particle oxide**: Spatter particles, which are re-introduced into powder feedstock during LPBF processing, exhibit coarse nodular oxide scales 50–125 nm thick, composed mainly of Al and Mg oxides—far thicker and more heterogeneous than the native oxide on standard powder particles (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 4-6, raza2021degradationofalsi10mg media 5149095b, raza2021degradationofalsi10mg media 66456a65).

**Implications for EDS quantification**: These surface phenomena mean that EDS analysis of loose, surface-mounted powder on carbon tape will systematically overestimate Mg (due to surface segregation), overestimate O (due to surface oxide), and potentially underestimate Si relative to certified bulk composition values obtained by ICP-OES. The effect worsens with powder aging, reuse, and the presence of spatter contaminants. Cross-sectioning and polishing powder particles in epoxy to expose bulk interior cross-sections is therefore essential for obtaining EDS compositions that are representative of the true alloy chemistry. Even with cross-sections, the outermost ring of each particle section will contain oxide-affected material, and analyses should target particle interiors away from the surface oxide shell.

---

**Summary Recommendation**: For reliable quantitative compositional analysis of AlSi10Mg powder, a multi-technique approach is recommended. ICP-OES should serve as the primary method for certified bulk composition verification. SEM-EDS with standards-based k-ratio quantification on polished cross-sections provides spatially resolved compositional data at the particle and phase level. WDS/EPMA should be employed when the highest accuracy for trace Mg quantification is required or when peak overlap validation is needed. XPS/AES analysis provides essential complementary information about surface oxide chemistry and Mg segregation that explains discrepancies between surface-sensitive and bulk analytical methods.
