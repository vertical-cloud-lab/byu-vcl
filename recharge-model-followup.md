## Governance and Approval of a University Recharge / Service Center: Offices, Roles, and Regulatory Framework

### 1. Introduction and Scope

At a private U.S. university such as Brigham Young University (BYU), establishing a recharge center (also called a service center, core facility, or cost-recovery unit) that provides additive-manufacturing and materials-testing services to both internal and external users requires a multi-layered governance process. Although BYU's specific institutional recharge-center policy is an internal administrative document not published in the scholarly literature, the governance architecture is well-documented across peer private research universities—including Northwestern University, Vanderbilt University Medical Center, Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, the University of California system, and the University of North Carolina—and is shaped by binding federal cost-accounting requirements. The analysis below synthesizes the authoritative literature on core facility administration, federal regulations, and best practices to describe the typical approval chain and governance steps.

### 2. Federal Regulatory Framework

The foundational federal authority governing university service centers is the Uniform Guidance at **2 CFR 200.468**, which superseded OMB Circular A-21 in 2014. This provision addresses "specialized service facilities" (e.g., animal care facilities, computing centers, and by extension additive-manufacturing or materials-characterization centers) and requires that the costs of services provided be recovered through user charges based on actual allowable costs (hockberger2026abrfcoreadministrators pages 1-2). Key principles include:

- **Allowability and Allocability**: Only allowable direct costs may be included in rates charged to federally sponsored users. Certain costs (e.g., entertainment, alcohol, bad debts, fundraising) are expressly unallowable (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6).
- **Break-Even Operation**: The center may not operate as a profit center. If cumulative surpluses exceed approximately two to three months of operating income, rates must be reduced, additional staff hired, or other appropriate expenses incurred in the following year. Conversely, deficits should trigger rate increases or expense reductions (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6).
- **Uniform Internal Rates**: All federally funded users—whether internal or external—must be charged the same base rate. This base rate is the allocable and allowable direct cost of delivering the service at reasonable capacity (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6).
- **External Users**: Indirect costs (F&A) may be charged on top of the base rate to external users, including both nonprofit and commercial organizations (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).
- **No Commercial Undercutting**: If a comparable commercial vendor operates within a defined radius, the university may not offer services to commercial customers below the commercial market rate (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).

In addition, the NIH published **FAQs for Costing of NIH-Funded Core Facilities (NOT-OD-13-053)** in 2013, which provides comprehensive operational guidance. This document was developed with iterative input from the ABRF Core Administrators Network (CAN), ensuring alignment with the practical realities of university service centers (hockberger2026abrfcoreadministrators pages 2-3).

Tax implications also arise: income from commercial customers may constitute **Unrelated Business Income (UBI)** under IRS Publication 598 unless it can be shown that the service is substantially related to the university's educational or research mission. The Finance Office, General Counsel, and Office of Research must assess UBI exposure before accepting external commercial work (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).

### 3. The Typical Approval Chain

The literature consistently identifies a core leadership team and a set of central administrative offices that must participate in establishing and governing a recharge center. Ideally, this includes "a committed scientific director, experienced core director or manager, business administrator with experience in core facility support, engaged faculty advisory committee, and central oversight and support provided by a department, school, or center along with the Office for Research and/or Finance" (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3). Each member of this team contributes a critical element: technical experience, business savvy, research needs and vision, financial support, or regulatory compliance (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3).

### 4. Governance Steps: Establishing a New Center and Annual Rate Approval

The process of standing up a new recharge center and subsequently renewing its rates each year follows a structured multi-step workflow. The literature on university core facility management describes this as analogous to the budgeting cycle used in any cost-recovery unit: planning, costing, allocating income, comparing expenses to revenue, setting priorities, adjusting, approving, and monitoring (jr2001fiscalmanagement pages 4-6, jr2001fiscalmanagement pages 1-4).

#### 4.1 Establishment Process

The establishment of a new center begins with a **planning and needs-assessment stage**, in which the faculty lead and department chair consult with senior management and investigators to determine demand, service scope, and mission fit (jr2001fiscalmanagement pages 4-6, jr2001fiscalmanagement pages 1-4). A **business plan** is then prepared by the core director/manager, defining cost centers, projected utilization, staffing, equipment, space, subsidy assumptions, and a service menu (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 1-2, jr2001fiscalmanagement pages 6-9). **Cost analysis and rate calculation** follow, with the Controller/Financial Services office reviewing methodology to ensure that rates are based on allowable, allocable, and appropriate direct costs and that the center will break even over time rather than become a profit center (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10). The Office of Research and Sponsored Programs reviews **compliance implications**, including whether work for external users should be handled as fee-for-service or as sponsored research/contract activity, and whether export control, safety, IRB/IACUC, or other regulatory approvals are required (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 2-3, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4). **Academic approvals** from the department chair, faculty advisory committee, and college dean confirm strategic fit, resource commitments, and subsidy support (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3). Finally, **central financial/research approval** validates the overall structure, billing procedures, and audit readiness before launch (hockberger2026abrfcoreadministrators pages 2-3, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11).

#### 4.2 Annual Rate Approval Process

Federal guidelines require that rates be reviewed and adjusted regularly (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10). The annual cycle begins with the core director collecting prior-year financial actuals and current utilization data, then recalculating rates using updated allowable costs and utilization forecasts (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11). Carryforward surpluses or deficits from the prior year must be factored in to maintain break-even compliance (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6, jr2001fiscalmanagement pages 9-12). The Controller/Cost-Accounting Office validates the rate methodology, and the Office of Research confirms ongoing federal compliance. Internal vs. external pricing, subsidy levels, and UBI exposure are reassessed (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10). Academic leadership—the faculty director, advisory committee, department chair, and dean—review whether the service mix, demand, and subsidies remain justified (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 11-13). The final proposed rates are submitted for central approval, implemented as of the new fiscal year, and documented with supporting cost-study records for audit purposes (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5, hockberger2026abrfcoreadministrators pages 2-3).

### 5. Application to an Additive-Manufacturing and Materials Services Center at BYU

For a BYU additive-manufacturing and materials service center specifically, the governance steps outlined above would apply with particular attention to several domain-specific considerations:

- **Cost Centers**: Separate cost centers should be established for each major service line (e.g., metal laser powder bed fusion, polymer FDM/SLA printing, mechanical testing, materials characterization), as cost-center granularity improves rate accuracy and equitable charging (jr2001fiscalmanagement pages 6-9, jr2001fiscalmanagement pages 9-12).
- **Equipment Depreciation**: Additive manufacturing equipment is capital-intensive; depreciation may be included in rates provided it is not already captured in the institution's negotiated F&A rate (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10, gregory2020buildingaquality pages 1-2).
- **External Users**: Commercial users of additive-manufacturing services may generate significant revenue, but this creates UBI risk. The Finance Office and General Counsel must assess whether the services are "substantially related" to the university's research and educational mission (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).
- **Quality Management**: Because additive manufacturing involves process-sensitive outputs, establishing SOPs, quality controls, and possibly QMS principles analogous to ISO 9001 is best practice and may be required for external contract work (gregory2020buildingaquality pages 2-4, gregory2020buildingaquality pages 4-5, gregory2020buildingaquality pages 1-2).

### 6. Summary

The governance of a university recharge center is a shared responsibility distributed across academic, research, and financial offices. The federal Uniform Guidance at 2 CFR 200.468—together with NIH costing FAQs (NOT-OD-13-053) and IRS rules on UBI—establishes the regulatory floor for rate-setting and compliance (hockberger2026abrfcoreadministrators pages 1-2, hockberger2026abrfcoreadministrators pages 2-3, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7). Internally, the approval chain flows from the originating faculty lead and department chair, through the faculty advisory committee and college dean, to central offices including the Office of Research/Sponsored Programs, the Controller/Financial Services/Cost-Accounting Office, the Budget Office, and (where one exists) a Rate-Review or Service Center Oversight Committee (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6, hockberger2026abrfcoreadministrators pages 2-3). Annual rate review is not optional; it is required to maintain break-even compliance and audit readiness (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5). Although BYU's specific internal policies were not available in the scholarly literature, the governance framework described here reflects the consensus of peer private research institutions and binding federal requirements that apply uniformly to all institutions receiving federal research funding.

**Note on Limitations**: BYU-specific institutional policy documents (e.g., BYU Financial Services recharge-center policy, BYU Office of Research and Creative Activities guidelines) are internal administrative documents not indexed in the academic literature. Readers seeking BYU-specific procedures should consult the BYU Controller's Office and the BYU Office of Research and Creative Activities directly. The framework presented here is generalized from authoritative peer-institution practices and federal regulations that govern all U.S. universities administering federal research funds.

## Administrative & financial operation (internal vs. external billing)

Question: How does a university recharge / service center operate administratively and financially on a day-to-day basis? Describe the mechanics: the internal recharge fund/account structure, how internal users are billed via internal journal/interdepartmental transfers vs. how external (non-university) users are invoiced through accounts receivable, sales-tax and unrelated business income tax (UBIT) considerations for external sales, surplus/deficit carry-forward, and the financial controls and reporting cadence. Include practical examples and cite institutional policy and Uniform Guidance (2 CFR 200.468).

## Administrative and Financial Operations of University Recharge / Service Centers

### 1. Overview and Regulatory Framework

University recharge centers (also called service centers, core facilities, or shared resource laboratories) are discrete organizational units that provide specialized instrumentation, technical expertise, and analytical services to the institution's research community. The U.S. National Institutes of Health defines core facilities as "centralized shared resources that provide access to instruments, technologies, services, as well as expert consultation for scientific and clinical investigators," noting that "in general, core facilities recover the cost, or a portion of the cost, of providing service in the form of user fees that are charged to an investigator's funds, often to NIH or other federal grants" (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 1-2). These facilities operate as small businesses embedded within an academic research institution, requiring a dual management structure encompassing both scientific excellence and sound financial administration (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 1-2, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3).

The governing federal regulatory framework for U.S. institutions is found in the Uniform Guidance (2 CFR 200, Subpart E), which superseded OMB Circular A-21 for educational institutions and OMB Circular A-122 for nonprofits (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6). Section 2 CFR 200.468 specifically addresses the costs of "specialized service facilities," requiring that such facilities charge users on the basis of actual costs at reasonable operating capacity, using rates that reflect only allocable and allowable costs, and that the facility operate on a break-even basis over time. The literature confirms that "core facilities may only pass along allocable and allowable direct cost to internal users of the facility" and that "the indirect cost cannot be included in charges to internal users, as these costs have already been charged to the federal grant paying for the service" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6). Certain direct costs are categorically unallowable under federal rules, including advertising, alcoholic beverages, bad debts, entertainment, fund-raising, public relations, contributions, donations, and debt principal payments (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6).

### 2. The Internal Recharge Fund / Account Structure

Each service center is typically assigned a dedicated self-supporting fund or account (often called a "recharge account" or "operating fund") within the university's general ledger. This account functions as a clearing account through which all operating expenses flow as debits and all recharge revenue flows as credits. Each laboratory within a larger facility may hold its own budget with a pre-agreed end-of-year income, expenditure, and percentage recovery forecast (otoole2024aperspectiveinto pages 2-3, otoole2024aperspectiveinto pages 2-2). At the University of York's Bioscience Technology Facility, for example, six specialist laboratories each maintain individual budgets but report collectively to the department/faculty, so that fluctuations in any one lab's budget are offset against the others (otoole2024aperspectiveinto pages 2-3).

The financial management system should be able to "track expenses and revenue on a daily, weekly, monthly, and yearly basis," with metrics for expenses including "personnel, service contracts, supplies, and professional development" and metrics for income including "recharge revenue (instrument usage and services), grant support, external income, program income, and institutional subsidies" (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11). Annual reports typically include a balance sheet for the last completed fiscal year, percentage change in revenue and expenses year-to-date, and estimated expenses and revenue for the next fiscal year (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5).

Federal guidelines require that rates be "reviewed and adjusted regularly" and that they be based on allowable direct costs in the context of projected utilization (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10). As a practical example, the University of York calculates instrument rates using the UK Transparent Approach to Costing (TRAC) model. For a hypothetical instrument, the rate calculation might include: annual service contract (£20,000), specific consumables (£10,000), specific instrument staff time (£10,000), instrument depreciation at £15,000, facility and administrative staff time (£10,000), and overheads (£5,000), totaling £70,000 in full economic costs. Dividing by an estimated 1,000 efficient usage hours per year yields a full economic cost rate of £70/hour. Charity-funded users, who cannot be charged depreciation or overhead, would pay a reduced rate of £50/hour (otoole2024aperspectiveinto pages 4-5, otoole2024aperspectiveinto pages 5-5). It is common practice to create rates for different categories of users—internal, external academic, external commercial, and subsidized—but all rates must abide by the principles outlined in federal cost guidelines (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10).

Rate-setting is typically performed annually or at regular intervals, with rates "approved annually" by appropriate institutional authorities (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5). If the estimate of efficient usage is set too high, rates will be underpriced and the facility will under-recover its costs; if set too low, it may generate a surplus that "is usually not permitted for governmental or charity funded research and would result in a failure at audit" (otoole2024aperspectiveinto pages 4-5).

### 4. Internal Billing: Journal Entries and Interdepartmental Transfers

When an internal university user (a PI, department, or center) uses a service center, billing is accomplished through internal recharge mechanisms rather than external invoicing. The typical workflow proceeds as follows:

1. **Usage recording:** The user reserves an instrument or orders a service through a web-based core facility management system (e.g., iLab Solutions, Stratocore), which captures the user's university account/chartstring, the service performed, the duration, and the applicable rate (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11).

2. **Charge posting:** At a regular billing cycle (typically monthly), the system generates internal journal entries or interdepartmental transfer (IDT) transactions that debit the user's sponsored project account, departmental fund, or center grant and credit the service center's recharge account. The system can "automatically transmit charges to the primary financial systems" (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11). Users can "split charges for a single facility order across multiple accounts" and "use available accounts across all of the facilities in the system" (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11).

3. **Reconciliation:** PIs receive "electronic notification of who is using their accounts and monthly account summaries," while business administrators can "track and report on all orders placed on accounts" and "dispute facility charges during the billing process" (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 11-13).

4. **Controls:** The system validates internal users and account information against university enterprise systems before charges are posted (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11).

### 5. External Billing: Invoicing Through Accounts Receivable

When the service center serves an external (non-university) customer—whether a nonprofit organization, another academic institution, or a commercial entity—the billing process shifts to formal invoicing through the institution's Accounts Receivable (A/R) office (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 2-3).

Key procedural elements include:

- **Service agreements and purchase orders:** Before commencing work, institutions typically require a Laboratory Service Agreement or equivalent contract that specifies the scope of work, project amount, invoicing schedule, and terms and conditions. Many customers prefer to pay with a purchase order (PO), and most institutions are set up to accept them (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 10-11).

- **Invoice generation:** External invoices are generated and sent through the university's A/R system. The financial management system should allow facility managers to "send invoices to external users" and allow external users to "pay external invoices with a credit card through the system" where institutionally permitted (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11).

- **Revenue tracking:** It is "extremely useful to be able to 'see' all of your expenses, revenue, and core account balances and to track this against your own recordkeeping of core usage and billing so that you know what you are owed" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4). When an external client uses services from more than one core and makes a lump-sum payment, systems must be in place to direct funds to the appropriate core accounts (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4).

- **Aging and collections:** Financial management systems should support "aging reports (accounts receivable)" to track outstanding balances (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11).

### 6. Rate Differentiation: Internal vs. External Users

A critical compliance requirement is that all customers paying with federally sponsored funds must be charged the same base rate, regardless of whether they are internal or external to the institution (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6). However, external users may appropriately be charged additional amounts:

- **External nonprofit users:** It is "highly recommended" to charge external nonprofit clients the appropriate indirect-cost rate on top of the base direct-cost rate. Institutions often have "a process for internal 'taxation' of external revenue received by cores to recover indirect costs, in which case, your core will lose money if the indirect cost is not part of your charge to external customers" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6).

- **Commercial (for-profit) users:** There is no upper limit on what can be charged to commercial clients, though rates may not be lower than those of comparable commercial providers within a defined radius of the institution (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7). Academic external users from outside the university are typically charged the same rate plus an additional indirect cost, while commercial users are charged with an additional standard commercial uplift (otoole2024aperspectiveinto pages 4-5).

### 7. Sales Tax and Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT) Considerations for External Sales

External sales by university service centers raise two distinct tax considerations:

**Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT):** IRS Publication 598 defines unrelated business income (UBI) as income not "substantially related to the charitable, educational, or other purpose that is the basis of the organization's exemption" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7). The IRS has further clarified that "the term research, for this purpose, does not include activities of a type normally carried on as an incident to commercial or industrial operations, such as testing or inspecting materials or products, or designing or constructing equipment" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7). In practice, income from external nonprofit clients paid using federally sponsored grants is "unlikely to be considered UBI," and "the IRS has typically shown no interest in NPO clients during audits, regardless of the form of payment" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7). Income from commercial organizations, however, "may be taxable, unless it can be shown that it is related to the research mission of your organization, e.g., through a sponsored project, which results in benefits for the common good of the institution or results that appear in a scientific publication" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7). The IRS has shown particular interest in "pass-through" services that are part of a supply chain for the customer, in which case "the revenue will very likely be considered unrelated and taxable" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7). Annual reports should document whether UBI was reported to central administration (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5). Institutions are strongly advised to consult with General Counsel, Research, and/or Finance Offices for guidance on the taxable consequences of external business (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).

**Sales tax:** While the published literature on service center operations focuses primarily on UBIT, state-level sales tax obligations also apply in many jurisdictions. Services provided to external customers may be subject to state and local sales tax depending on the nature of the service and applicable state law. Institutions typically rely on their Tax or Finance Office to determine whether specific services are taxable and to ensure proper collection and remittance.

### 8. Surplus/Deficit Carry-Forward and the Break-Even Requirement

The overarching financial goal for a university service center is to break even over time (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10). Under Uniform Guidance principles (2 CFR 200.468), service centers must not operate as profit centers when charging federally funded users.

**Surplus management:** If a facility generates an overall surplus exceeding approximately two to three months of operating income, corrective action must be taken for the following year. Actions may include "lowering the base direct-cost recovery rate, hiring new staff, or incurring additional appropriate expenses" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7). A surplus is "usually not permitted for governmental or charity funded research and would result in a failure at audit" (otoole2024aperspectiveinto pages 4-5). The University of York's BTF "does not run at a profit and so does not generate surplus income, which can be set aside for renewing ageing equipment," though depreciation income is captured by the university for broader equipment replacement programs (otoole2024aperspectiveinto pages 7-7).

**Deficit management:** If a deficit is generated, the facility must "take action to reduce expenses or raise rates the following year to recoup the loss" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7). An accurate cost study is necessary "to avoid generating deficits that force managers to raise rates above what customers are willing to pay. Once this occurs, the facility may become unsustainable and force the institution to consider unpleasant options" (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10).

**Carry-forward mechanism:** Surpluses and deficits are carried forward within the service center's recharge account into the next fiscal year's rate calculation. The accumulated balance informs the annual rate review: a growing surplus signals that rates should be reduced, while a persistent deficit indicates rates must increase or costs must be cut. "Either way, careful records must be maintained and made available to federal officials when requested" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).

### 9. Financial Controls and Reporting Cadence

Effective financial management requires a layered system of controls and regular reporting:

- **Cost study and rate approval:** Rates must be supported by a documented cost study and approved annually by the appropriate institutional authority (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10).

- **Automated billing systems:** Institutions increasingly deploy centralized, web-based financial management systems that allow managers to track reservations, accept multiple payment methods, facilitate billing and reconciliation, and export usage data for reports (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11).

- **Annual reporting:** Core facilities are typically required to submit annual reports to institutional administrators covering management, staffing, financial performance, customer base, resource management, communications, institutional impact, and strategic planning (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5). One scoring methodology used at Northwestern University evaluates financial management on a 10-point scale based on the prior fiscal year recharge income/operating expense ratio scored relative to peer facilities, along with grant activity (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 7-8).

- **Ongoing monitoring:** The financial management system should track "expenses and revenue on a daily, weekly, monthly, and yearly basis" and compute key ratios such as "ratio of recharge income to total operating costs, ratio of internal to external income, ratio of recharge income to subsidy, percentage change in recharge income over time" (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11).

- **Compliance assurance:** Facilities must follow institutional policies and procedures, NIH FAQs for costing of funded core facilities (NOT-OD-13-053), and applicable federal regulations including the Uniform Guidance (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 6-7, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 8-10). Annual report evaluation should confirm that research safety certifications and business continuity plans are updated, and that the facility follows institutional and federal compliance requirements (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 6-7, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3).

- **Program review:** Some institutions have built periodic external program review into their standard processes for evaluating core facilities, "picking 2 or 3 to evaluate each year," which "has the added benefit of removing the stigma of being reviewed, given that all facilities are expected to undergo review eventually" (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 2-3).

### 10. Practical Example: End-to-End Financial Workflow

Consider a hypothetical university microscopy core facility with annual operating costs of $500,000 (staff salaries $300,000; service contracts $80,000; consumables $40,000; depreciation $50,000; administrative support $30,000). The facility projects 5,000 billable instrument-hours per year at reasonable capacity.

**Internal rate calculation:** Allowable direct costs total $450,000 (excluding depreciation if already in F&A). Dividing by 5,000 hours yields an internal rate of $90/hour. This rate is charged to all federally funded internal users equally.

**External academic rate:** The base $90/hour plus the institution's negotiated indirect cost rate (e.g., 55% of modified total direct costs on applicable components) might yield approximately $115–$130/hour.

**External commercial rate:** The full cost plus indirect plus an institutional commercial uplift might yield $150–$200/hour. Revenue from commercial users can fund equipment purchases that would otherwise not be appropriate expenses for a core serving only internal users, provided "accurate and precise accounting is maintained to ensure that new equipment purchases are made only from COM-generated funds" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).

**Billing:** Internal users are billed monthly through automated journal entries debiting their sponsored project chartstrings. External users receive formal invoices through A/R, typically with Net-30 payment terms. The facility tracks recharge income/operating expense ratios monthly and reports annually (turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 10-11, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5).

**Year-end reconciliation:** If the facility ends the year with a $50,000 surplus (representing approximately 1.2 months of operating costs), this is within the acceptable range but signals that rates should be modestly reduced for the following year. A $100,000 surplus (2.4 months) would require more aggressive action (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).

In summary, university recharge/service centers operate within a tightly regulated financial framework that requires cost-based rate setting, nondiscriminatory pricing for federally funded users, distinct billing pathways for internal versus external customers, careful attention to UBIT and sales tax on external sales, mandatory break-even operations with surplus/deficit carry-forward, and robust financial controls supported by automated management systems and regular institutional reporting.

## Credit-card payment by external users

Question: Can a US university recharge center / core facility accept payment by credit card from external customers? Address PCI-DSS compliance, merchant-account requirements, credit-card surcharge / convenience-fee rules, whether card processing fees can be recovered, and how credit-card payments interact with federal cost principles when some users pay federal grant funds. Note any restrictions that prevent internal (university) users from paying recharge charges by card (they typically must use internal transfers). Cite institutional policies and PCI/merchant guidance.

## Credit Card Acceptance by University Recharge Centers / Core Facilities for External Customers

### 1. Overview: Can a Core Facility Accept Credit Cards from External Customers?

Yes, a US university recharge center or core facility can accept credit card payments from external customers, but only if the university has established the institutional infrastructure and procedures necessary for authorizing and handling credit card transactions. As Hockberger et al. (2013) caution in their ABRF best-practices guidance, "your institution may not accept credit cards, as it has not established procedures for authorizing and handling credit-transaction confirmation," and they advise core facility managers to "check whether your institution has a policy or infrastructure in place for handling credit cards before proceeding" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4). External customers frequently prefer to pay by credit card, but purchase orders (POs) and invoicing under a formal Laboratory Service Agreement are the more common mechanisms (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 10-11).

### 2. PCI-DSS Compliance

Any university unit that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data is subject to the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI-DSS). Under PCI-DSS, all merchants—including university departments—must comply with a set of data-security controls covering network security, encryption, access management, vulnerability testing, and information security policies. Universities are typically classified as Level 4 merchants (processing fewer than 1 million Visa transactions annually across all merchant IDs combined, though some large institutions may reach higher levels), and must annually complete a Self-Assessment Questionnaire (SAQ) appropriate to their payment channel and validate compliance.

Critically, universities generally operate under a centralized PCI governance model: a treasury, bursar, or information-security office oversees all merchant identification numbers (MIDs) and payment channels institution-wide. A core facility that wishes to accept credit cards must coordinate with this central office to obtain or be covered by an approved merchant account, use only approved payment terminals or payment gateways, and ensure that cardholder data is never recorded or stored outside approved systems. Hockberger et al. (2013) reinforce this point by noting that card acceptance depends entirely on whether the institution has built the necessary procedures and infrastructure (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4). A core facility that independently collects card numbers—for example, by writing them on paper forms or storing them in spreadsheets—would violate PCI-DSS and expose the university to fines and breach liability.

### 3. Merchant Account Requirements

Merchant accounts at universities are virtually always established and managed centrally—typically by the treasury, controller, or bursar's office. Individual departments or core facilities generally cannot open their own merchant accounts. To begin accepting cards, a recharge center must request approval from the central merchant-services office, justify the business need, select an approved payment method (point-of-sale terminal, hosted payment page, or integrated billing-system gateway such as iLab/CORES), and agree to ongoing PCI compliance obligations. The central office negotiates processing rates with the acquirer/processor, manages the relationship with card brands, and ensures that all departmental merchants operate within the university's PCI scope. Hockberger et al. (2013) advise core directors to consult their Finance Office and become familiar with "standard business procedures and recordkeeping practices at your institution, including handling of invoices, billing, and accounts receivable" before offering services to external customers (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 2-3).

### 4. Credit Card Surcharge and Convenience Fee Rules

#### Surcharges
Following the settlement in *In re Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation* (2013), US merchants are generally permitted by Visa and Mastercard rules to impose a surcharge on credit card transactions, subject to several conditions: the surcharge cannot exceed 4% (or the merchant's actual cost of acceptance, whichever is lower); the surcharge must be clearly disclosed before the transaction; and surcharges cannot be applied to debit or prepaid card transactions. However, several US states (e.g., Connecticut, Massachusetts, and Puerto Rico, among others that have varied over time) prohibit or restrict surcharges by state law. Many universities also prohibit surcharges as a matter of institutional policy, even where state law permits them.

#### Convenience Fees
A convenience fee is legally and functionally distinct from a surcharge. Under card-network rules, a convenience fee may be imposed only when the merchant offers a non-standard payment channel as an alternative (e.g., paying by phone or online for a service that is normally billed in person or by mail). Convenience fees are generally subject to acquirer approval, must be a flat fee (not a percentage), and typically cannot be imposed in a card-present (point-of-sale) environment. For a core facility, labeling a card-processing cost recovery as a "convenience fee" would require meeting these specific criteria and obtaining acquirer and institutional approval.

#### Practical Implication for Core Facilities
Hockberger et al. (2013) explicitly note that "credit card companies charge a transaction fee for processing requests" and warn that "depending on your institutional direct-cost guidelines, you may not be able to recover this cost in your service rates and may need to identify an alternate source of funding to cover this expense" (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4). This underscores the practical difficulty: even if surcharging or convenience fees are technically permissible under card-network and state rules, institutional policy or federal cost-principle constraints may prevent the core from passing these costs on to customers.

### 5. Interaction with Federal Cost Principles (2 CFR 200 / Uniform Guidance)

The question of processing-fee recovery becomes considerably more complex when some users pay with federal grant funds. Under the OMB Uniform Guidance (2 CFR Part 200, formerly OMB Circular A-21), core facility/recharge center rates charged to federally sponsored projects must reflect only the allocable and allowable direct costs of providing the service (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6). Hockberger et al. (2013) articulate four key guidelines:

1. **Uniform base rate.** Federally funded customers must be charged the same base rate regardless of whether they are internal or external (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6).
2. **Indirect-cost treatment.** Internal users cannot be charged indirect costs in the recharge rate because facilities and administrative (F&A) costs are already recovered on the grant through the institution's negotiated F&A rate. However, indirect costs may be—and should be—charged to external users (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).
3. **Allowability.** Certain direct costs are never allowable as charges to federal grants (e.g., advertising, entertainment, bad debts) (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6).
4. **Break-even requirement.** Core facilities must not become profit centers; surpluses beyond approximately 2–3 months of operating income require rate adjustments in the following year (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 6-7).

The Uniform Guidance framework, as described in detail by Azoulay et al. (2026), requires universities to meticulously separate direct and indirect costs and to apply federally negotiated F&A rates only to modified total direct costs (azoulay2026indirectcostrecovery pages 9-12, azoulay2026indirectcostrecovery pages 7-9). Credit card processing fees present a classification challenge: if they are treated as an allocable direct cost of the core's operations, they could theoretically be included in the base service rate, but this would raise the rate charged to all federally funded users (internal and external alike), which may be viewed as inappropriate since the fee arises only from the payment method chosen by particular external customers. Alternatively, if the processing fee is treated as an institutional overhead or administrative cost, it would typically be captured in the F&A pool rather than the recharge rate. Either way, a differential surcharge that applies only to credit card users paying with federal funds would be inconsistent with the uniform-rate requirement.

### 6. Restrictions on Internal (University) Users Paying by Credit Card

Internal university users—faculty, staff, and students paying from departmental or grant accounts—typically must pay recharge charges through internal mechanisms such as interdepartmental transfers (IDTs), journal entries, or automated billing through core-management software (e.g., iLab Solutions/Agilent CORES). There are several reasons for this restriction:

- **Avoidance of unnecessary transaction costs.** An internal credit card payment would cause the university to pay merchant discount fees (typically 2–3%) to process a transaction that is effectively an internal transfer of funds within the same legal entity.
- **F&A accounting integrity.** Internal recharge rates exclude indirect costs because F&A is already recovered on the underlying grant or departmental budget. A credit card transaction would bypass the internal accounting controls that ensure this separation (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 4-6, hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4).
- **Grant cost-tracking.** Internal transfers create a direct audit trail linking the recharge to the specific grant or cost center, which is essential for federal reporting and compliance. Credit card transactions would obscure this linkage.
- **Institutional policy.** Most universities explicitly prohibit the use of university-issued purchasing cards (P-cards) for internal recharge payments as a matter of financial policy.

Hockberger et al. (2013) describe core facilities as operating on a "direct-cost recovery model" with formal internal and external billing structures, and emphasize the importance of tracking "expenses, revenue, and core account balances" against recordkeeping of core usage and billing (hockberger2013bestpracticesfor pages 3-4, turpen2016metricsforsuccess pages 3-5).

In summary, a US university recharge center can accept credit card payments from external customers, but doing so requires centralized institutional infrastructure for PCI-DSS compliance and merchant-account governance. Credit card processing fees pose a significant cost-recovery challenge: they generally cannot be passed through as a surcharge or convenience fee without navigating card-network rules, state law, and institutional policy, and they cannot easily be embedded in the federally mandated base recharge rate without raising uniform-rate and allowability concerns under 2 CFR 200. Internal university users are restricted from paying by credit card because internal transfers preserve the accounting controls needed for proper F&A treatment and federal grant compliance, and avoid incurring unnecessary merchant discount fees on intra-institutional transactions.

# Analysis of a 501(c)(3) Nonprofit Intermediary for University Recharge Center Services

## 1. Overview and Feasibility

The concept of spinning out a separate 501(c)(3) nonprofit—or utilizing a university-affiliated nonprofit foundation or research institute—to act as an external intermediary that aggregates demand from outside companies and individuals and purchases services from a university recharge center is structurally feasible and has precedent in several related domains. However, it introduces a complex web of tax, governance, and compliance considerations that must be carefully managed. The analysis below addresses each dimension the user identified, drawing on literature regarding nonprofit governance, university financial structures, cloud laboratory networks, and distributed manufacturing intermediaries.

## 2. Advantages of the Intermediary Model

Several structural advantages justify considering this model. First, demand aggregation can smooth utilization of university equipment, lower per-unit transaction costs, and create a single point of contact for external users who may find university procurement processes cumbersome (armer2023supportacademicaccess pages 1-2). Cloud lab providers such as Emerald Cloud Lab and Strateos have demonstrated this value: commercial cloud labs charge high entry prices (greater than $250,000/year for Emerald Cloud Lab general access; greater than $100,000 for a single Strateos method), and academic institutions have found that departmental sponsorship and bulk-rate negotiation can reduce barriers (armer2023supportacademicaccess pages 2-4, armer2023supportacademicaccess pages 1-2). Carnegie Mellon University became the first university to sponsor cloud lab accounts by negotiating bulk rates and offering access, training, and community support to its members (armer2023supportacademicaccess pages 2-4).

Second, the intermediary can serve as a liability partition, absorbing contract, IP, data privacy, and regulatory compliance risk that the university might prefer not to bear directly. Third, researchers at universities have noted that open innovation networks, technology transfer offices, and research parks create valuable boundary-spanning functions between academic research and industry (markman2008researchandtechnology pages 1-3). Markman et al. (2008) identified three technology transfer office archetypes—a traditional university structure, a nonprofit research foundation, and a for-profit venture extension—noting that these structural forms vary in autonomy and commercialization effectiveness (markman2008researchandtechnology pages 1-3, huyghe2014technologytransferoffices pages 1-4).

## 3. Conflict of Interest

Conflict of interest is a central risk. University faculty or administrators who control or influence both the recharge center and the intermediary nonprofit face loyalty conflicts that can manifest as preferential pricing, biased access, or steering of business. Molk and Sokol (2021) extensively document that nonprofits generally have weaker governance monitoring than for-profit entities, and that universities specifically are vulnerable to self-dealing—one study found that one-fourth of private colleges do business with trustees' companies (molk2021thechallengesof pages 3-5). The same authors note that nonprofits lack shareholder enforcement mechanisms, and state attorneys general serve as the primary external enforcement agent for nonprofit governance (molk2021thechallengesof pages 12-15).

To mitigate conflict-of-interest risk, the intermediary should adopt a written conflict-of-interest policy, maintain a board with an independent majority, require recusals for conflicted transactions, and ensure arm's-length contracting with documented fair-market-value analysis.

## 4. Related-Party Transactions

The intermediary's primary commercial relationship—purchasing services from its affiliated university—is by definition a related-party transaction. Under state nonprofit corporation law, management owes fiduciary duties of loyalty and care that require scrutiny of related-party dealings (molk2021thechallengesof pages 12-15). Under federal tax law, Section 4958 of the Internal Revenue Code imposes excise taxes on "excess benefit transactions" between a 501(c)(3) and its "disqualified persons" (insiders who exercise substantial influence over the organization) (molk2021thechallengesof pages 15-17). Form 990 Schedule L requires public disclosure of transactions with interested persons. State attorneys general also have concurrent oversight authority over conflicted transactions, including the power to approve or challenge them (molk2021thechallengesof pages 12-15).

Mitigation requires a formal master services agreement with benchmarked pricing, independent board approval, no below-market terms unless clearly mission-justified and documented, and periodic external audit.

## 5. Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT)

Under IRC Sections 511–514, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit owes UBIT on income from a trade or business that is (1) regularly carried on and (2) not substantially related to the organization's exempt purpose. If the intermediary's primary activity is reselling university recharge center services to private companies for their commercial benefit, the IRS could characterize this as an unrelated trade or business, triggering UBIT liability. The IRS requires nonprofits to operate in accordance with their exempt purpose, and excessive commercial activity can jeopardize exempt status entirely (molk2021thechallengesof pages 15-17).

However, IRC Section 512(b)(7)–(9) provides a notable research exception: income from research performed for any person by a college, university, or hospital, or by an organization operated primarily for fundamental research with results made freely available to the public, is excluded from UBIT. If the intermediary nonprofit is itself a university or qualifies under a related exception, some income may be sheltered. The intermediary should avoid a pure resale framing and instead tie activities to educational access, research infrastructure democratization, workforce development, or open-science purposes. A taxable subsidiary may be appropriate for clearly commercial service lines.

## 6. Private Inurement and Private Benefit

Section 501(c)(3) prohibits net earnings from inuring to the benefit of any private individual—the private inurement doctrine (molk2021thechallengesof pages 15-17). The private benefit doctrine is broader: it applies to any private party, not just insiders, and requires that any private benefit be qualitatively and quantitatively incidental to the organization's exempt purposes. If the nonprofit primarily channels subsidized university capacity to a small number of private firms, or if university insiders receive discounted access, the IRS may find impermissible private benefit or inurement.

The intermediary must ensure a broad charitable class of beneficiaries, publish eligibility and pricing criteria, avoid insider discounts, and document how any private benefit to commercial users is incidental to the broader educational or scientific mission.

## 7. University External-User Rate and Markup Policy

Under OMB Uniform Guidance (2 CFR 200), university service centers and recharge facilities must charge internal users rates designed to recover costs without generating a surplus, and these rates must not discriminate among federally sponsored and non-federally sponsored activities (radecki2021universitybudgetmodels pages 7-9, radecki2021universitybudgetmodels pages 9-11). However, universities are generally permitted—and often required by policy—to charge external (non-university) users at market-based or higher rates to avoid subsidizing outside parties with institutional resources funded in part by tuition, state appropriations, or federal grants.

A critical compliance question is whether the intermediary nonprofit would be classified as an internal or external user. If the university treats the intermediary as "internal" when the ultimate beneficiaries are external commercial users, this could distort cost recovery, violate recharge center policies, and create audit risk with the university's cognizant federal agency for indirect costs. The university should classify the intermediary based on the ultimate beneficiary, apply external-user rates when services flow to non-university customers, and document the pricing policy transparently. Carnegie Mellon's approach of department-sponsored cloud lab access with negotiated bulk rates offers a model, though it was structured transparently as an academic access arrangement rather than a disguised internal pricing scheme (armer2023supportacademicaccess pages 2-4).

## 8. Liability

A separately incorporated 501(c)(3) intermediary can partition liability, protecting the university from downstream contract disputes, customer claims, data breaches, and regulatory violations arising from the intermediary's operations. However, separateness must be real—if the intermediary is merely an alter ego of the university, courts may pierce the corporate veil. The intermediary should maintain separate formalities (separate board, separate finances, separate insurance, separate contracts), and should carry appropriate general liability, errors and omissions, cyber, product liability, and directors and officers insurance.

## 9. Analogous Structures in Cloud Labs and Distributed Manufacturing

Several existing models illustrate how intermediaries have been structured in analogous contexts:

**Cloud labs.** Emerald Cloud Lab and Strateos operate as commercial entities that serve as intermediaries between researchers and automated laboratory infrastructure (adam2024theautomatedlab pages 1-3, armer2023supportacademicaccess pages 1-2). Their pricing models cater primarily to industry, and their entry costs have been prohibitive for most academic users. Carnegie Mellon addressed this by sponsoring departmental cloud lab accounts, negotiating bulk rates, and providing training—functioning as a university-level demand aggregator (armer2023supportacademicaccess pages 2-4).

**Manufacturing USA institutes.** The Digital Manufacturing and Design Innovation Institute (DMDII), managed by UI LABS, is a nonprofit consortium that includes private companies, research universities, community colleges, and nonprofit organizations, funded through a $70 million federal investment matched by nonfederal partners (beckmann2016developingthedigital pages 1-4, beckmann2016developingthedigital pages 4-7). The Digital Manufacturing Commons platform, hosted by DMDII as an "industry-neutral, trusted third party," provides an open collaboration environment connecting vendors, researchers, and intermediaries into a digital manufacturing ecosystem (beckmann2016developingthedigital pages 4-7). Similarly, America Makes (formerly NAMII) is managed by the National Center for Defense Manufacturing and Machining (NCDMM), a nonprofit operator (beckmann2016developingthedigital pages 4-7).

**Biofoundries.** The London Biofoundry at Imperial College operates as a university-based facility providing shared automated infrastructure and standardization for industrial translation, embedded in multiple consortia and partnerships that serve the broader bioeconomy (farzaneh2021biofoundriesarea pages 1-2, farzaneh2021biofoundriesarea pages 2-4). The Global Biofoundry Alliance enables protocol and data sharing across distributed facilities worldwide. Gomez-Marquez and Hamad-Schifferli (2019) documented the broader trend from centralized to distributed biological manufacturing, noting that distributed foundries can be organized as elite-access or open-access facilities with varying governance models (gomez‐marquez2019distributedbiologicalfoundries pages 2-3, gomez‐marquez2019distributedbiologicalfoundries pages 1-2).

**University-affiliated nonprofit research foundations.** Markman et al. (2005, 2008) identified the nonprofit research foundation as a recognized archetype for university technology commercialization—distinct from both the traditional university structure and the for-profit venture extension (markman2008researchandtechnology pages 1-3, huyghe2014technologytransferoffices pages 1-4). Well-known examples include the Wisconsin Alumni Research Foundation (WARF), SRI International (originally the Stanford Research Institute), and Battelle Memorial Institute, each of which serves as an intermediary between academic research and external users with separate nonprofit governance.

## 10. Recommendations and Conclusions

Spinning out a 501(c)(3) nonprofit intermediary to aggregate external demand for university recharge center services is structurally viable and has precedent in multiple domains, from Manufacturing USA institutes to university-affiliated research foundations to cloud lab access programs. However, the model introduces significant compliance obligations across multiple regulatory regimes.

**The concept makes the most sense when** (a) the intermediary has a genuine public-benefit mission (e.g., democratizing access to advanced research infrastructure, supporting SMEs, training, reproducibility, or regional innovation); (b) the volume of external demand is large enough to justify the governance and compliance overhead of a separate entity; (c) the intermediary is structured with sufficient independence from the university to maintain arm's-length transactions; and (d) the university's recharge center policies and federal cost-accounting obligations are respected by treating the intermediary as an external user paying appropriate external rates.

**The concept is riskiest when** (a) the intermediary primarily serves a small number of private firms without broader public benefit; (b) university insiders control both sides of the transaction without independent oversight; (c) the intermediary is essentially a pass-through entity whose activities are indistinguishable from ordinary university operations; or (d) the demand-aggregation function resembles ordinary commercial brokering without a substantial nexus to exempt purposes.

Key structural safeguards include: independent board governance with a majority of disinterested directors; written conflict-of-interest and related-party transaction policies; arm's-length master services agreements with benchmarked pricing; classification of the intermediary as an external user of the recharge center; UBIT analysis and filing of Form 990-T if required; transparent Form 990 reporting including Schedule L for related-party transactions; separate legal formalities, insurance, and finances; and mission-linked eligibility and access criteria that demonstrate public benefit exceeds any private benefit.
