# Precedents for Enterprise–Academic Interactions

Survey of established models for how universities and industry (including venture capital) interact, compiled to inform how the Vertical Cloud Lab (VCL) at BYU might structure relationships with companies, investors, and other external partners. See [issue #155](https://github.com/vertical-cloud-lab/byu-vcl/issues/155).

The precedents below fall into five broad families:

1. [Industry-sponsored research and consortium models](#1-industry-sponsored-research-and-consortium-models)
2. [Shared facility and "user facility" models](#2-shared-facility-and-user-facility-models)

## 1. Industry-sponsored research and consortium models

### NSF Industry–University Cooperative Research Centers (IUCRC)

The longest-running formal template (established 1973) for pooled industry funding of academic research. Companies pay tiered membership fees (Full / Associate / Affiliate) in exchange for a vote on the research portfolio and non-exclusive, royalty-free access to center IP. NSF requires that **90% of membership fees go to direct research costs (max 10% overhead)**, which makes membership attractive relative to one-off sponsored research agreements. Affiliate membership exists specifically to handle conflict-of-interest cases such as faculty-founded companies.

- [IUCRC program overview](https://iucrc.nsf.gov/about/) · [Membership agreement structure](https://iucrc.nsf.gov/industry/joining-a-center/membership-agreement/) · [NSF 20-570 solicitation](https://www.nsf.gov/pubs/2020/nsf20570/nsf20570.htm)
- **Relevance to VCL:** the natural "many small sponsors" model — a membership consortium around a shared self-driving-lab capability, rather than one anchor sponsor.

### Anchor-sponsor labs: MIT–IBM Watson AI Lab and BP–Berkeley EBI

At the other end of the spectrum, a single corporation funds a named lab on campus:

- **MIT–IBM Watson AI Lab** (2017): 10-year, **$240M** IBM investment; joint MIT/IBM research staffing; 210+ funded projects and 1,500+ peer-reviewed papers. Widely cited as the modern template for a co-branded, co-staffed industry lab embedded in a university. ([MIT News announcement](https://news.mit.edu/2017/ibm-mit-joint-research-watson-artificial-intelligence-lab-0907), [lab site](https://mitibmwatsonailab.mit.edu/about/))
- **Energy Biosciences Institute** (2007): BP committed **$500M over 10 years** to UC Berkeley, LBNL, and UIUC — one of the largest industry–academic collaborations ever formed. It also drew sustained criticism about corporate influence over public-university research agendas, a useful cautionary note. ([Berkeley announcement](https://newsarchive.berkeley.edu/news/media/releases/2007/02/01_ebi.shtml), [EBI overview](https://vcresearch.berkeley.edu/research-unit/energy-biosciences-institute), [SSTI commentary on the controversy](https://ssti.org/unholy-alliance-uc-berkeleys-500-million-deal-bp-challenges-traditional-public-private-partnerships))

### Acceleration Consortium (University of Toronto)

The closest precedent in the self-driving-lab (SDL) space specifically. Launched 2021; anchored by a **$200M Canada First Research Excellence Fund grant** (largest federal research grant to a Canadian university) that leveraged roughly **$300M in matching partner commitments** from ~30 academic, government, and industry partners. Operates 6+ SDLs at U of T plus partner facilities, with member access to 30+ SDLs worldwide.

- [U of T announcement](https://www.utoronto.ca/news/u-t-receives-200-million-grant-support-acceleration-consortium-s-self-driving-labs-research) · [AC labs and facilities](https://acceleration.utoronto.ca/ac-labs-and-facilities)
- **Relevance to VCL:** demonstrates that large public funding can be the anchor that *attracts* industry matching, rather than industry leading.

## 2. Shared facility and "user facility" models

### CMU–Emerald Cloud Lab

The most direct precedent for a university *cloud lab*: Carnegie Mellon partnered with Emerald Cloud Lab (ECL) to build the first academic cloud lab (~**$40M facility**, part of a $250M science-facilities investment). The deal structure is instructive: **CMU owns the building, instruments, and all resulting IP; ECL licenses the orchestration software and operated the facility initially**. The facility runs 100+ instrument types, 24/7, with 100+ concurrent experiments.

- [CMU announcement](https://www.cmu.edu/news/stories/archives/2021/august/first-academic-cloud-lab.html) · [C&EN coverage](https://cen.acs.org/business/informatics/Carnegie-Mellon-partners-cloud-based/99/i32) · [Drug Discovery World analysis](https://www.ddw-online.com/learn-about-carnegie-mellons-40-million-life-science-gamble-14670-202111/)
- **Relevance to VCL:** a clean split between university-owned hardware/IP and enterprise-provided software/operations is a proven contract shape for exactly the kind of facility VCL is building.

### Core facilities / recharge centers and federal user facilities

Universities routinely run shared instrumentation as **recharge centers**: fee-for-service access billed at cost to internal users (federal rules require break-even direct-cost rates), with external/industry users typically charged direct cost plus allocated F&A. DOE national-lab user facilities extend the same idea nationally — free access for open (publishable) research, full-cost recovery for proprietary work.

- [NYU recharge center policy](https://www.nyu.edu/about/policies-guidelines-compliance/policies-and-guidelines/recharge-center-policy.html) · [Drexel core facility definitions](https://drexel.edu/core-facilities/resources/definitions/) · [UNC core facilities guidance](https://osp.unc.edu/core-facilities/core-facilities-guidance-faqs/)
- **Relevance to VCL:** the default administrative vehicle for letting companies *pay to use* VCL equipment without a full sponsored-research agreement. The open-vs-proprietary pricing split from DOE user facilities is a well-tested pattern.

## 3. Venture capital and spinout models

### Baseline: Bayh–Dole and the technology transfer office (TTO)

Since the Bayh–Dole Act (1980), universities own inventions made with federal funds and license them out — the legal substrate for everything below. BYU's TTO handles IP protection, licensing, and startup formation ([techtransfer.byu.edu](https://techtransfer.byu.edu/about-us)).

### The Engine (MIT)

MIT's answer to the "deep tech is too slow for conventional VC" problem: spun out in 2016 as a venture firm + accelerator for *tough tech* (hardware, materials, energy, bio). Now split into **The Engine Accelerator** (nonprofit incubator: shared wet labs, fab space, machine shops) and **Engine Ventures** (VC fund, **>$1B AUM** across three funds, including a $398M third fund; LPs include MIT and Harvard). Funds run up to **18-year timeframes** — explicitly structured to de-risk technical work over the first ~4 years.

- [The Engine (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Engine_(organization)) · [MIT TLO on Engine Ventures](https://tlo.mit.edu/resources/engine) · [Forbes on the $398M Fund III](https://www.forbes.com/sites/alexknapp/2024/06/18/mit-spinout-the-engine-ventures-raises-new-400-million-fund-to-tackle-techs-toughest-problems/) · [TechCrunch on Fund II](https://techcrunch.com/2020/10/27/the-engine-fund-two/)
- **Relevance to VCL:** materials/hardware startups emerging from VCL-type work fit the "tough tech" thesis precisely; patient-capital structures exist for them.

### Oxford Science Enterprises (OSE) and Osage University Partners (OUP)

Two complementary university-VC arrangements:

- **OSE** is an independent **~£1.1B** investment company with a standing relationship to the University of Oxford: it receives (in most cases) **half of the university's equity stake in each spinout** in exchange for guaranteed early capital, investing £50k–£25M from pre-seed through exit. ([OSE (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford_Science_Enterprises), [OSE site](https://www.oxfordscienceenterprises.com/))
- **OUP** is a US fund that partners with **100+ universities and research institutes**, taking assignment of the universities' *participation rights* in their spinouts' funding rounds — a low-friction way for a university to monetize its spinout pipeline without running a fund. Oxford joined as an Associate Partner. ([Oxford–Osage MoU](https://innovation.ox.ac.uk/news/oxford-university-welcomes-osage-university-partners-oxford-innovation-ecosystem/))
- **Relevance to VCL:** the OUP model in particular is accessible to a single lab/university without raising a fund.

### Flagship Pioneering (venture creation)

Rather than investing in external startups, Flagship *originates* companies in-house (~100 explorations/year → 8–10 spinouts/year, e.g., Moderna) — the "venture foundry" model. Relevant as the pattern VC firms use when they want to own the discovery process itself, and as a potential partner type for labs that generate platform technologies. ([Flagship model overview](https://www.northernbiostrategies.com/insights/flagship-pioneering-reinventing-vc/), [Flagship site](https://www.flagshippioneering.com/news/press-release/flagship-ventures-evolves-name-flagship-pioneering))

### Physical Intelligence: professors co-founding a VC-backed startup while staying in post

The strongest *current* example of active professors co-founding a heavily VC-backed company without giving up their faculty positions. [Physical Intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_Intelligence_Inc.) (San Francisco, founded early 2024) builds foundation models for robots; its co-founders include **Chelsea Finn** — [Stanford assistant professor in CS/EE](https://ai.stanford.edu/~cbfinn/) who continues to run the IRIS lab and [is still accepting PhD students](https://ai.stanford.edu/~cbfinn/) — and **Sergey Levine** — [UC Berkeley associate professor in EECS](https://vcresearch.berkeley.edu/faculty/sergey-levine), who continues to run the RAIL lab. The CEO seat went to a *non-tenure-line* co-founder: **Karol Hausman**, a former Google DeepMind researcher who [also taught at Stanford](https://techcrunch.com/2026/01/30/physical-intelligence-stripe-veteran-lachy-grooms-latest-bet-is-building-silicon-valleys-buzziest-robot-brains/) as an adjunct. Remaining co-founders (Brian Ichter, Quan Vuong, Adnan Esmail, and investor Lachy Groom) are full-time.

The capital involved is far beyond any faculty-founder precedent in the BYU docs: a [$70M seed at ~$400M (March 2024; Thrive Capital, OpenAI, Khosla, Sequoia)](https://startupintros.com/orgs/physical-intelligence), [$400M at $2.4B (Nov 2024; Jeff Bezos, OpenAI, Thrive, Lux)](https://www.therobotreport.com/physical-intelligence-raises-600m-advance-robot-foundation-models/), a [$600M Series B at $5.6B led by Alphabet's CapitalG (Nov 2025)](https://www.therobotreport.com/physical-intelligence-raises-600m-advance-robot-foundation-models/), and — as of July 2026 — [reported talks to raise ~$1B at an $11B valuation](https://news.fundsforngos.org/2026/07/13/ai-robotics-startup-physical-intelligence-targets-1-billion-funding-round-at-11-billion-valuation/).

Three structural features make the professor-co-founder shape work:

1. **Faculty co-founders hold equity and provide scientific direction, but the executive titles sit with full-time people.** This is exactly the split [Stanford's COI policy](https://doresearch.stanford.edu/policies/research-policy-handbook/conflicts-commitment-and-interest/faculty-policy-conflict-commitment-and-interest) prescribes: full-time faculty may found and own, but may not hold "line management or significant managerial responsibilities, or titles that suggest or connote such responsibilities" — so the professional CEO (Hausman) was in place from day one rather than installed later.
2. **The company was not built on a university IP license.** Physical Intelligence formed around the founders' robot-learning research lineage (largely developed at Google DeepMind and in academic labs, with new work done in-house) rather than around patents licensed from Stanford or Berkeley — so no university sits on the cap table and no TTO license shapes the deal. That sidesteps the entire licensee-specific rulebook (board-seat caps, royalty splits, university equity).
3. **The professors' academic labs continue in the same field.** Finn and Levine still publish academic robot-learning research adjacent to the company's — the overlap is managed through their universities' disclosure/COI processes rather than by separating the fields. The *formal* arrangement (percent effort, consulting-day allocation, or partial leave) is not public for either founder; both are publicly listed only as active faculty plus "co-founder."

- **Relevance to VCL:** proof at the largest scale that "professor stays a professor" is compatible with institutional VC — but under Stanford/Berkeley policy, which permits precisely this shape. Mapped onto BYU's rules (see [the rules doc](byu-vc-founder-rules.md)), the same structure is posture 3 (equity founder) with the advisory/non-officer role BYU favors, and — because no BYU IP license would be involved — the $3M board-seat cap and ≥10% royalty forfeiture wouldn't even bite; the binding constraints would be the 4-days/month cap, the resource wall, and the officer-title bar. The transferable lesson is the org design: hire the full-time CEO at founding, keep faculty roles scientific and advisory.

### SBIR/STTR (non-dilutive federal bridge)

The standard non-dilutive path for faculty-founded startups to fund early R&D while partnering with their university. Key constraints: STTR requires **≥40% of work at the small business and ≥30% at the partner research institution**; SBIR requires the PI to be majority-employed by the company and ≥2/3 of work done there; routing an award's work back to the university wholesale is considered fraud. ([NSF SBIR/STTR Phase I solicitation](https://www.nsf.gov/funding/opportunities/sbirsttr-phase-i-nsf-small-business-innovation-research-small-business/505960/nsf24-579/solicitation), [SBIR vs STTR for university spinouts](https://www.bwcoconsulting.com/blog//nsf-sbir-vs-sttr-university-spinouts))

## 4. VC/philanthropy-backed alternative research organizations

A post-2020 wave of organizations funded by investors/philanthropists as alternatives or complements to academia:

- **Arc Institute / Arcadia Science** — billionaire-backed independent institutes offering academics freedom from grant cycles, often with university affiliations (Arc partners with Stanford, Berkeley, UCSF).
- **Arena BioWorks** — a **$500M**, for-profit institute (2024) where investors received up to 30% of spinout profits and research was done by permanent staff scientists rather than students. It **shut down in November 2025**, a significant cautionary data point on the pure-VC-funded institute model. ([Arena BioWorks (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arena_BioWorks), [STAT analysis](https://www.statnews.com/2024/02/02/arena-bioworks-scientific-research-model-funding-academia/), [Boston Globe on the shutdown](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2025/11/04/business/arena-bioworks-shutdown-research-institute/))
- **Focused Research Organizations (FROs)** via **Convergent Research** — philanthropically funded ($20–50M, 3–7 years), milestone-driven nonprofit "startup-shaped" research orgs for public-good problems that fit neither academia nor a for-profit. ([Convergent Research on FROs](https://www.convergentresearch.org/about-fros), [Issues in Science & Technology field notes](https://issues.org/focused-research-organizations-fro-marblestone-gamick-wang-fridman/))
- **Relevance to VCL:** an SDL infrastructure effort with clear technical milestones and public-good outputs is close to the FRO archetype; Arena's collapse suggests caution about models where investor returns are the primary sustaining mechanism for open-ended research.

## 5. BYU- and Utah-local programs

> For BYU's own policy rules on VC money and faculty founder roles (arm's-length vs. co-founder vs. equity holder), see [BYU rules on VC money and founder involvement](byu-vc-founder-rules.md).

- **BYU Technology Transfer Office** — IP protection, licensing, and startup formation for BYU inventions. ([techtransfer.byu.edu](https://techtransfer.byu.edu/about-us))
- **BYU Sandbox** (Rollins Center) — two-semester startup-building program (2020–); companies collectively valued at ~$149M with six Y Combinator acceptances — evidence of a functioning venture pipeline at BYU, though software-centric so far. ([Sandbox](https://rollins.byu.edu/programs/sandbox))
- **Crocker Innovation Fellows** — donor-funded (Gary & Ann Crocker) interdisciplinary innovation program; alumni built companies including Owlet. A precedent for *philanthropic* rather than corporate sponsorship of innovation training. ([Program overview](https://marriott.byu.edu/news/article?id=1827))
- **University of Utah PIVOT Center** — the nearest regional peer: a centralized commercialization office managing licensing, equity, accelerators, and a wet-lab incubator; 330+ startups to date, 7 spinouts and 25 licenses in FY2023. ([PIVOT Center](https://pivotcenter.utah.edu/), [launch announcement](https://attheu.utah.edu/announcements/u-establishes-partners-for-innovation-ventures-outreach-technology-pivot-center/))

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Membership consortium | NSF IUCRC | Many companies, tiered fees | ~$50k–100k/member/yr | University owns; members get royalty-free non-exclusive license | Pre-competitive research shared across firms |
| Anchor corporate sponsor | MIT–IBM; BP–EBI | One corporation | $240M–$500M / 10 yr | Negotiated; sponsor gets first rights | Big, aligned corporate partner |
| SDL consortium | Acceleration Consortium | Government anchor + industry match | $200M grant + $300M match | Consortium terms | National-scale SDL infrastructure |
| University cloud lab | CMU–Emerald Cloud Lab | University capital + vendor software | ~$40M | University owns instruments & IP; vendor licenses software | Exactly the VCL facility shape |
| Recharge / user facility | Core facilities; DOE user facilities | User fees | Cost recovery | Users keep their results; proprietary use pays full cost | Industry paying for instrument access |
| University-linked VC | The Engine; OSE | LPs incl. university endowments | $230M–$1B+ funds | Fund equity in spinouts | Capitalizing spinouts from lab IP |
| Pipeline participation | Osage University Partners | External fund | 100+ university partners | University assigns participation rights | Monetizing spinouts without running a fund |
| Venture creation | Flagship Pioneering | VC firm's own capital | 8–10 cos/yr | Firm founds & owns companies | Platform tech a VC wants to build around |
| Professor-co-founded VC startup | Physical Intelligence (Stanford/Berkeley) | Institutional VC (Thrive, CapitalG, Bezos, OpenAI) | $1.1B+ raised; $5.6B valuation | Company owns IP; no university on cap table; faculty founders hold equity, not officer titles | Faculty co-founding at venture scale while staying in post |

## Takeaways for the VCL

1. **The facility itself has a proven contract shape.** CMU–ECL shows a university can own the cloud-lab hardware and all resulting IP while an enterprise partner supplies orchestration software and operations know-how. That split protects academic interests while giving the company a durable commercial role.
2. **Industry access and industry sponsorship are different products.** Paid instrument access is cleanly handled through a recharge/user-facility mechanism (with an open-science vs. proprietary pricing split); influence over the research agenda is what consortium memberships (IUCRC-style) or anchor sponsorships sell. Keeping these separate avoids the agenda-capture criticism that dogged BP–Berkeley.
3. **For "venture capitalism" specifically, the accessible entry points are downstream of IP, not upstream of research.** A single lab realistically engages VC via (a) faculty/student spinouts licensed through BYU's TTO, funded by SBIR/STTR and tough-tech investors like The Engine; and (b) pipeline arrangements like Osage's, where the university assigns spinout participation rights rather than raising its own fund. Direct VC funding *of the lab itself* (Arena BioWorks) is the model with the weakest track record.
4. **Public/philanthropic anchors attract industry match, not the reverse.** The Acceleration Consortium turned a $200M federal grant into ~$300M of partner matching. For an SDL effort at BYU scale, a federal or philanthropic anchor (NSF center, FRO-style grant, or a donor in the Crocker mold) is the likelier first domino than a corporate one.
5. **Local proof points exist.** BYU Sandbox demonstrates BYU can produce venture-backed companies; U of U's PIVOT Center shows what a mature commercialization apparatus looks like in-state. VCL spinouts would be pushing on open doors, but in hardware/materials rather than software — which is precisely the gap patient-capital structures like The Engine were built for.
