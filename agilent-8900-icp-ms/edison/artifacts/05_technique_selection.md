## 2. Concentration Range and Dynamic Range

**XRF (WDXRF and EDXRF):** XRF is inherently well-suited for major and minor constituent quantification in solid metals, covering the range from low ppm up to tens of weight percent. The comparative table of analytical techniques presented by Adesina et al. lists WDXRF detection limits at the ppm level and EDXRF at sub-ppm for some configurations (adesina2025waystomeasure pages 7-8). However, practical quantification on handheld devices is strongest above approximately 0.1–0.5 wt%, and relative errors increase substantially below ~1 wt% (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 8-10).

**ICP-OES:** ICP-OES provides a wide linear dynamic range from low parts-per-billion in solution up to percent-by-mass levels, spanning approximately 4–6 orders of magnitude. It is widely used for routine multi-element analysis of metal sample digests because of its high matrix tolerance and simultaneous multi-element capability. The technique can determine over 70 elements from a single solution, including light elements (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 1-3, fontoura2022ismipoesa pages 6-7, fontoura2022ismipoesa pages 13-15).

**ICP-MS (ICP-QQQ):** Modern quadrupole ICP-MS instruments such as the Agilent 7900/8900 offer up to 11 orders of magnitude operating dynamic range, with sub-ng/L detection limits for many elements. For example, Al detection limits of 0.10 ng/L and Fe of 0.75 ng/L have been demonstrated on the 8900 platform (takahashiUnknownyearsiliconwaferanalysis pages 50-52, hsuUnknownyearanalysisofnanoparticles pages 50-52). However, this extraordinary sensitivity is optimized for trace and ultra-trace analysis. When applied to bulk alloy digests with high total dissolved solids (TDS), substantial dilution is required, which can push major-element concentrations out of the optimal measurement range while still leaving trace elements well within detection capability (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 1-3, sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 3-5).

## 3. Accuracy and Precision for Bulk Quantification

**XRF:** In a systematic comparison of EDXRF, WDXRF, LIBS, and spark-OES on nine commercial alloys (including aluminum alloys), Seidel et al. found that absolute deviations from the spark-OES benchmark were generally less than 1 wt% for main components and less than 0.25 wt% for minor elements below 2 wt%. Instrumental precision (2σ) was reported below 0.1 wt%, and EDXRF lateral homogeneity measurements showed central 50% spans typically within less than 0.2 wt% (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 8-10). WDXRF performed best for quantifying the main metal element directly because XRF can measure the matrix element itself, unlike difference-based OES methods (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 14-15). However, notable accuracy problems arise for light elements in light matrices: EDXRF overestimated Mg in an AlMg4.5Mn0.7 alloy by approximately 2 wt% (about 40% relative), and showed a 5000% relative deviation for Al in a very pure Al99.5 sample (though on a small absolute scale) (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 10-13). Kallio's thesis comparing OES, XRF, and SEM-EDS on metal alloys found XRF showed the largest variation among the three methods for precision, with about 50% match to standard values, and noted XRF limitations for elements below Z < 11 (kallio2025accuracyandprecision pages 41-44, kallio2025accuracyandprecision pages 51-55).

**ICP-OES:** ICP-OES is widely regarded as the most robust solution-based workhorse for quantitative alloy analysis. In the comparative Ti-alloy study by Figueiredo et al., ICP-OES served as the reference method against which WDXRF was compared, with trueness values generally between 87% and 118% (figueiredo2018qualitativeandquantitative pages 5-7). ICP-OES is commonly used for routine metal analysis because it tolerates the high matrix levels present in metal digests (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 1-3). Kallio found OES to be the most precise technique, with RSD values ≤8% even for elements at low concentrations, and low deviation even for light elements (kallio2025accuracyandprecision pages 41-44, kallio2025accuracyandprecision pages 51-55).

**ICP-MS:** ICP-MS excels at trace-level accuracy with spike recoveries of 90–110% and RSD below 6% for 38 elements demonstrated in high-matrix work. However, bulk alloy analysis is complicated by matrix-driven effects including signal suppression, cone deposition, and elevated background equivalent concentrations (BECs) in high-Al or high-Si matrices (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 1-3, shimamura2022multielementnanoparticleanalysis pages 88-90, hsuUnknownyearanalysisofnanoparticles pages 88-90). Calibration linearity is excellent (correlation coefficients >0.9999 over 10–10,000 ppb), but the need for heavy dilution of alloy digests means that major-element quantification becomes less advantageous compared to ICP-OES (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 3-5).

## 4. Sample Preparation Burden

**XRF:** This is the single greatest advantage of XRF: it can analyze solid metal directly, requiring only surface preparation (cutting a flat face, grinding, or polishing). No acid digestion is needed. The measurement is non-destructive, and sample integrity is preserved (adesina2025waystomeasure pages 5-7, figueiredo2018qualitativeandquantitative pages 1-2). Surface condition matters—rusty or heavily oxidized surfaces should be polished for reliable results (kallio2025accuracyandprecision pages 51-55). For pressed or fused bead analysis of powders, some preparation is needed, but it is far simpler than acid digestion.

**ICP-OES and ICP-MS:** Both require complete dissolution of the sample into a clear, particle-free aqueous solution before nebulization. For aluminum alloys, HCl is generally effective for dissolving the aluminum metal matrix, but HNO₃ alone tends to form passivating oxide layers on aluminum and is insufficient (lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 76-80). For silicon-containing alloys such as AlSi10Mg, hydrofluoric acid (HF) is typically required for complete dissolution of silicon phases (lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 76-80, kebbekus2003preparationofsamples pages 250-254, lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 48-53). The EPA 3052 protocol specifies microwave-assisted digestion with approximately 9 mL concentrated HNO₃ and 3 mL HF at 180°C for 15 minutes in sealed PTFE vessels (glaubensklee2022casestudiesof pages 102-102). Multicomponent acid mixtures (e.g., HCl + HNO₃ + HF ± H₂SO₄ or H₃PO₄) are used for refractory alloy phases (nakayama2022…anacid pages 3-4). HF use carries serious safety risks (extreme toxicity requiring calcium gluconate antidote, dedicated fume hoods, PTFE labware, and institutional safety protocols) (lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 76-80, lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 72-76). Fluoride can also form insoluble MgF₂ precipitates in Mg-bearing alloys, necessitating boric acid complexation post-digestion (lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 48-53). For silicon powder feedstock, HF is essentially mandatory for total dissolution. Overall, digestion for ICP analysis takes hours of active laboratory work per batch and introduces contamination risks, reagent blanks, and potential analyte losses (kebbekus2003preparationofsamples pages 250-254).

## 5. Element Coverage: Light Elements, Matrix Element, and Interferences

**XRF:** Most EDXRF/handheld devices can detect elements from Mg (Z = 12) to U, and WDXRF instruments under vacuum can reach down to approximately Na (Z = 11) or even lighter with specialized configurations (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 4-5, adesina2025waystomeasure pages 5-7). However, elements lighter than about Z = 11–14 are generally problematic, and even Mg, Al, and Si in an aluminum matrix present fundamental difficulties due to low X-ray fluorescence cross-sections combined with absorption by air and detector windows (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 10-13). Li, B, and Be are essentially unmeasurable by conventional XRF. The aluminum matrix itself is directly quantifiable by XRF—a significant advantage since ICP methods cannot easily measure the dominant Al component without very high dilution factors. Matrix effects (inter-element absorption and enhancement) require fundamental parameter (FP) corrections or empirical calibration with matrix-matched standards (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 4-5).

**ICP-OES:** After successful digestion, ICP-OES can measure essentially all elements of interest including Mg, Na, Li, B, Si, Al, and heavier transition metals. Spectral interferences are managed through wavelength selection, and modern simultaneous instruments offer multiple wavelength options per element. ICP-OES handles light elements better than XRF after dissolution is achieved (fontoura2022ismipoesa pages 6-7). The main spectral interference concern in Al alloy digests is line overlap from high Al concentrations on nearby analyte lines, manageable through line selection and background correction.

**ICP-MS (ICP-QQQ):** ICP-MS provides the broadest isotopic coverage but faces polyatomic interference challenges in Al and Si matrices. Specifically, ²⁸Si²⁸Si⁺ interferes with ⁵⁶Fe⁺, ³⁰Si¹⁶O⁺ interferes with ⁴⁶Ti⁺, and numerous other Si-based polyatomics affect Ni, Cu, and Ge (shimamura2022multielementnanoparticleanalysis pages 88-90, hsuUnknownyearanalysisofnanoparticles pages 88-90, takahashiUnknownyearsiliconwaferanalysis pages 88-90). The ICP-QQQ's MS/MS mode with reaction gases (H₂, He, NH₃, O₂) can effectively remove many of these interferences, but method development is required for each problematic analyte-matrix combination. Elements like P and S remain challenging at trace levels even with ICP-QQQ (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 3-5). B is volatile and may be lost during sample preparation if protocols are not specifically designed to retain it (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 3-5).

## 6. Standards, Calibration, and Certified Reference Materials

**XRF:** Calibration typically uses solid certified reference alloy discs from suppliers such as BAM (Germany), NIST, and MBH. WDXRF instruments commonly employ fundamental parameter (FP) corrections for inter-element matrix effects, calibrated against suites of Al-alloy CRMs covering elements including Mg, Si, Cr, Mn, Fe, Ni, Cu, Zn, Pb, and Sn (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 4-5, seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 2-4). Handheld EDXRF devices use built-in alloy libraries that may be less customizable. The number of calibration standards required for XRF is generally fewer than for OES methods due to simpler physics (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 13-14).

**ICP-OES/ICP-MS:** Calibration uses liquid standards prepared from 1000 mg/L single-element stock solutions, diluted in acid-matched matrices. For highest accuracy, matrix matching and use of SRMs (e.g., NIST SRM 1258-I, 1259) processed through the same digestion procedure is recommended (yamani2021preparationandcertification pages 3-6, yamani2021preparationandcertification pages 2-3). Multi-method certification of Al-alloy CRMs using XRF, AES, ICP-OES, and AAS across multiple laboratories is well-established and ensures traceability (yamani2021preparationandcertification pages 1-2, yamani2021preparationandcertification pages 9-10, yamani2021preparationandcertification pages 7-9). Internal standards (e.g., Sc, Y, Tb for ICP-MS) compensate for matrix-induced signal variations (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 1-3).

## 7. Throughput, Cost, and University Lab Practicality

**EDXRF/Handheld XRF:** Fastest measurement (approximately 60 seconds per spot), lowest infrastructure requirement, and lowest per-sample cost. Instrument costs range from approximately $50,000 to $200,000 (adesina2025waystomeasure pages 4-5, seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 4-5). Ideal for rapid screening in a university setting.

**WDXRF:** Laboratory-based, with measurement times of approximately 430–570 seconds per sample in the Seidel study. Higher capital cost than handheld EDXRF but much lower running costs than ICP methods. Once calibrated, routine per-sample cost is very low (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 4-5, seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 13-14).

**ICP-OES:** Measurement time is approximately 1–2 minutes per sample once digested, but the digestion step dominates total analysis time (hours per batch). Capital costs are moderate (approximately $100,000–$200,000). Running costs include argon gas and consumables (fontoura2022ismipoesa pages 6-7).

**ICP-MS/ICP-QQQ:** Highest capital cost ($400,000–$700,000 for an Agilent 8900), highest running costs, and greatest need for skilled operators and method development. Analysis throughput per se is adequate, but high-matrix method development, digestion, and quality control overhead make it the most labor-intensive option for routine bulk work (adesina2025waystomeasure pages 2-4).

### Overarching Recommendation

**XRF should be the first-line bulk composition tool** for aluminum alloys in a university setting. If WDXRF is available at BYU, it provides the best combination of accuracy, minimal sample preparation, and direct matrix-element measurement for major and most minor elements in solid alloy samples. Handheld EDXRF is excellent for rapid screening and sorting but should not be solely relied upon for accurate quantification of light elements (Mg, Si) in Al matrices, where it can show errors of 40% or more relative (seidel2021comparisonofelemental pages 10-13).

**ICP-OES adds value** when: (a) accurate quantification of light elements (Mg, Na, Li, B) is needed beyond XRF capabilities; (b) wet-chemistry verification of XRF results is desired; (c) elements below the XRF detection floor must be quantified; or (d) a traceable, widely standardized method (ASTM, JIS) is required for reporting (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 1-3, fontoura2022ismipoesa pages 6-7). The digestion burden is significant, especially for AlSi10Mg (requiring HF), but ICP-OES tolerates high-matrix digests well and provides excellent precision and dynamic range for alloy-level concentrations.

**ICP-MS / ICP-QQQ (the Agilent 8900) is best reserved** for: (a) trace impurities at ppm-to-ppb levels that are below ICP-OES detection limits; (b) elements where ICP-QQQ's reaction-cell chemistry uniquely resolves interferences (e.g., As, Se, P at ultra-trace levels in high matrices); (c) method development research; or (d) verification of specific trace specifications on incoming feedstock (sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 1-3, sakai2015determinationoftrace pages 3-5, adesina2025waystomeasure pages 2-4). It is generally not the most efficient first-line tool for bulk wt% composition of Al alloys because high dilution is required, major-element precision may not exceed ICP-OES, and operational complexity and cost are substantially higher.

**For silicon powder feedstock**, XRF provides the least burdensome path to bulk screening. If solution-based analysis is needed, HF-assisted microwave digestion is mandatory, and the resulting digest can be analyzed by either ICP-OES (for major/minor elements) or ICP-MS (for trace impurities) (lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 76-80, kebbekus2003preparationofsamples pages 250-254, lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 48-53).

A practical two-tier workflow—XRF for every sample, supplemented by periodic ICP-OES confirmation on digested aliquots—minimizes the digestion burden while maintaining analytical rigor and traceability through solid and solution CRMs (yamani2021preparationandcertification pages 3-6, yamani2021preparationandcertification pages 2-3, tao1990xrfproceduresfor pages 1-2).
