# Acid Digestion Protocols for Aluminum and Aluminum-Silicon Alloys for ICP-QQQ Analysis

## 1. Introduction and Dissolution Chemistry Fundamentals

Complete acid digestion of aluminum alloys and silicon-containing powders for ICP-MS analysis requires careful selection of acid mixtures matched to the sample matrix. The fundamental dissolution chemistry for the materials in question can be summarized as follows:

**Aluminum metal** dissolves readily in hydrochloric acid (HCl). A comparative study of aluminum alloy dissolution found that 5% HCl digested aluminum alloy pins at room temperature within one hour, whereas HNO₃ at room temperature was unable to digest a pin within 24 hours (brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 46-50). This is because aluminum forms a resistant oxide passivation layer in nitric acid, rendering HNO₃ alone ineffective for Al dissolution (lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 76-80, lotter2014analysisofzirconiuma pages 76-80). Aqua regia (HCl + HNO₃, 3:1 v/v) generates oxidizing chlorinated species (Cl₂, NOCl) that are more effective for rapid dissolution than single acids (lotter2014analysisofzirconiumc pages 76-80). Mixed acid systems with HCl dominant are therefore preferred for Al-matrix dissolution.

**Elemental silicon and silicides** are not dissolved by HCl or HNO₃ alone. Silicon is soluble only in HF + HNO₃ combinations (brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 46-50). Hydrofluoric acid is the primary reagent for dissolving silicate samples and silicon phases, producing volatile silicon tetrafluoride (SiF₄) during the reaction (lotter2014analysisofzirconiuma pages 76-80, lotter2014analysisofzirconiumb pages 76-80). For AlSi10Mg alloy (nominally ~10 wt% Si), significant silicon-rich phases (eutectic Si, silicides) will remain as undissolved particulates unless HF is included in the digestion mixture. For pure 1100 aluminum wire (>99% Al, Si typically <0.1%), HF is generally unnecessary.

Microwave-assisted digestion heats the liquid volumetrically rather than only at the surface, resulting in faster and more efficient digestion (williams1990microwaveovendigestions pages 25-31). Closed PTFE-lined vessels can be pressurized to allow digestion at temperatures well above the boiling points of the acid mixtures (williams1990microwaveovendigestions pages 25-31). For silicate-containing matrices, closed-vessel microwave digestion is considered superior to open-vessel hot-plate techniques because it provides efficient and homogeneous heat transfer, prevents loss of volatile elements and compounds (including fluorides and chlorides), and minimizes airborne contamination (zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 3-4).

**Critically, when HF is used on silicon-containing samples, closed-vessel digestion is essential.** HF reacts with Si to form volatile SiF₄; in an open vessel, SiF₄ escapes, leading to loss of silicon from the analytical solution and incomplete digestion. Closed-vessel digestion prevents SiF₄ volatilization and enables smaller HF doses with more complete digestion (wang2025determinationofsilicon pages 5-8).

For low-Si samples such as 1100 aluminum wire, a simple open-vessel hot-plate digestion in dilute HCl is adequate, fast, and avoids the complexity of microwave equipment.

### 3.1 Protocol A: 1100 Aluminum Wire (Open-Vessel, HCl-Based)

For low-silicon aluminum (≤0.1% Si), HCl-based dissolution on a hot plate is straightforward and well-supported. Weigh 50–100 mg of cleaned wire into a PTFE or PFA beaker, add 25–50 mL of 5% v/v HCl (trace-metal grade), and heat on a hot plate at 50–70 °C (brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 46-50, brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 50-55). At elevated temperature (>50 °C), aluminum alloy pins dissolved rapidly, enabling precise results within 2 minutes of digestion initiation (brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 50-55). At room temperature, dissolution requires approximately 1 hour. More impure aluminum alloys dissolve faster than high-purity grades (brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 50-55). After complete dissolution (clear solution, no visible residue), cool the solution, transfer quantitatively, and dilute to a final volume of 50 mL with 1–2% HNO₃ or 1% HCl to match your calibration matrix.

### 3.2 Protocol B: AlSi10Mg Powder (Closed-Vessel Microwave, HNO₃/HCl/HF + Boric Acid)

This is the recommended primary protocol for complete dissolution of both the aluminum matrix and the ~10% silicon content. Weigh 50–100 mg of AlSi10Mg powder into a PTFE/TFM microwave digestion vessel. Add 5.0 mL concentrated HNO₃, 2.0 mL concentrated HCl, and 1.0–2.0 mL concentrated HF (all trace-metal grade). Seal the vessel and run a microwave program ramping to 180–200 °C with a hold time of 20–30 minutes (nakayama2022selectinganacid pages 1-2, wang2025determinationofsilicon pages 5-8, lotter2014analysisofzirconiumb pages 48-53). After cooling, open the vessel and add saturated boric acid solution to complex residual fluoride (see Section 5 below).

### 3.3 Protocol C: AlSi10Mg Powder (Safer Alternative with HBF₄)

Tetrafluoroboric acid (HBF₄, 38–48% w/w) generates HF in situ while simultaneously providing boric acid to complex excess fluoride, thereby reducing the hazards of direct HF handling (zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 9-10, zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 1-3). An optimized protocol uses 5 mL HNO₃ + 2 mL HCl + 1 mL HBF₄ (total 8 mL) for 50–100 mg of sample, with microwave digestion at 180 °C for 30–60+ minutes (zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 1-3, zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 4-5). This method achieved quantitative recoveries (80–120%) for 34 elements in silicate-rich sediment reference materials (zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 1-3). HBF₄ is not acutely toxic like HF, no special HF-resistant ICP sample introduction is typically required, and no separate boric acid addition step is needed (zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 1-3, zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 9-10). This protocol should be validated against Protocol B using your specific alloy composition or a suitable certified reference material.

### 3.4 Protocol D: Silicon Powder Feedstock

For pure silicon powder, closed-vessel digestion with 5 mL HNO₃ + 2 mL HF is recommended, as HF is directly supported for complete dissolution of elemental silicon (balski2014determinationofboron pages 1-3, wang2025determinationofsilicon pages 5-8). A microwave program at 180 °C with 20–30 minutes hold, or a sealed water-bath digestion at equivalent conditions for approximately 30 minutes, is appropriate (wang2025determinationofsilicon pages 5-8). Post-digestion boric acid complexation is required to neutralize residual HF.

## 4. Is HF Required? Assessment for Each Sample Type

**For 1100 aluminum wire:** HF is **not required**. The silicon content is negligible (<0.1%), and HCl readily dissolves the Al matrix (lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 76-80, brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 46-50).

**For AlSi10Mg alloy:** HF (or HBF₄) **is required** for complete dissolution. Without a fluoride source, the ~10 wt% silicon in AlSi10Mg will remain as undissolved particulates, because elemental Si is insoluble in HCl, HNO₃, and aqua regia alone (brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 46-50). HF is specifically designated for "removal of silicon and destruction of silicates" (lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 76-80, lotter2014analysisofzirconiumc pages 76-80).

**For silicon powder feedstock:** HF **is essential**. Silicon is soluble only in HF + HNO₃ (brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 46-50, balski2014determinationofboron pages 1-3). Without HF, complete dissolution cannot be achieved.

## 5. Boric Acid Complexation: Neutralizing Residual HF

After HF-containing digestions, residual free fluoride ions in solution pose two problems: (1) they are corrosive to quartz components in the ICP sample introduction system (torch, nebulizer, spray chamber), and (2) they can form insoluble fluoride precipitates with elements such as Al, Ca, Mg, and Na, causing co-precipitation losses of analytes (makishima2009preciseelementaland pages 2-4, pinna2022howtoclean pages 1-2).

Boric acid (H₃BO₃) reacts with HF at room temperature to form fluoboric acid [BF₄]⁻ complexes. These complexes do not dissociate into free fluoride ions at room temperature, effectively "masking" the fluoride and making the solution compatible with standard ICP-MS sample introduction (wang2025determinationofsilicon pages 5-8). The stoichiometry for the complexation reaction requires a fluoride-to-boric-acid molar ratio of approximately 4:1 (4 moles F⁻ per mole H₃BO₃) to form [BF₄]⁻, meaning each mole of H₃BO₃ complexes 4 moles of fluoride (wang2025determinationofsilicon pages 5-8).

**Practical procedure:** For 2 mL of concentrated HF (~0.055 mol F⁻), the theoretical H₃BO₃ requirement is approximately 0.014 mol (~0.85 g). In practice, use an excess: add 10 mL of a 5% w/v H₃BO₃ solution (~0.5 g H₃BO₃), allow the reaction to proceed for 10–20 minutes at room temperature with mixing, then dilute to final volume (wang2025determinationofsilicon pages 5-8). Note that boric acid addition increases the total dissolved solids in the matrix, which can elevate ICP-MS background signals and introduce impurities from the boric acid itself (pinna2022howtoclean pages 1-2).

An alternative approach is evaporative HF removal: HF-containing digests can be evaporated to dryness (open-vessel or microwave-assisted), then reconstituted in HNO₃/HCl (pinna2022howtoclean pages 1-2, zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 3-4). A microwave-assisted "vessel-inside-vessel" protocol can remove up to 99.9% of HF by evaporative migration to a water scavenger inside a sealed system (pinna2022howtoclean pages 1-2). This avoids adding boric acid matrix but is more time-consuming.

## 6. HF Safety: Hazards, PPE, and Emergency Procedures

Hydrofluoric acid is one of the most hazardous reagents in the analytical chemistry laboratory. Unlike other mineral acids, HF penetrates intact skin and causes deep tissue destruction, including destruction of bone and tendons (juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 1-2, bajraktarovavaljakova2018hydrofluoricacidburns pages 2-3). Concentrated HF (>70%) can penetrate to the dermis within minutes, while low-concentration solutions (<14.5%) may produce delayed symptoms that are not immediately apparent or painful, with pain onset up to 24 hours after exposure (cornils2020hydrofluoricacid pages 1-4, juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 1-2). Systemic effects include hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, hyperkalemia, cardiac arrhythmias, and death from cardiac arrest; burns exceeding approximately 160 cm² can cause significant systemic toxicity (quickUnknownyearhoneywellhydrofluoricacid pages 6-7, bajraktarovavaljakova2018hydrofluoricacidburns pages 2-3).

**Required PPE for HF work:** Laboratory coat, long pants, closed-toed shoes, acid-resistant apron with upper-chest protection, tightly sealed vapor-protecting chemical splash goggles, full-face shield, inner nitrile gloves under HF-resistant outer gloves (e.g., Trionic O-240 or equivalent neoprene/butyl), and all work performed in a properly functioning fume hood (bajraktarovavaljakova2018hydrofluoricacidburns pages 2-3, juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 4-5, juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 3-4).

**Administrative controls:** Never work with HF alone; use a trained buddy system. Post "HF in use" signage. Ensure a dedicated HF first-aid kit with 2.5% calcium gluconate gel is immediately accessible in the work area. Confirm that safety showers and eyewash stations are functional and routinely tested (juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 4-5, juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 3-4).

**Emergency response:** Immediately flush exposed skin with copious water for at least 15 minutes. Apply 2.5% calcium gluconate gel and continuously massage, reapplying every 10–15 minutes until medical help arrives. Call 911 and inform responders of HF exposure. For eye exposure, irrigate for at least 30 minutes. Do not induce vomiting if ingested (cornils2020hydrofluoricacid pages 1-4, juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 3-4).

**Engineering controls:** Use HF-compatible containers (PTFE/PFA only). Closed-vessel microwave digestion is preferred to reduce operator exposure during the digestion step. Automated liquid-handling systems inside closed ventilated boxes have been implemented at some institutions to dispense and dilute HF (juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 3-4). Ensure all HF leaving a hood is diluted to <1% or fully sealed (juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 3-4).

## 8. Practical Recommendations for a University Lab Setting Up This Workflow

1. **Start with the simplest protocol that works.** For 1100 aluminum wire, begin with open-vessel HCl digestion (Protocol A) — no HF needed, minimal safety burden.

2. **For AlSi10Mg, strongly consider Protocol C (HBF₄) first.** Tetrafluoroboric acid provides the fluoride chemistry needed to dissolve silicon phases while avoiding the extreme acute toxicity of concentrated HF (zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 1-3, zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 9-10). Validate against a certified reference material (CRM) such as an aluminum-silicon casting alloy CRM.

3. **If HF is used (Protocol B or D), invest in safety infrastructure first.** Ensure your institutional Environmental Health & Safety (EHS) office has approved an HF SOP. Purchase 2.5% calcium gluconate gel, an HF spill kit, and HF-rated gloves before ordering HF. Train all personnel in HF first-aid procedures (juba2021lessonslearned—fluorideexposure pages 4-5).

4. **Use closed-vessel microwave digestion for all Si-containing samples.** This prevents SiF₄ loss, reduces HF exposure, and enables higher temperatures for more complete digestion (wang2025determinationofsilicon pages 5-8, williams1990microwaveovendigestions pages 25-31, zimmermann2020substitutinghfby pages 3-4).

5. **Validate completeness of digestion visually and analytically.** After digestion, inspect solutions for clarity — any suspended particulates indicate incomplete dissolution. Spike recovery tests and analysis of CRMs should be performed during method development.

6. **Manage fluoride after digestion.** If your Agilent 8900 ICP-QQQ is equipped with standard quartz sample introduction (torch, spray chamber), residual HF will damage these components. Either (a) complex fluoride with boric acid, (b) evaporate HF and reconstitute, (c) use HBF₄ which auto-complexes, or (d) install HF-resistant PFA/Pt sample introduction components.

7. **Use appropriate sample masses.** For ICP-MS (not ICP-OES), 50–100 mg of a metallic alloy diluted to 50 mL already provides very high analyte concentrations for major elements (Al, Si, Mg). Further dilution (100–1000×) will likely be needed to bring major-element concentrations into the linear calibration range while still maintaining adequate sensitivity for trace impurities.

8. **Prepare matrix-matched calibration standards.** The high-Al matrix can cause matrix effects in ICP-MS. Use internal standards (e.g., In, Rh, Sc) and consider standard addition or matrix-matched calibration for the most accurate results.
