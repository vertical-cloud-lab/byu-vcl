# HF-Free Digestion Methods for Silicon-Bearing Aluminum Alloys and Silicon Powder: A Comprehensive Review for ICP-QQQ Analysis

## 1. Introduction

Preparing silicon-bearing aluminum alloys (such as AlSi10Mg with ~10 wt% Si) and silicon powder feedstock for ICP-MS analysis presents a fundamental challenge: silicon and siliceous phases are among the most chemically refractory materials encountered in analytical chemistry. Hydrofluoric acid (HF) is the classical reagent for dissolving silica and silicon, forming volatile SiF₄, but HF poses extreme safety hazards—it penetrates skin readily, releases freely dissociable fluoride ions that cause deep tissue necrosis, systemic hypocalcemia, cardiac arrhythmias, and potentially fatal outcomes even from small exposures (ozcan2012possiblehazardouseffects pages 1-2, bajraktarovavaljakova2018hydrofluoricacidburns pages 2-3, ozcan2012possiblehazardouseffects pages 2-4). For a university laboratory seeking to avoid HF entirely, five alternative approaches are evaluated below.

## 2. Method-by-Method Evaluation

### 2.1 Tetrafluoroboric Acid (HBF₄) Digestion

**Reagents and Protocol:** HBF₄ is commercially available as ~48–50% aqueous solution, manufactured by reacting 70% HF with boric acid (meshri1986themoderninorganic pages 20-23). A demonstrated microwave digestion protocol for polymer samples uses ~250 mg sample + 4 mL high-purity HNO₃ + 2 mL 50% aqueous HBF₄ in a pressurized microwave system (e.g., ultraWAVE digester), with digestates subsequently diluted to ~3% acid concentration before ICP-MS screening of 68 elements (cuthbertson2024characterizationofpolymer pages 19-20).

**Hazard Comparison to Free HF:** HBF₄ is often described as a "safer" alternative to HF, but this characterization requires significant qualification. Aqueous HBF₄ exists as the solvated ion pair H₃O⁺BF₄⁻, and at room temperature the BF₄⁻ ion does not readily dissociate into free fluoride, meaning it does not aggressively etch glass or penetrate skin in the same manner as free HF (wang2025determinationofsilicon pages 5-8). However, HBF₄ "slowly hydrolyzes in a stepwise manner to [BF₃OH]⁻, [BF₂(OH)₂]⁻, [BF(OH)₃]⁻ and finally to the starting materials, H₃BO₃ and HF" (meshri1986themoderninorganic pages 20-23). This means that on heating during digestion, or upon prolonged storage, free HF is regenerated. HBF₄ is still classified as "hazardous and highly corrosive" (grant2019straightforwardandrelatively pages 6-7). Concentrated solutions cannot be stored in glass indefinitely, and commercial preparations include excess boric acid to suppress HF fumes and burns (meshri1986themoderninorganic pages 20-23). In practical terms, HBF₄ requires many of the same precautions as HF (fluoropolymer-compatible vessels, PPE, calcium gluconate on hand), though acute skin penetration risk is lower at room temperature.

**Silicon Recovery:** HBF₄ can attack silica-bearing matrices by generating fluoride ions at elevated temperature during digestion. However, direct evidence for quantitative dissolution of eutectic metallic silicon or Mg₂Si silicide phases in AlSi10Mg is lacking. The published protocols are validated primarily for polymers, soils, and sediments, not for high-Si metallic alloys (cuthbertson2024characterizationofpolymer pages 19-20). Validation with alloy-specific certified reference materials would be essential.

**ICP-MS Implications:** HBF₄ introduces boron into the digest, precluding boron determination. Fluoride species in solution can corrode glass spray chambers and torches, though the Agilent 8900 can be fitted with an inert sample introduction kit. The dissolved solids burden is moderate compared to fusion methods (pansu2006analysisofextractable pages 53-56).

### 2.2 Alkaline Fusion / Sinter Methods

**Lithium Metaborate (LiBO₂) Fusion—Preferred Flux:** Lithium metaborate is described as "quite basic, making it ideal for the dissolution of high silica-containing samples" (lotter2008identificationandquantification pages 44-47, lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 80-83, lotter2014analysisofzirconiumd pages 80-83). A key advantage over Na₂CO₃ fusion is that silica does not separate or precipitate upon acid dissolution of the LiBO₂ melt, whereas with sodium carbonate "this separation does occur" (lotter2008identificationandquantification pages 44-47). Optimized conditions for graphite (analogous high-refractory matrix) used a flux:sample ratio of 8:1 (e.g., 2.000 g LiBO₂ for 0.2500 g sample) in a platinum crucible at 950°C for 2.5 hours, followed by dissolution of the cooled melt in ~200 mL of 2.5–5% HCl with ultrasonic assistance for 2 hours (zhao2021optimizationoflithium pages 3-4). Lithium metaborate melts are "easily soluble in dilute acids" (lotter2014analysisofzirconium pages 80-83).

**Other Fluxes:** Na₂CO₃ fusion (mp ~850°C, or ~710°C mixed with K₂CO₃) can dissolve silica and siliceous compounds but causes silica precipitation during acidification (lotter2014analysisofzirconiumb pages 80-83, lotter2008identificationandquantification pages 44-47). Na₂O₂ (sodium peroxide) is a powerful oxidizing flux effective for refractory minerals but introduces very high sodium loads and is violently reactive with metallic aluminum. Li₂B₄O₇ (lithium tetraborate) is an acidic flux and is "less effective in dissolving high silica content materials" (lotter2014analysisofzirconiumc pages 80-83, lotter2014analysisofzirconiumd pages 80-83). Mixed lithium borate fluxes (49.75% Li₂B₄O₇ + 49.75% LiBO₂ + 0.5% LiBr) have been used at 1200°C for 30 min with flux:sample ratios of ~1.4:1 for nuclear decommissioning matrices, dissolved in 25% v/v HNO₃ and filtered at 0.22 µm before ICP-MS/MS measurement on an Agilent 8800 (braysher2019completedissolutionof pages 5-6).

**Silicon Recovery:** This is the strongest HF-free route for quantitative total silicon. LiBO₂ fusion "does not present the risk of elimination of silica" and "usually results in complete dissolution of the sample" (pansu2006analysisofextractable pages 18-21).

**ICP-MS Implications:** The large flux mass introduces substantial lithium and boron into solution. Li⁺ causes significant matrix effects on ionization/excitation equilibria in the plasma, with a "particularly strong effect on Na" determination (zhao2021optimizationoflithium pages 3-4). Both lithium and boron are precluded as analytes (pansu2006analysisofextractable pages 18-21). Greater dilution is needed "to avoid clogging the nebulizers which can result in a decrease in sensitivity of titration of trace and ultra-trace elements" (pansu2006analysisofextractable pages 18-21). For ICP-MS, typical TDS should remain below ~0.1–0.2%; with an 8:1 flux ratio and 0.25 g sample, the total dissolved solids in 200 mL would be ~1.1%, requiring further 10–50× dilution. This severely compromises trace-element detection limits. High-temperature fusion may also cause losses of volatile trace elements (pansu2006analysisofextractable pages 18-21). Platinum crucibles minimize contamination but are expensive.

### 2.3 Alkaline (NaOH/KOH) Solution Dissolution

**Protocol:** Concentrated NaOH (3–10 M) at 60–100°C dissolves aluminum metal to aluminate (Al(OH)₄⁻) within hours. Documented procedures include metered addition of 10 M NaOH over ~17 hours at ~92°C with NaNO₃ background electrolyte to limit hydrogen evolution, yielding ~1.25 M dissolved Al (mcfarlane2015dissolutionandseparation pages 1-4, mcfarlane2015dissolutionandseparation pages 12-15). Smaller-scale protocols use ~1 g Al in 50 mL of 3 M NaOH + 1.5 M NaNO₃ at 100°C (mcfarlane2015dissolutionandseparation pages 8-12). KOH at 0.1–1 M (pH >12) has been validated for dissolving amorphous SiO₂ nanoparticles at room temperature or under microwave conditions (ramp to 200°C, hold 7 min), with a minimum KOH:SiO₂ molar ratio of ~1.2 and recoveries >84% (bossert2019ahydrofluoricacidfree pages 8-9, bossert2019ahydrofluoricacidfree pages 1-2, bossert2019ahydrofluoricacidfree pages 9-10).

**Silicon Recovery:** This is the critical limitation. While NaOH readily dissolves the aluminum matrix, silicon in AlSi10Mg exists as eutectic metallic Si dendrites and possibly Mg₂Si, not as amorphous SiO₂. In caustic media, dissolved silicon (as H₄SiO₄/silicate) readily precipitates as aluminosilicate phases (amorphous → zeolite → sodalite → cancrinite), depending on temperature, pH, ionic strength, and seeding (mcfarlane2015dissolutionandseparation pages 4-8). Dissolution experiments on Al-6061 (0.52 wt% Si) in NaOH produced up to ~10 wt% solid precipitates including aluminosilicates (mcfarlane2015dissolutionandseparation pages 1-4). For AlSi10Mg with ~10 wt% Si, the aluminosilicate precipitation problem would be far more severe. Quantitative silicon recovery is therefore not achievable by this route.

**ICP-MS Implications:** The extremely high Na (or K) matrix causes severe ionization suppression and polyatomic interferences including NaAr⁺ (m/z 63, interfering with ⁶³Cu), NaCl⁺ species, and Na₂O⁺/Na₂OH⁺ (mccurdy2001optimisingicpmsfor pages 2-3). Physical matrix effects from high dissolved solids cause drift and cone fouling (rowley2000fundamentalstudiesof pages 41-47). The Agilent 8900's MS/MS mode can mitigate some polyatomic interferences but cannot eliminate the fundamental ionization suppression from high-Na matrices.

### 2.4 HNO₃ + HCl / Aqua Regia–Only Digestion

**Protocol:** Hydrochloric acid readily dissolves aluminum metal, while HNO₃ alone tends to form a resistant oxide passivation layer on aluminum (lotter2014analysisofzirconiumc pages 76-80, lotter2014analysisofzirconiuma pages 76-80). Aqua regia (HCl:HNO₃, 3:1 v/v) is "often more effective in the rapid dissolution of samples" because evolved Cl₂ and NOCl provide multiple oxidation/complexation pathways (lotter2014analysisofzirconiumc pages 76-80). A standard protocol for high-purity aluminum: "0.2500 g Al decomposed in 7 mL acid mixture (HCl/HNO₃ 3:1), made up to 25.00 mL with water" (kudermann1988characterizationofhighpurity pages 4-5). Alternatively, for larger samples: "10 g Al in 300 mL HNO₃ (1+2), evaporate to syrupy consistency (~60 mL), dilute to 300 mL" (kudermann1988characterizationofhighpurity pages 4-5).

**Silicon Recovery:** Silicon is "usually lost" during HCl-based dissolution of aluminum alloys (brown1999rapidaluminumalloy pages 14-17). Mineral acids without fluoride cannot attack elemental silicon or silicate networks; the Si remains as an insoluble white/grey residue that must be filtered before nebulization to avoid clogging. In soil analysis, after acid digestion "the digest was filtered, to remove the silica content, through an ashless 90 [µm filter]" (lotter2014analysisofzirconiumb pages 80-83).

**Trace Element Recovery:** Elements dissolved in the aluminum solid solution (Cu, Mg, Mn, Fe, Zn, Ti, Cr, Ni, etc.) will be quantitatively released into the acid digest. However, any trace elements physically trapped within or chemically bound to the eutectic Si particles or silicide phases (e.g., Fe-bearing intermetallics adjacent to Si) may be partially lost when the Si residue is filtered off. For AlSi10Mg, where Si constitutes ~10 wt% of the alloy, this is a non-trivial concern for elements that partition into Si-rich phases.

**ICP-MS Implications:** This approach produces the most ICP-MS-compatible matrix—dilute acid with moderate chloride content. The Agilent 8900 ICP-QQQ can effectively handle chloride-based polyatomic interferences (e.g., ⁴⁰Ar³⁵Cl⁺ on ⁷⁵As) using He collision mode or H₂/O₂ reaction modes (gour2026advancedmodesof pages 1-2, mccurdy2001optimisingicpmsfor pages 2-3). Blanks are low, and no extraneous elements (Na, Li, B) are introduced.

### 2.5 Pressurized/High-Temperature Microwave Digestion Without HF

**Protocol:** The most aggressive HF-free microwave conditions reported use a pressurized-cavity system ramping to 280°C under N₂ pressurization to 3 MPa, with 5 mL HNO₃ + 1 mL H₂O₂ for ~12.5 mg sample. Closed-vessel quartz systems have reached ~210–230°C at up to 7.4 MPa (nakazato2026asimplehydrofluoric pages 2-4).

**Silicon Recovery:** Even at these extreme conditions, "the absence of HF led to incomplete decomposition of the silica" components (nakazato2026asimplehydrofluoric pages 1-2). This was validated by comparison with HF-added digestion that "completely decomposes silica" (nakazato2026asimplehydrofluoric pages 1-2). Refractory phases such as rutile-type TiO₂ also showed limited decomposition without HF, suggesting an inherent limitation of purely oxidative acid systems against covalently bonded oxide/metalloid phases (nakazato2026asimplehydrofluoric pages 2-4, nakazato2026asimplehydrofluoric pages 1-2). For elemental silicon powder, which is even more chemically resistant than amorphous SiO₂, HF-free microwave digestion would be expected to fail entirely for the Si matrix.

**Trace Element Recovery:** Despite incomplete silica decomposition, the HF-free microwave method showed good agreement with HF-added methods for 13 impurity elements (Na, Mg, Al, K, Ca, Ti, Cr, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, Pb) in carbon fiber matrices, and 10 of 13 elements showed lower detection limits than the HF method due to reduced contamination (nakazato2026asimplehydrofluoric pages 1-2). This validates the approach for non-Si analytes in many matrices.

**ICP-MS Implications:** Low blank levels, simple acid matrix (dilute HNO₃), and no introduction of extraneous elements make this approach analytically clean. The residual Si-containing particles must be removed by centrifugation or filtration before analysis (nakazato2026asimplehydrofluoric pages 2-4).

## 3. Special Consideration: Silicon Powder Feedstock

For bulk silicon powder, every route except alkaline fusion fundamentally fails to dissolve the silicon matrix without fluoride chemistry. LiBO₂ fusion at 950°C can decompose silicon powder by oxidizing it to silicate in the molten borate flux. Alternatively, concentrated NaOH or KOH at elevated temperature can dissolve amorphous and partially crystalline silica (with recoveries >84% demonstrated for amorphous SiO₂ nanoparticles at KOH:SiO₂ ≥ 1.2 molar ratio, pH >12) (bossert2019ahydrofluoricacidfree pages 8-9, bossert2019ahydrofluoricacidfree pages 1-2), but crystalline bulk silicon powder may require much harsher conditions. HBF₄ with microwave digestion may work but has not been specifically validated for metallic Si powder.

## 4. Recommended HF-Free Workflows

### 4.1 Route A: When Silicon MUST Be Quantified

**Recommended Method: Lithium Metaborate (LiBO₂) Fusion**

1. **Sample preparation:** Grind/mill alloy chips or powder to <74 µm; dry at 105°C for 2–3 h.
2. **Fusion:** Weigh 0.100–0.250 g sample + 8× mass LiBO₂ (≥99.99% purity) into a Pt crucible. Use a "sandwich" arrangement (flux–sample–flux) to ensure contact.
3. **Heating:** Muffle furnace at 950–1050°C for 2–2.5 h until a clear, homogeneous melt is obtained.
4. **Dissolution:** Pour/transfer cooled melt into 200 mL of 5% v/v HNO₃ (or HCl) in a PTFE beaker; use ultrasonic bath or magnetic stirring until fully dissolved.
5. **Filtration:** Filter through 0.22–0.45 µm membrane if any particles persist.
6. **Dilution for ICP-MS:** Dilute an aliquot further so that TDS < 0.1–0.2% (typically 10–50× additional dilution). Prepare matrix-matched calibration standards containing equivalent LiBO₂ concentrations.
7. **ICP-QQQ measurement:** Use He collision mode for most elements; use MS/MS O₂ mass-shift mode for Si (²⁸Si → ²⁸Si¹⁶O⁺ at m/z 44) to separate from ¹⁴N₂⁺/CO⁺ interferences. Internal standards (e.g., ⁴⁵Sc, ⁷²Ge, ¹⁰³Rh, ¹⁸⁷Re) are essential for drift correction.

**Caveats:** B and Li cannot be determined. Volatile elements (e.g., Hg, some Cd) may be partially lost. Detection limits for trace elements will be degraded by the required high dilution. Consider running a parallel aqua regia digest (Route B) for better trace-element sensitivity.

**Alternative for labs willing to handle HBF₄:** The HBF₄ + HNO₃ microwave route (4 mL HNO₃ + 2 mL 50% HBF₄ per 250 mg sample in a pressurized microwave system) is a promising lower-salt-burden alternative but requires validation with AlSi10Mg certified reference materials before adoption (cuthbertson2024characterizationofpolymer pages 19-20). HBF₄ handling requires HF-equivalent safety protocols.

### 4.2 Route B: When Silicon Does NOT Need to Be Quantified

**Recommended Method: Aqua Regia (HCl + HNO₃) Open or Closed Digestion**

1. **Sample preparation:** Weigh 0.100–0.250 g alloy chips/turnings into a PTFE or PFA digestion vessel.
2. **Acid addition:** Add 5–7 mL aqua regia (HCl:HNO₃, 3:1 v/v, trace-metal grade). Allow initial reaction to subside at room temperature (H₂ evolution from Al).
3. **Heating:** Heat gently (hotplate at ~80–120°C or microwave at moderate temperature, ~160–180°C) until aluminum is fully dissolved. A white/grey Si-rich residue will remain.
4. **Cool and dilute:** Cool, dilute to 25–50 mL with ultrapure water.
5. **Filtration:** Filter through 0.22–0.45 µm syringe filter to remove Si particles and ensure a clear, particle-free solution.
6. **Final matrix:** Adjust to ~2–5% v/v HNO₃ by appropriate dilution.
7. **ICP-QQQ measurement:** Standard He/H₂/O₂ collision-reaction cell modes. Matrix-matched calibration in dilute aqua regia. Internal standards for drift correction.

**Advantages:** Simplest workflow, lowest blanks, most ICP-MS-compatible matrix, fastest turnaround, no exotic reagents. Excellent for quantifying Cu, Fe, Mg, Mn, Zn, Ti, Cr, Ni, Pb, and many other trace metals in the Al matrix.

**Limitation:** Silicon and any trace elements trapped exclusively in Si-rich phases will be lost on filtration. For AlSi10Mg, where most trace metals are dissolved in the Al solid solution rather than sequestered in eutectic Si, this loss is generally minor for typical quality-control analytes.

## 5. Summary

No single HF-free method provides the combination of complete silicon dissolution, low dissolved-solids burden, and broad trace-element compatibility that HF-based digestion offers. The fundamental chemistry of silicon—requiring fluoride to form volatile SiF₄ or soluble SiF₆²⁻—means that every HF-free route involves significant compromises. For university laboratories prioritizing safety, the two-workflow approach (LiBO₂ fusion for total composition including Si; aqua regia for routine trace metals excluding Si) provides the most practical and evidence-supported solution. HBF₄ represents an intermediate option with reduced acute hazard compared to free HF but still requires fluoride-aware safety protocols and alloy-specific method validation.
