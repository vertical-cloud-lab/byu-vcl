Question: What compact pH sensors or probes are suitable for automated, robotic
self-driving laboratory (SDL) use where the probe must fit inside a standard 20 mL
scintillation vial (outer diameter under ~12 mm preferred, and under ~25 mm at most)?

Specifically:
1. Which small-form-factor pH probes can be stored dry (or semi-dry) rather than
   requiring continuous immersion in storage solution (e.g., ISFET / solid-state pH
   sensors such as Sentron, Endress+Hauser Memosens CPS47/77, Hamilton, Vernier
   Tris-compatible ISFET, or others)? What are the tradeoffs (drift, calibration
   frequency, cost, lifetime)?
2. Are there combined ionic conductivity + pH + temperature multiparameter probes in
   a small form factor suitable for lab automation, and what are representative
   products or published examples?
3. What approaches have self-driving labs / automated chemistry platforms published
   for maintaining glass pH electrodes between measurements (automated wetting
   stations, storage vials with caps, press-fit capping, rehydration protocols)?

Please cite specific products, papers, and quantitative specs (diameter, pH range,
accuracy, storage requirements, approximate cost) where possible.

## Compact pH Sensors for Self-Driving Laboratory Use in Standard 20 mL Scintillation Vials

### 1. Small-Form-Factor pH Probes Suitable for Dry or Semi-Dry Storage

**ISFET (Ion-Sensitive Field Effect Transistor) sensors** represent the most promising class of compact pH probes for automated SDL workflows requiring dry storage. The key advantage of ISFET sensors over conventional glass pH electrodes is their smaller outer diameter—Sentron Europe BV produces ISFET probes with an outer diameter of only 8 mm, compared to the typical 12 mm of glass pH electrodes (oelssner2005encapsulationofisfet pages 5-6). This 8 mm form factor comfortably fits inside a standard 20 mL scintillation vial (inner diameter ~25 mm). Sentron offers several probe configurations: the ConeFET for thick or dirty samples up to 105 °C, the SurFET for direct surface measurements, and the LanceFET with a pointed tip for penetrating dense or viscous materials (oelssner2005encapsulationofisfet pages 5-6). These sensors use manual encapsulation with hot-cured epoxy and are designed for both laboratory and field applications (oelssner2005encapsulationofisfet pages 5-6).

A critical advantage of ISFETs for SDL automation is their ability to be stored dry over extended periods, which is explicitly noted as making them "particularly well-suited" for single-use and automated systems (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 7-9). In contrast, conventional glass pH electrodes require continuous wet storage in KCl solution to maintain their hydrated gel layer and cannot tolerate prolonged drying without performance degradation (oelssner2005encapsulationofisfet pages 1-2, mokhtarifar2022itoasthe pages 20-24).

**Tradeoffs for ISFET sensors include:**

- **Drift:** ISFET drift is a well-documented non-ideality. An intelligent ISFET system with dual temperature and drift compensation achieved maximum accuracy errors of 0.02 pH at 23 °C, with time drift measured at 0.003 pH/hour at that temperature (chen2008anintelligentisfet pages 1-2). Advanced gate oxide materials such as TbTa₍ₓ₎O₍ᵧ₎ achieve drift rates as low as 0.12 mV/h with hysteresis of only 1 mV (sinha2022acomprehensivereview pages 11-11). However, drift rates increase with both pH value and temperature and can vary between nominally identical devices (vilouras2021ultrathinandflexible pages 40-42, chen2008anintelligentisfet pages 1-2). The drift mechanism involves slow ion diffusion into the gate insulator, producing monotonic temporal changes that can persist for weeks after fabrication (vilouras2021ultrathinandflexible pages 40-42).

- **Sensitivity:** Modern ISFET sensors using Ta₂O₅ and Al₂O₃ gate materials achieve near-Nernstian pH response. The Sentron 1090 with Al₂O₃ gate demonstrates 53.23 mV/pH sensitivity (sinha2022acomprehensivereview pages 10-11). Other materials such as HoTiO₃ reach 59.5 mV/pH and TiO₂ achieves 58.21 mV/pH (sinha2022acomprehensivereview pages 11-11).

- **Calibration frequency:** Due to drift, ISFET sensors typically require more frequent calibration than well-maintained glass electrodes, particularly in the first hours after immersion. Machine learning and hardware compensation approaches have been developed to counteract this drift (chen2008anintelligentisfet pages 1-2).

- **Encapsulation and lifetime:** The encapsulation resistance of ISFET probes decreases by up to three orders of magnitude over 5 months in buffer solution due to moisture absorption; however, this resistance recovers to original high values during dry storage, confirming the mechanism is moisture absorption rather than permanent structural degradation (oelssner2005encapsulationofisfet pages 9-10). Encapsulation quality is critical—elastomer gasket-based encapsulation using EPDM seals provides superior long-term performance compared to epoxy-only encapsulation (oelssner2005encapsulationofisfet pages 6-7).

- **Cost:** Sentron-style ISFET probes typically cost several hundred to over one thousand USD depending on configuration and readout electronics.

**Optical pH sensors** (e.g., PreSens sensor spots) offer an alternative strategy that eliminates the need for a dipping probe entirely. These fluorescence-based sensor spots can be attached to the inside of transparent vessels and read through the wall using external optical hardware. They achieve drift rates below 0.01 pH per day in some cultivation applications (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 5-6) and can be miniaturized to milliliter-scale or below (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 6-7, demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 9-10). Dynamic ranges exceeding 5.5 pH units have been reported (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 4-5). However, their long-term stability remains a key limitation for extended deployments, and they require optical access to the vessel (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 9-10).

### 2. Combined Multiparameter Probes (pH + Conductivity + Temperature)

Truly integrated, compact multiparameter probes combining pH, ionic conductivity, and temperature in a single body with an outer diameter under 12 mm remain scarce in the commercial market. However, several research-stage and niche solutions have been described:

**Solid-state multi-parametric device (Daoudi et al., 2017):** An innovative electrochemical probe was developed combining monocrystalline antimony electrodes for pH measurement, AgCl/Ag-based reference electrodes, platinum/gold/glassy-carbon electrodes for redox potential, and galvanostatic electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (GEIS) for conductivity measurement. The probe holder is fabricated from PVDF and can accommodate up to 20 electrodes in a 210 cm³ internal volume (daoudi2017amultiparametricdevice pages 5-8). This device demonstrated good reproducibility and accuracy—conductivity of 0.78 ± 0.02 S·m⁻¹ versus conventional meters—and only 4 mV of reference potential drift over 1200 hours (daoudi2017amultiparametricdevice pages 17-20, daoudi2017amultiparametricdevice pages 20-21). It was validated over 16 months of testing (daoudi2017amultiparametricdevice pages 20-21). While designed for nuclear waste repository monitoring rather than benchtop SDL use, the all-solid-state electrode approach could be miniaturized.

**Wireless RFID-based sensors:** Demuth et al. (2016) described passive RFID tags that enable simultaneous contactless measurement of conductivity, pH, and temperature. These tags are attached to single-use cultivation systems and operate without batteries; an external reader generates an electric field that modulates the RFID antenna impedance based on dielectric properties of the process medium (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 7-9). Key advantages include battery-free operation, contactless measurement, low cost, and digital identification of the single-use component (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 7-9). This approach is particularly attractive for disposable SDL vessels.

**ISFET-based multimodal sensors:** Futagawa et al. (2012) reported a miniature integrated multimodal sensor combining ISFET-based pH sensing with electrical conductivity and temperature measurement on a single chip platform, designed for precision agriculture applications. Similarly, Mross et al. (2016) developed an integrated multi-sensor system for parallel in-situ monitoring of cell nutrients, metabolites, cell density, and pH in biotechnological processes.

No commercially available, off-the-shelf probe combining all three parameters in a single shaft under 12 mm OD was identified in the literature. For SDL use, the practical recommendation is to use separate compact sensors (e.g., an 8 mm ISFET pH probe + a miniature conductivity cell + a thermocouple), or to adopt the RFID/optical contactless approach when vessel redesign is feasible.

**Strategy A: Permanent insertion through vessel caps.** The Multiple Microfermentor Battery (Frachon et al., 2006) employs a Teflon headplate with three screw adaptors for inserting pH, DO, and temperature probes permanently into each 80 mL glass reactor vessel (frachon2006multiplemicrofermentorbattery pages 1-1, frachon2006multiplemicrofermentorbattery pages 1-3). This approach keeps probes continuously wetted and avoids repeated robotic pick-and-place operations, though it limits throughput to one probe per reactor.

**Strategy B: Modular robotic nozzle/carousel systems.** The Electrolab platform (Oh et al., 2023) integrates pH measurement as a probe-like attachment within a modular nozzle system, supporting electrode maintenance through automated gas sparging and solvent cleaning of cells between measurements (oh2023theelectrolaban pages 8-9, oh2023theelectrolaban pages 3-4). The pHBot system described within the FLAB framework (Jose et al., 2024) uses a pH probe mounted on a 3-axis robotic gantry with a rotating carousel, enabling automated dipping into sample vials for iterative pH measurement and adjustment with active machine learning control (jose2024auniversalframework pages 6-10).

**Strategy C: Avoid glass electrodes entirely.** Several platforms circumvent the electrode maintenance problem by using alternative sensing modalities. The BrickSDLab platform (Böser & Bräse, 2025) uses a color/light sensor combined with universal pH indicator dyes, guiding autonomous acid/base addition based on color changes without any electrode to maintain (boser2025bricksdlabalowcost pages 23-28). Optical pH sensor spots (PreSens and similar) attached to vessel walls provide contactless readout without probe insertion or storage requirements (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 6-7, demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 5-6). ISFET sensors, with their inherent dry-storage tolerance, largely eliminate the wet-maintenance problem (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 7-9, oelssner2005encapsulationofisfet pages 9-10).

**Strategy D: Automated capping and station-based storage.** Chemspeed automated synthesis platforms feature automated door operation and screw-capping capability for vials, which in principle supports workflows where a pH electrode could be parked in a storage vial containing KCl solution between measurements. While explicit publications describing dedicated automated wetting stations for pH electrodes in SDLs are sparse, the hardware capability for such an approach exists in platforms like Chemspeed (frachon2006multiplemicrofermentorbattery pages 3-4).

**Strategy E: 3D-printed robotic analytical systems.** The NuPE-Robot (Assunção et al., 2025) and similar 3D-printed liquid handling robots incorporate automated cleaning routines and modular detection cells for potentiometric (including pH) and colorimetric measurements. These systems use automated liquid-handling cleaning protocols between measurements and can exchange modular detection cells to manage electrode condition (assuncao20253dprintedliquidhandling pages 2-4, assuncao20253dprintedliquidhandling pages 10-11).

### Summary and Recommendations

For SDL applications requiring pH measurement inside standard 20 mL scintillation vials, ISFET sensors—particularly the Sentron-style probes at 8 mm OD—offer the best combination of compact form factor and dry-storage tolerance (oelssner2005encapsulationofisfet pages 5-6, demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 7-9). The main tradeoff is higher drift relative to glass electrodes, necessitating more frequent calibration (chen2008anintelligentisfet pages 1-2, vilouras2021ultrathinandflexible pages 40-42). For applications where the vessel can be redesigned, optical pH sensor spots or RFID-based contactless sensors provide maintenance-free alternatives (demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 5-6, demuth2016novelprobesfor pages 7-9). When glass electrodes are required for accuracy, the most practical automated approaches are permanent insertion through vessel caps, robotic dipping with automated rinse stations, or parking the probe in a KCl-filled storage vial between measurement cycles (frachon2006multiplemicrofermentorbattery pages 1-1, oh2023theelectrolaban pages 8-9). The field would benefit from purpose-built automated wetting/storage station designs tailored to SDL workflows, as this remains a gap in the published literature.
