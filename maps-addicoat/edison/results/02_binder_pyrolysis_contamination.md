Question: Critically assess the effect of the polymeric binder in powder-sheet metal additive manufacturing (MAPS) on the final part chemistry. How much carbon, oxygen, and nitrogen pickup results from binder pyrolysis during laser fusion, how is residual char/ash quantified, and how does this interstitial contamination affect mechanical properties, corrosion, and weldability compared with gas-atomized powder feedstock? Is binder burn-off complete and reproducible, and what is the evidence?

## Critical Assessment of Polymeric Binder Effects on Final Part Chemistry in Powder-Sheet Metal Additive Manufacturing (MAPS)

### 1. Overview of the MAPS Process and Binder Role

Metal Additive manufacturing using Powder Sheets (MAPS) is a modified powder bed fusion (PBF) technique in which flexible composite sheets—comprising metal powder bound with a polymeric binder—replace loose powder as the feedstock material. The sheets are positioned over the build plate using rollers, and a laser selectively melts the metal in the prescribed pattern; the polymer degrades during the laser-melting process and does not participate in the weld (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 1-2). In the foundational demonstration by Lupoi et al. (2022), sheets were fabricated via solvent casting of spherical 304 stainless steel powder (15–45 µm, gas-atomized from Carpenter Additive) mixed with a polycaprolactone (PCL) solution, yielding roughly A4-sized sheets approximately 80–100 µm thick (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 1-2). This approach offers advantages in safety (powder is not airborne), rapid material changeover (~10 min vs. ~3 h for loose powder), and multi-material capability (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4).

### 2. Carbon, Oxygen, and Nitrogen Pickup from Binder Pyrolysis

**Carbon pickup in MAPS:** Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) to 800 °C demonstrated that the PCL binder mass fully stabilizes by approximately 500 °C, regardless of the initial polymer weight percentage in the sheet, indicating total thermal degradation well below the ~1400 °C melting temperature of 304 SS (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). Energy-dispersive X-ray (EDX) mapping of MAPS-printed 304 SS cross-sections showed no significant carbon signal pickup within the iron matrix; the only strong carbon signals originated from the conductive resin used for metallographic mounting (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). On this basis, the authors concluded that "the polymer binder will completely decompose and does not contaminate the final print when melt-pool conditions are reached" (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3).

**Oxygen and nitrogen pickup in MAPS:** The available MAPS literature does not report quantitative oxygen or nitrogen analyses (e.g., by inert gas fusion per ASTM E1019) on as-printed MAPS parts. The TGA data show slight mass increases above 500 °C attributed to oxide formation on the steel powder, but this is not distinguished from atmospheric oxygen pickup during TGA itself (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). This constitutes a significant gap: no published MAPS study to date has provided element-specific interstitial chemistry (C, O, N) by combustion or inert gas fusion analysis on the printed metal, nor has a direct comparison with the starting gas-atomized powder feedstock composition been reported. Several key MAPS papers that may contain such data—including Zhang et al. (2024, *Materials & Design*), Sasnauskas et al. (2024, *CIRP Annals*; 2025, *Journal of Manufacturing Processes*; 2025, *Additive Manufacturing*), and Zhang et al. (2025, *Additive Manufacturing Frontiers*)—were identified but were not obtainable through the literature retrieval system.

**Context from analogous binder-based AM processes:** In binder jet additive manufacturing (BJAM) of H13 tool steel, where the binder is deposited as a liquid and must be thermally removed before sintering, commercial binders (e.g., ExOne Aquafuse) leave behind 0.028–0.07 wt% residual carbon above baseline, with combustion analysis values of 0.363–0.557 wt% total carbon in sintered parts depending on saturation level and sintering temperature (gilmer2024predictivebinderjet pages 11-15, gilmer2024predictivebinderjet pages 3-6). In extrusion-based AM of Ti-6Al-4V using an EVA-containing binder, carbon from the polymer backbone dissolves into titanium above 500 °C, with ≥0.1% C causing severe embrittlement (krinitcyn2023extrusionbasedadditivemanufacturing pages 9-12, krinitcyn2023extrusionbasedadditivemanufacturing pages 12-14). Titanium alloys are also noted for their high affinity for oxygen and nitrogen, which form oxides, nitrides, and carbides that increase strength but drastically reduce elongation (eickhoff2024developmentofflexible pages 2-4, jacob2024areviewof pages 21-23).

### 3. Quantification of Residual Char and Ash

Several complementary analytical techniques have been employed to assess the completeness of binder removal:

- **Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and TGA-MS:** Used to track polymer mass loss as a function of temperature and to identify gaseous decomposition products. In MAPS, TGA showed that PCL fully decomposes by ~500 °C (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). In BJAM, TGA-MS of PVP-VAc copolymers identified O₂ and CO₂ as major degradation products, confirming that oxygen released from the vinyl acetate monomer reacts with carbon to produce volatile CO₂ rather than leaving solid char (gilmer2024predictivebinderjet pages 11-15).

- **Combustion infrared detection (ASTM E1019-18):** The standard quantitative method for measuring residual carbon in sintered metal parts. In H13 BJAM, this method revealed carbon contents ranging from 0.013 wt% (PVP-VAc binder, 85% saturation, 1400 °C sintering) to 0.557 wt% (commercial binder, 105% saturation, 1360 °C sintering) (gilmer2024predictivebinderjet pages 11-15). The standard deviation for these measurements was reported as 0.00122% (gilmer2024predictivebinderjet pages 11-15).

- **EDX elemental mapping:** Provides spatial information on carbon distribution. In MAPS 304 SS, EDX confirmed no significant carbon enrichment in the Fe matrix relative to the surrounding mounting resin (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). However, EDX is semi-quantitative and has limited sensitivity for light elements, so it cannot substitute for combustion analysis.

- **X-ray computed tomography (microCT):** Used to assess porosity and density as indirect indicators of successful processing. MAPS-printed 304 SS achieved 99.98% relative density by microCT, with only small dispersed pores and no large voids (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4).

The MAPS samples exhibited a finer average grain size (29.2 µm vs. 48.7 µm for PBF), which largely explains the higher measured microhardness (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4). The authors attributed this difference primarily to the smaller MAPS sample volume (~25 mm³ vs. ~1000 mm³), which results in different thermal histories during manufacturing, rather than to compositional changes from the binder (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4). Neither sample exhibited a preferred crystallographic texture, and both showed minor anisotropy in hardness (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 4-4). The conclusion was that "the polymer fully decomposes and does not affect the key characteristics of the fusion process" (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4).

By analogy in other binder-based AM processes, the consequences of incomplete binder removal can be severe. In BJAM of H13 tool steel, residual carbon shifts the supersolidus liquid phase sintering temperature, causing distortion from 1–5% (with clean PVP-VAc binder) up to major geometric failure (with commercial binders), and alters hot working and wear-resistant properties (gilmer2024predictivebinderjet pages 3-6, gilmer2024predictivebinderjet pages 15-18, gilmer2024predictivebinderjet pages 1-3). In extrusion-based AM of Ti-6Al-4V with EVA binder, carbon dissolution produced extremely high hardness (806 ± 23 HV) but catastrophically low bending strength (182 ± 16 MPa) with entirely brittle fracture surfaces, precluding use in structural or implant applications (krinitcyn2023extrusionbasedadditivemanufacturing pages 9-12, krinitcyn2023extrusionbasedadditivemanufacturing pages 12-14).

### 5. Corrosion and Weldability

**Corrosion:** No published MAPS-specific corrosion data were identified in the available literature. From analogous metal FFF processes, 316L stainless steel and 17-4PH parts showed corrosion behavior comparable to or better than metal injection molded (MIM) equivalents, with 17-4PH FFF samples exhibiting improved tribocorrosion resistance attributed to their specific microstructure (jacob2024areviewof pages 21-23). In principle, elevated interstitial carbon in austenitic stainless steels promotes sensitization (chromium carbide precipitation at grain boundaries), which degrades intergranular corrosion resistance. However, this concern has not been experimentally validated or refuted for MAPS.

**Weldability:** No published studies were found examining the weldability of MAPS-printed components. Elevated carbon, oxygen, or nitrogen would be expected to degrade weldability through increased susceptibility to hot cracking, porosity, and embrittlement in heat-affected zones, but this remains entirely uncharacterized for the MAPS process.

### 6. Is Binder Burn-Off Complete and Reproducible? Assessment of the Evidence

The evidence for complete binder burn-off in MAPS rests on three observations from the initial proof-of-concept study (Lupoi et al. 2022): (i) TGA showing total PCL degradation by ~500 °C, well below the steel melting point (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3); (ii) EDX mapping showing no significant carbon pickup in printed parts (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3); and (iii) microhardness, density, and crystallographic texture comparable to conventional PBF (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 4-4).

**Critical assessment of completeness:** While these data are encouraging, the evidence has notable limitations. First, TGA characterizes isolated polymer decomposition under controlled heating, not the rapid, non-equilibrium thermal conditions of laser melting where heating rates reach thousands of degrees per second. The pyrolysis pathway in a melt pool may differ substantially from slow TGA heating. Second, EDX is semi-quantitative for light elements like carbon, and typical EDX detection limits (~0.1–1 wt%) may not capture the ~0.01–0.05 wt% levels of residual carbon that are significant for carbon-sensitive alloys. Third, the MAPS samples in the foundational study were limited to ~50 layers and ~25 mm³ total volume, with manual sheet placement, making it difficult to assess process reproducibility at industrially relevant scales.

**Reproducibility:** No systematic studies of binder burn-off reproducibility (e.g., batch-to-batch variation in residual carbon, effects of sheet thickness variation, or effects of different polymer loading levels on interstitial chemistry) have been published for MAPS to date. The BJAM literature provides much more rigorous quantification—for example, Gilmer et al. (2024) systematically varied binder saturation (85%, 100%, 105%) and sintering temperature (1360, 1380, 1400 °C) and quantified residual carbon by combustion analysis with a reported standard deviation of 0.00122%, demonstrating both the analytical methodology and the sensitivity of residual carbon to processing parameters (gilmer2024predictivebinderjet pages 11-15). This level of systematic characterization has not yet been applied to MAPS.

### 7. Summary and Identified Gaps

The MAPS process benefits from a fundamental thermodynamic advantage over sinter-based binder processes: because the laser rapidly heats material to temperatures far exceeding the polymer decomposition temperature, the binder should fully volatilize before or during melt-pool formation. Preliminary evidence from TGA and EDX supports this assertion for PCL-bonded 304 SS sheets (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). However, several critical gaps remain:

1. **No quantitative interstitial chemistry (C, O, N by combustion/inert gas fusion)** has been published for MAPS-printed parts, nor has a direct comparison with the starting gas-atomized powder composition been reported.
2. **No corrosion or weldability data** exist for MAPS parts.
3. **No systematic reproducibility studies** of binder burn-off completeness across varying sheet compositions, thicknesses, polymer loadings, or alloy systems have been published.
4. **The effect of polymer content on carbon pickup** is being actively investigated (e.g., Sasnauskas et al. 2024, 2025; Zhang et al. 2025), but these recent papers were not obtainable for this review.
5. **Scale-up validation** beyond small proof-of-concept samples is needed to confirm that binder burn-off remains complete under automated, high-throughput conditions.

In conclusion, while the available MAPS literature presents a thermodynamically plausible case that the polymeric binder fully decomposes during laser fusion without contaminating final parts, the quantitative analytical evidence is insufficient to definitively confirm this for all alloy systems, processing conditions, and binder compositions. The broader binder-based AM literature (BJAM, MEX) demonstrates clearly that incomplete binder removal can leave 0.01–0.6 wt% residual carbon, causing property-altering or property-destroying effects depending on alloy sensitivity. Rigorous interstitial chemistry characterization, corrosion testing, and weldability evaluation of MAPS parts remain urgent research needs before the technology can be qualified for structural or safety-critical applications.
