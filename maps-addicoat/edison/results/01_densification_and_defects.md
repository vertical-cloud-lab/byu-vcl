Question: Critically evaluate the evidence that metal additive manufacturing using powder sheets (MAPS), in which metal powder is held in a polymer binder sheet and laser-fused, achieves relative density, porosity, and defect populations comparable to conventional laser powder bed fusion (LPBF). What independent or peer-reviewed evidence supports the claimed >99% density, and what porosity, lack-of-fusion, or delamination defects are reported between stacked sheets? Where is the evidence weak, single-source, or not independently reproduced?

## Critical Evaluation of MAPS Density, Porosity, and Defect Evidence Relative to Conventional LPBF

### 1. Technology Overview

Metal Additive Manufacturing using Powder Sheets (MAPS) is a modified powder bed fusion (PBF) process introduced by Lupoi and colleagues at Trinity College Dublin (TCD) in 2022. In MAPS, spherical metal powder (e.g., 15–45 µm 304 stainless steel) is combined with a polycaprolactone (PCL) polymer binder via solvent casting to form flexible composite sheets approximately 80–100 µm thick (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 1-2). These sheets are positioned over a build plate in lieu of loose powder recoating, and a laser scans each layer to melt the metal powder while the polymer binder fully decomposes below 500 °C—well below the ~1400 °C melting point of 304 SS—without contaminating the final part (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). The process is intended to eliminate loose powder handling hazards, simplify material changeover (approximately 10 minutes versus up to 3 hours for conventional PBF), and enable multi-material printing (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 1-2).

### 2. Reported Density and Porosity Values

The foundational and sole fully accessible quantitative density study is Lupoi et al. (2022), published in *CIRP Annals*. This study reported that a 50-layer MAPS sample of 304 SS achieved a relative density of 99.98% (total porosity of 0.02%) as measured by micro-CT with a 1.5 µm voxel size (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). The conventional PBF control sample, measured by Archimedes testing, achieved 99.93% relative density (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). The authors concluded that "MAPS is capable of achieving a similar density to that which is obtainable from traditional PBF processing" and noted that "porosity is not clearly visible in the cross-section" (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4).

From the bibliographic record, subsequent papers from the same group have extended MAPS to multiple alloys (austenitic steel, AlSi10Mg, CoCrNiFeMn high-entropy alloy, bimetallic composites), but the full-text density and porosity data from these studies were not accessible for independent verification in this review. Notably, Zhang et al. (2026) in *Journal of Manufacturing Processes* claims "defect-free 3D bimetallic composites" in its title, but the quantitative evidence underlying this claim could not be examined.

### 3. Defects Reported Between Stacked Sheets

A critical gap in the accessible evidence is the absence of systematic characterization of defects specific to MAPS inter-layer interfaces. The foundational study does not explicitly address lack-of-fusion defects, delamination at sheet-to-sheet boundaries, or kissing bond formation (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). These defect types are well documented as major failure modes in conventional sheet lamination processes (FSAM, UAM), where interfacial porosity, microvoids, interfacial segregation, and kissing bonds are commonly observed (xu2024areviewof pages 25-27). The MAPS process, while fundamentally different from solid-sheet lamination because the powder is melted rather than bonded in solid state, still introduces material layer-by-layer from discrete sheets, making the inter-sheet interface a natural candidate for defect accumulation—a question not systematically addressed in the accessible literature.

The only defect-related observations in the accessible study are the uneven surfaces and side walls attributed to the large beam diameter (170 µm) and manual sheet placement imprecision (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4). Guo et al. (2025) published in *Optics & Laser Technology* explicitly addresses "mass transfer and defect formation during AlSi10Mg additive manufacturing using powder sheets" through in-situ monitoring and metallographic observations, but this study (a collaboration between Luleå University of Technology and TCD) was not accessible for full-text analysis.

### 4. Methodological Weaknesses in the Density Comparison

Several methodological issues weaken the strength of the >99% density claim:

- **Measurement method mismatch**: The MAPS sample density was measured by micro-CT while the PBF control was measured by Archimedes testing. These techniques have different sensitivities to closed versus open porosity and surface roughness, making a direct numerical comparison unreliable (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3).

- **Sample volume disparity**: The MAPS sample was approximately 25 mm³ (50 layers, ~1 mm height), whereas the PBF control was approximately 1000 mm³—a 40-fold difference. The MAPS sample was too small for reliable Archimedes testing (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). Such a small coupon may miss build-height-dependent or low-frequency defect populations that manifest in larger builds.

- **Manual process**: Sheets were manually placed through glove ports on a Realizer SLM-50 machine from which the recoater had been removed. This introduces operator-dependent variability and limits scalability. The authors acknowledged that "successful implementation of MAPS will require the design of a bespoke printer that is fully automated" (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4).

- **No mechanical validation beyond hardness**: The comparison included microhardness (showing MAPS was harder, likely due to finer grains of 29.2 µm versus 48.7 µm in PBF), but no tensile, fatigue, or fracture toughness data were reported (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4). High density alone does not guarantee structural equivalence if interface-specific defects degrade performance under load.

### 5. Independence and Reproducibility of Evidence

This is perhaps the most significant concern. The entire MAPS literature originates from a single core research network centered at Trinity College Dublin under Rocco Lupoi. The search identified approximately 15–20 publications (2022–2026) spanning *CIRP Annals*, *Materials & Design*, *Virtual and Physical Prototyping*, *Additive Manufacturing Letters*, *CIRP Journal of Manufacturing Science and Technology*, *Composites Part B*, *Journal of Laser Applications*, *Optics & Laser Technology*, *Journal of Manufacturing Processes*, *Additive Manufacturing*, *Additive Manufacturing Frontiers*, *Results in Engineering*, and *Composites Part A*. While several involve collaborating institutions—Luleå University of Technology (Kaplan, Volpp, Guo), Politecnico di Milano (Casati, Marola), Imperial College London (Pham, Hooper), University of Manchester (Mirihanage), and Politecnico di Torino (Manfredi)—all include TCD authors (Lupoi, Zhang, and/or other TCD co-authors) as collaborators, meaning none constitutes a fully independent replication (murugesan2026enablingsustainablepowder pages 2-3, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 4-4).

The Manfredi et al. (2025) study from Politecnico di Torino on reusing AlSi10Mg powder in MAPS is the closest to a semi-independent evaluation, reporting "consistent, fine microstructure, near-nominal chemical composition, and superior hardness compared with alternative quick-solidification techniques" (murugesan2026enablingsustainablepowder pages 2-3). However, it examines feasibility of powder reuse rather than independently benchmarking the >99% density claim, and it still includes TCD-network authors.

### 6. Summary Assessment

**What the evidence supports**: The foundational Lupoi et al. (2022) study credibly demonstrates that laser melting of metal powder embedded in polymer binder sheets can produce metallurgically sound 304 SS material with very low porosity as measured by micro-CT, and that the polycaprolactone binder fully decomposes without contaminating the final print (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3). The concept is technically plausible and the published proof-of-concept results are encouraging. The rapid growth of the publication record (>15 papers in 4 years) across reputable journals indicates active and expanding research.

**Where the evidence is weak or single-source**:
1. The >99% density claim rests on a single accessible study using a very small sample (25 mm³, 50 layers), measured by a different technique than the PBF control, with no statistical replication reported (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3).
2. No systematic characterization of inter-sheet interface defects (lack-of-fusion, delamination, kissing bonds) has been published in the accessible literature, despite these being the most MAPS-specific concern (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4).
3. All publications originate from a single research network; no independent laboratory has published a stand-alone verification of MAPS density or defect claims (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 2-3, lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 4-4).
4. No mechanical property data beyond hardness is accessible for MAPS versus LPBF comparison (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4).
5. Scalability to production-relevant build volumes and automation remains undemonstrated in the accessible literature, though the authors explicitly acknowledge this limitation (lupoi2022metaladditivemanufacturing pages 3-4).

The MAPS technology represents a conceptually innovative approach with promising early-stage results, but the evidence for density equivalence with conventional LPBF remains preliminary, single-source, and methodologically limited. Independent replication by groups without collaborative ties to the originating laboratory, systematic interface defect characterization, and mechanical property benchmarking on production-scale builds are essential before the claim of LPBF-comparable quality can be considered robustly established.
