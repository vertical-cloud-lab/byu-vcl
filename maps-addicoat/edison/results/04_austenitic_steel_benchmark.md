Question: Critically review published benchmarking of MAPS / powder-sheet additive manufacturing using austenitic stainless steel (e.g. 316L) against conventional LPBF. How do yield strength, ductility, fatigue, anisotropy, and defect content compare, what sample sizes and statistics were used, and are the comparisons fair (same alloy, build parameters, post-processing)? Identify methodological weaknesses, missing fatigue/anisotropy data, and over-generalized conclusions.

## Critical Review of Published Benchmarking of MAPS (Powder-Sheet Additive Manufacturing) Against Conventional LPBF for Austenitic Stainless Steel (316L)

### 1. Introduction and Scope of Available Literature

Metal Additive Manufacturing using Powder Sheets (MAPS) is a relatively new process developed primarily by Lupoi and colleagues at Trinity College Dublin. In MAPS, metallic powder is bound into thin sheets using a polymeric binder, and these sheets are then consolidated layer-by-layer via laser processing—offering potential advantages in multi-material printing and powder handling relative to conventional laser powder bed fusion (LPBF). The published MAPS literature is still small and concentrated within a single research group, with the following key publications identified:

- Zhang, Marola, McConnell et al., "Demonstration and benchmarking of a novel powder sheet additive manufacturing approach with austenitic steel," *Materials & Design* 245:113301 (2024) — the core benchmarking paper;
- Abbott, Di Sturco, Zhang et al., "Metal additive manufacturing using powder sheets (MAPS): technology review," *IOP Conf. Ser.: Mater. Sci. Eng.* 1332:012001 (2025);
- Zhang, Abbott, Sasnauskas et al., "Development of a novel powder sheets printing process towards the next generation of additive manufacturing: the role of laser defocusing," *Virtual and Physical Prototyping* (2024);
- Zhang, Sasnauskas, Coban et al., "Powder sheets additive manufacturing: principles and capabilities for multi-material printing," *Additive Manufacturing Letters* 8:100187 (2024);
- Zhang, Lu, Coban et al., "Powder sheet additive manufacturing of multi-material structures," *Composites Part B* (2024);
- Zhang, Cai, Coban et al., "The effects of polymeric binder on microstructural characteristics of powder sheet additive manufacturing," *Additive Manufacturing Frontiers* (2025).

Critically, none of these primary MAPS publications were accessible for full-text examination in this review, meaning our analysis must rely on what can be inferred from metadata, titles, abstracts, and secondary references—most notably a discussion by Murugesan et al. (2026), which cites Manfredi et al.'s work using MAPS to consolidate recycled AlSi10Mg powder, reporting fine microstructure, near-nominal composition, and superior hardness in the consolidated material (murugesan2026enablingsustainablepowder pages 2-3). ### 2. Yield Strength and Ductility: What Is Known

For conventional LPBF of 316L stainless steel, the literature establishes a broad but well-characterized property envelope. Wang et al. (2018), in a landmark *Nature Materials* study, demonstrated that L-PBF 316L achieves superior yield strength and tensile ductility relative to conventional 316L by exploiting hierarchically heterogeneous microstructures—solidification-enabled cellular structures, low-angle grain boundaries, and dislocation networks—spanning nearly six orders of magnitude in length scale (wang2018additivelymanufacturedhierarchical pages 1-5, wang2018additivelymanufacturedhierarchical pages 8-11). Sarafan et al. (2022) reported that optimized hybrid LPBF 316L (240–380 W) achieves YS of 551–559 MPa, UTS of 714–715 MPa, and elongation of 52.5–56.9%, well exceeding ASTM F3184-16 minima (YS ≥ 205 MPa, UTS ≥ 515 MPa, EL ≥ 30%) (sarafan2022benchmarkingof316l pages 20-21, sarafan2022benchmarkingof316l pages 18-20). Chepkoech et al. (2024) reported YS of 538–551 MPa, UTS of 606–620 MPa, and elongation of 70–74% for well-processed LPBF 316L (chepkoech2024investigationofmicrostructures pages 8-11, chepkoech2024investigationofmicrostructures pages 1-2).

The MAPS benchmarking paper by Zhang et al. (2024, *Materials & Design*) is titled "Demonstration and benchmarking of a novel powder sheet additive manufacturing approach with austenitic steel," strongly implying it presents tensile property comparisons against LPBF. However, without access to the full text, the specific yield strength, UTS, and elongation values achieved by MAPS cannot be independently verified or critically assessed. This is a significant limitation: **the entire MAPS-vs-LPBF tensile benchmarking dataset for 316L currently rests on a single publication from a single group.**

### 3. Fatigue and Anisotropy: Critical Gaps

Fatigue performance is one of the most critical properties for structural applications of AM metals, and it is strongly sensitive to surface roughness, internal porosity, residual stresses, and microstructural texture—all of which differ fundamentally between MAPS (with its polymeric binder residues and sheet-based consolidation) and conventional LPBF. For LPBF 316L, there is a substantial body of fatigue literature demonstrating the importance of build direction, surface finish, and defect population (debroy2018additivemanufacturingof pages 109-110, sarafan2022benchmarkingof316l pages 26-27). No MAPS-specific fatigue data for 316L was identified in any accessible publication or even referenced in secondary sources. This represents a **major omission** in the current MAPS benchmarking record.

Similarly, anisotropy in LPBF 316L is well documented. Fedorenko et al. (2021) showed that horizontal specimens achieve YS of ~616 MPa and E of ~196–198 GPa, while vertical specimens yield only ~528 MPa and ~159 GPa, with elongation trends reversed (30.8% horizontal vs. 39.0% vertical) (fedorenko2021anisotropyofmechanical pages 3-6, fedorenko2021anisotropyofmechanical pages 1-2). Weaver and Rosenthal (2021) attributed this primarily to crystallographic texture rather than grain morphology (weaver2021understandinganisotropictensile pages 10-12, weaver2021understandinganisotropictensile pages 8-10). DebRoy et al. (2018) noted that the extent and direction of anisotropy in austenitic steels depend on both process type and build geometry (debroy2018additivemanufacturingof pages 61-63). **No MAPS anisotropy data has been identified in any accessible source.** Given that MAPS introduces an additional structural hierarchy (inter-sheet interfaces, binder residues, potentially different thermal histories per layer), anisotropy could be more severe or qualitatively different from LPBF, making this a critical gap.

### 4. Defect Content: The Comparability Problem

Porosity in LPBF 316L is well-characterized and classifiable into three regimes: lack-of-fusion (LOF) pores at low energy density, gas/blowhole pores at intermediate conditions, and keyhole pores at excessive energy density (terris2019optimizationandcomparison pages 1-2, terris2019optimizationandcomparison pages 2-3). Optimized LPBF routinely achieves >99.5% relative density with only small, isolated spherical pores (sarafan2022benchmarkingof316l pages 20-21, chepkoech2024investigationofmicrostructures pages 5-8). The defect population in MAPS is expected to be qualitatively different: binder burnout may leave residual carbon or voids, inter-sheet interfaces may introduce planar defects analogous to LOF layers, and the different powder packing characteristics of sheets vs. spread powder beds may alter gas entrapment behavior. Zhang et al. (2025, *Additive Manufacturing Frontiers*) specifically studied the effects of polymeric binder on microstructural characteristics, suggesting binder-related defects are a recognized concern. Without access to defect characterization data from the MAPS benchmarking paper, it is impossible to assess whether MAPS porosity is comparable to, better than, or worse than LPBF in type, size distribution, or morphology.

### 5. Sample Sizes and Statistical Rigor

Across the LPBF 316L literature, sample sizes for tensile testing vary considerably: Wang et al. (2018) reported 2–3 repeat tests per condition (wang2018additivelymanufacturedhierarchical pages 16-18); Sarafan et al. (2022) used 3 specimens per laser power condition with formal t-tests at α = 0.05 (sarafan2022benchmarkingof316l pages 4-6, sarafan2022benchmarkingof316l pages 18-20); Chepkoech et al. (2024) tested a minimum of 5 specimens per thickness (chepkoech2024investigationofmicrostructures pages 4-5); Fedorenko et al. (2021) used 4–6 specimens per orientation (fedorenko2021anisotropyofmechanical pages 3-6). By contrast, large-scale data-driven studies such as Scime et al. (2023) used 6,299 tensile specimens but with a different objective (property prediction rather than benchmarking).

For a novel process like MAPS, where process variability is not yet well understood, **a sample size of 2–3 per condition—common in early AM studies—is insufficient to establish statistically meaningful comparisons with LPBF.** This is especially true because LPBF itself exhibits substantial machine-to-machine and batch-to-batch variability: Obeidi et al. (2021) found that even with a single powder batch and identical parameters across four LPBF machines, elongation ranged from 4% to 42% and UTS from 200 to 716 MPa (obeidi2021comparisonofthe pages 1-2). Reijonen et al. (2021) reported ~7% variability in tensile properties and ~20% in impact toughness across machines and powders even after standardized heat treatments (reijonen2021crosstestinglaserpowder pages 1-2). These findings demonstrate that **any MAPS-vs-LPBF comparison must account for inherent LPBF scatter, which is large.**

### 6. Fairness of Comparisons

A fair benchmarking comparison between MAPS and LPBF requires: (i) identical alloy composition from the same powder batch; (ii) optimized (not arbitrary) build parameters for each process; (iii) comparable post-processing (stress relief, solution annealing, HIP); (iv) identical specimen geometry, orientation, and testing protocol; and (v) comparable sample sizes with appropriate statistical analysis. Moshiri et al. (2019) demonstrated that even among established LPBF machines, user experience and expertise heavily influence outcomes, and older machines can match newer ones when operated by skilled users (moshiri2019benchmarkingoflaser pages 28-30, moshiri2019benchmarkingoflaser pages 24-28).

The MAPS benchmarking paper (Zhang et al. 2024) likely compares MAPS results against published LPBF literature values rather than against specimens produced in-house under controlled conditions. This introduces systematic biases: literature LPBF data spans a wide range of machines, powders, parameters, orientations, and post-processing states, making it easy to selectively cite favorable or unfavorable LPBF reference points. Without side-by-side production from the same powder lot on both MAPS and LPBF platforms, any claimed equivalence or superiority is inherently weakened.

### 7. Identified Methodological Weaknesses and Over-Generalized Conclusions

Based on the available evidence, the following weaknesses and gaps in the published MAPS benchmarking are identified:

1. **Single-group provenance**: All MAPS publications originate from a single research group (Lupoi et al., Trinity College Dublin), with no independent replication or validation by other laboratories.

2. **No fatigue data**: No fatigue performance data for MAPS-produced 316L has been identified. Given the known sensitivity of fatigue life to surface defects, internal porosity, and residual stresses (debroy2018additivemanufacturingof pages 109-110, sarafan2022benchmarkingof316l pages 26-27), this is a critical omission for any claim of structural suitability.

3. **No anisotropy data**: LPBF 316L shows significant anisotropy (up to ~17% difference in YS, ~20% in E between horizontal and vertical orientations) (fedorenko2021anisotropyofmechanical pages 3-6, weaver2021understandinganisotropictensile pages 10-12). MAPS, with its layered sheet consolidation, may exhibit different or more severe anisotropy, but no orientation-dependent data has been published.

4. **Limited sample sizes**: Early-stage MAPS studies likely use small sample sizes typical of novel process demonstrations (2–3 specimens per condition), which are insufficient for statistically robust comparisons given the known scatter in LPBF properties.

5. **Unfair comparison baselines**: Benchmarking against published LPBF literature values rather than side-by-side controlled experiments introduces selection bias and confounding variables (different powders, machines, operators, post-processing).

6. **Binder-related defect concerns unresolved**: The polymeric binder in MAPS introduces a unique defect pathway (carbon residues, binder burnout voids, inter-sheet delamination) that has no analog in LPBF. While Zhang et al. (2025) studied binder effects on microstructure, quantitative comparison of defect populations between MAPS and LPBF under matched conditions has not been established.

7. **Post-processing not matched**: LPBF 316L properties are highly dependent on post-processing (stress relief, solution annealing, HIP), and fair comparison requires identical treatments. It is unclear whether MAPS benchmarking applies the same post-processing to both processes.

8. **Over-generalization risk**: Claims of "comparable" or "competitive" mechanical properties based on single-condition, small-sample comparisons against literature averages risk over-generalizing from limited data. The LPBF 316L property space is extremely broad (YS 170–620 MPa, UTS 485–720 MPa, EL 4–74%) depending on parameters, machine, and post-processing (sarafan2022benchmarkingof316l pages 18-20, obeidi2021comparisonofthe pages 1-2), making it easy to appear competitive against the lower end while falling short of optimized LPBF.

### 8. Conclusions

MAPS is a promising emerging technology for multi-material additive manufacturing, but its benchmarking against LPBF for austenitic stainless steel 316L remains at an early and methodologically incomplete stage. The entire MAPS-316L benchmarking record appears to rest on a single publication (Zhang et al. 2024, *Materials & Design*) from a single research group, with no accessible independent validation. Critical property domains—fatigue, anisotropy, and systematic defect characterization—are absent from the published MAPS record. Sample sizes are likely small, comparisons against LPBF literature values rather than controlled side-by-side experiments introduce bias, and the unique defect pathways introduced by the polymeric binder in MAPS have not been quantitatively compared to LPBF defect populations. Before MAPS can be considered a validated alternative to LPBF for structural 316L applications, independent replication, fatigue testing across multiple build orientations, statistically powered mechanical property comparisons using the same powder batch and standardized post-processing, and comprehensive defect characterization (ideally by XCT) are all required.
