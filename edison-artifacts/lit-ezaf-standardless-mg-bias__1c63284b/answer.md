# Systematic Bias of Standardless eZAF / XPP / φ(ρz) EDS Quantification of Dilute Mg in Al Matrix

## 1. Documented Systematic Error in Standardless EDS Quantification

The systematic errors of standardless EDS quantification have been extensively characterized through proficiency testing by Newbury, Ritchie, and colleagues at NIST. The foundational study by Newbury, Swyt, and Myklebust (1995) evaluated first-principles standardless analysis embedded in the NIST Desktop Spectrum Analyzer (DTSA) against spectra from NIST standard reference materials, research materials, and binary compounds. The resulting distribution of errors ranged from **−90% to +150% relative**, encompassing 264 analyses of materials with concentrations above 1 wt% (newbury1995"standardless"quantitativeelectron pages 1-2, newbury1995"standardless"quantitativeelectron pages 6-6).

For commercial "remote-standards" standardless implementations—the category to which EDAX TEAM's eZAF Smart Quant belongs—the error distribution is narrower but still substantial. Newbury and Ritchie (2013) reported that the remote-standards standardless error histogram requires an envelope of approximately **±25% relative to capture ~95% of analyses** (newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 18-19). In a later assessment, Newbury and Ritchie (2015) characterized the error range of a commercial standardless implementation as approximately **±30% relative for 95% capture** (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 4-5). By comparison, standards-based k-ratio/ZAF analysis achieves **±5% relative for 95%** of analyses, and with closely matched standards can reach **<±2% relative** (newbury1995"standardless"quantitativeelectron pages 1-2, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 1-2).

Specific element-level errors from standardless proficiency tests illustrate the problem for low-Z and overlap-prone elements. On sulfide minerals analyzed with a commercial standardless protocol, covellite (CuS) showed Cu at +15% relative and S at −30% relative (yielding an incorrect Cu₃S₂ formula), and sphalerite (ZnS) showed Zn at +14% and S at −28% (giving incorrect Zn₃S₂) (newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 18-19). For NIST SRM K411 glass analyzed under ideal conditions with DTSA-II standards-based methods, Mg was measured at −1.0% to −1.1% relative error—close to ideal—demonstrating that standards-based approaches can handle Mg Kα accurately when properly implemented (newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 18-19, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 6-7).

## 2. How eZAF / Standardless φ(ρz) Handles Mg Kα Absorption in Al Matrix

The eZAF standardless routine, like all ZAF/φ(ρz)/XPP implementations, computes absorption corrections by modeling the depth distribution of X-ray generation φ(ρz) and applying the exponential attenuation law I/I₀ = exp[−(μ/ρ)·ρ·z·csc(ψ)], where μ/ρ is the mass absorption coefficient, z is the generation depth, and ψ is the X-ray takeoff angle (goldstein2018quantitativeanalysisfrom pages 17-18, goldstein2003quantitativexrayanalysis pages 20-24, goldstein2018quantitativeanalysisfrom pages 15-17). Absorption is typically the **largest correction** in electron-excited X-ray microanalysis, and this is especially true for low-energy lines (goldstein2003quantitativexrayanalysis pages 24-26, goldstein2003quantitativexrayanalysis pages 26-28).

For Mg Kα (1.253 keV) in an Al matrix, the mass absorption coefficient μ/ρ of Al for Mg Kα is high—on the order of ~4000 cm²/g—because the Mg Kα photon energy lies just above the Al L-absorption edge, making absorption very sensitive to path length and geometry. The Goldstein et al. textbook illustrates that the absorption correction for Mg in silicate glasses can reach ~1.18 (an 18% correction factor), and in an Al-rich matrix the correction is similarly large (goldstein2003quantitativexrayanalysis pages 20-24). Errors in the modeled φ(ρz) distribution, the adopted μ/ρ value, or the assumed specimen geometry propagate strongly into the final Mg concentration.

The standardless approach compounds this problem because it lacks locally measured standard intensities. Instead, it relies on a database of remotely measured or calculated standard intensities and a model of detector efficiency (goldstein2018quantitativeanalysisfrom pages 7-9, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 4-5). Small systematic errors in detector window modeling, low-energy efficiency calibration, or the mass absorption coefficient tables used for the Al matrix are not self-correcting and directly bias the Mg result. There is **no inherent tendency unique to eZAF** to consistently over- or underestimate Mg; the direction of bias depends on the specific implementation's database, detector model, and absorption coefficient tables. However, several compounding factors (discussed in Section 3) can produce large positive Mg errors in the specific case of dilute Mg in an Al matrix.

## 3. Can Standardless EDS Report Mg = 1.4–1.6 wt% When True Bulk Is 0.3 wt%?

**Yes, this magnitude of error is physically plausible**, though it represents a ~4–5× overestimate (approximately +370–430% relative error), which exceeds typical standardless errors for well-behaved systems. Multiple compounding factors specific to the Mg-in-Al system can produce such large discrepancies:

The following table summarizes the specific physical and analytical challenges:

| Challenge Factor | Description | Impact on Mg Quantification | Direction of Bias (over/underestimate) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Peak overlap | Mg Kα is at 1.253 keV and Al Kα at 1.486 keV, only 233 eV apart; with ~130–135 eV FWHM SDD resolution, Mg lies on the low-energy tail of the dominant Al peak, so deconvolution must separate a small peak from a much larger adjacent one. Peak-shape/tailing, pile-up, and fit-model errors can transfer counts between Al and Mg. (pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 5-7, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 14-16, newbury2012faultsandfoibles pages 1-2) | Small fitting errors in the Al tail can create a false Mg peak or inflate a real one, especially at 0.2–0.5 wt% Mg where the Mg signal is weak relative to Al. | Usually **overestimate** if Al-tail counts are assigned to Mg; can **underestimate** if Mg is swallowed by Al. |
| Absorption correction | ZAF/φ(ρz)/XPP correct Mg by modeling its generated depth distribution and applying exponential attenuation using the mass absorption coefficient; low-energy Mg Kα is strongly absorbed in Al, and absorption is often the largest matrix correction. For Mg Kα in Al, μ/ρ is high (order of ~4000 cm²/g), so small errors in assumed depth, takeoff angle, or MAC propagate strongly into concentration. (goldstein2018quantitativeanalysisfrom pages 17-18, goldstein2003quantitativexrayanalysis pages 20-24, goldstein2003quantitativexrayanalysis pages 24-26, goldstein2018quantitativeanalysisfrom pages 15-17) | If the software over-corrects for absorption, it raises the inferred generated Mg intensity and thus Mg wt%; if it under-corrects, Mg comes out low. | Either direction; **overestimate** if absorption is over-corrected, **underestimate** if under-corrected. |
| Normalization to 100% | Standardless routines typically compute relative concentrations and force the analytical total to unity because no local standards/dose-based absolute scale is preserved. Any misassigned or missing counts are redistributed across the normalized composition. (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 4-5, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 16-18, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 23-25, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 18-19) | A small absolute Mg error becomes a large relative error at 0.3 wt%; false Mg intensity from overlap/background modeling can survive and then be amplified by normalization. | Either direction; often **overestimate** for minor Mg when spurious counts are assigned to it. |
| Background subtraction | Mg Kα sits in the low-energy region where continuum background changes rapidly; EDS background is not measured directly under the peak but estimated by filtering/subtraction, and overlap/deconvolution uncertainty contributes to the detection limit. (pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 5-7, flude2017applicationofbenchtop pages 15-18, spasevski2026challengesinmeasuring pages 2-5) | Slight over-subtraction can erase dilute Mg; slight under-subtraction can leave excess counts that are fit as Mg. At trace levels this can dominate the result. | Either direction; **overestimate** from under-subtracted background, **underestimate** from over-subtraction. |
| Detector window and efficiency | Low-energy photons are preferentially attenuated by detector window/entrance layers and detector efficiency modeling matters strongly below ~1–2 keV. Standardless methods depend on an accurate detector-efficiency model; errors there directly bias Mg intensity recovery. (liritzis2011potassiumdeterminationsusing pages 8-9, pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 5-7, pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 1-3, pinard2020practicalaspectsfor pages 1-3) | Incorrect low-energy efficiency calibration shifts Mg k-ratio/intensity more than Al or Si, leading to systematic Mg bias even if spectral fitting is otherwise good. | Often **underestimate** if low-energy efficiency is worse than modeled; can **overestimate** if efficiency correction is excessive. |
| Surface condition / oxide layer | Mg Kα is much more sensitive than harder lines to surface roughness, scratches, contamination, and oxide films because of its short escape depth and strong absorption. Native Al₂O₃ and any polishing damage add extra attenuation not accounted for by ideal bulk-flat models. (liritzis2011potassiumdeterminationsusing pages 8-9, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 11-13, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 23-26, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 6-7) | Oxide/roughness suppresses escaping Mg more strongly than Al, making matrix correction unreliable and broadening spot-to-spot scatter; local geometry changes can produce very large errors. | Usually **underestimate** from extra surface absorption; roughness can also cause erratic results in either direction. |
| Counting statistics | At true Mg = 0.2–0.5 wt% in an Al matrix, the Mg peak has very low net counts compared with Al Kα and the continuum. The uncertainty of the deconvolved net Mg peak becomes large unless very high total counts are acquired. (newbury2019electronexcitedxraymicroanalysis pages 18-20, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 23-25, spasevski2026challengesinmeasuring pages 2-5) | Poor statistics increase the chance that noise or fit residuals are interpreted as Mg, or that real Mg is not detected at all; confidence intervals become wide relative to the signal. | Random both ways; apparent single-point **overestimates** or **underestimates** are common. |
| Microstructural heterogeneity | In AlSi10Mg and related Al-Si-Mg alloys, Mg may not be uniformly dissolved in α-Al; it can partition to ultrafine Mg₂Si or boundary/precipitate regions. SEM-EDS on the matrix may miss Mg, while TEM-EDS on precipitate-rich boundaries can show much higher local Mg. Lam et al. reported Mg could not be detected by SEM-EDS in bulk AlSi10Mg at 0.2–0.5 wt% overall, yet TEM-EDS measured ~10.84–12.55 wt% Mg locally at boundaries/precipitates. (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 6-7, lam2015phaseanalysisand pages 7-9) | A spot intersecting Mg-rich precipitates can report Mg far above bulk; a matrix-only spot can report near-zero Mg. Bulk composition cannot be inferred reliably from one local standardless spot. | Either direction depending on spot placement; **overestimate** on precipitates/boundaries, **underestimate** in matrix-only regions. |

**The most likely scenario producing Mg = 1.4–1.6 wt% from a 0.3 wt% Mg sample involves a combination of the following:**

**(a) Peak overlap/deconvolution error.** At ~130 eV FWHM resolution, Mg Kα (1.253 keV) and Al Kα (1.486 keV) are separated by only 233 eV—less than 2 FWHM. The Mg peak sits directly on the low-energy tail of the dominant Al Kα peak. Any error in the peak-shape model (e.g., incomplete Gaussian tailing, shelf artifacts) transfers Al counts into the Mg fitting window. At 0.3 wt% Mg versus ~89 wt% Al, the Mg:Al peak ratio is roughly 1:300, making the deconvolution extremely sensitive to tail modeling (pinard2020developmentandvalidation pages 5-7, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 14-16, newbury2012faultsandfoibles pages 1-2).

**(b) Microstructural heterogeneity.** In AlSi10Mg alloys—whether cast or additively manufactured—Mg is not uniformly distributed. Mg partitions strongly into Mg₂Si precipitates and eutectic boundaries. Lam et al. (2015) explicitly reported that **Mg could not be detected by SEM-EDS** in the bulk α-Al matrix of SLM AlSi10Mg (where the overall alloy is 0.2–0.5 wt% Mg), yet TEM-EDS on boundary regions measured approximately **10.84–12.55 wt% Mg** locally (lam2015phaseanalysisand pages 7-9). If the electron beam excitation volume overlaps a Mg₂Si precipitate cluster even partially, the local Mg concentration sampled can far exceed the bulk average.

**(c) Normalization amplification.** Standardless EDS normalizes all concentrations to 100%. If an undetected constituent (e.g., oxygen from surface oxide or subsurface porosity) is omitted, the remaining elements are systematically inflated. If oxygen is present but unlisted, even 2–3 wt% of missing oxygen redistributes proportionally, pushing minor elements higher (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 4-5, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 16-18, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 23-25).

**(d) Surface effects.** Native Al₂O₃ is ~3–5 nm thick on freshly polished Al, but oxide layers on AlSi10Mg can be thicker, especially at Mg-rich precipitates (MgO forms preferentially). This surface layer differentially absorbs Mg Kα relative to Al Kα, introducing additional systematic bias that the flat-polished bulk model does not account for (liritzis2011potassiumdeterminationsusing pages 8-9, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 6-7).

**(e) Pulse pile-up artifacts.** At high count rates, Al Kα + Al Kα sum peaks appear at ~2.97 keV, and other coincidence peaks can complicate the low-energy spectral region. If dead time is not properly controlled, coincidence events can distort the spectral shape near the Mg Kα region (newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 14-16, newbury2012faultsandfoibles pages 2-5).

It is important to note that typical standardless relative errors of ±25–30% applied to 0.3 wt% Mg would predict a range of roughly 0.21–0.39 wt%—well short of 1.4–1.6 wt%. Achieving a ~5× overestimate requires **compounding of multiple error sources**, with microstructural heterogeneity (beam intersecting Mg₂Si precipitates) being the most likely dominant contributor, amplified by peak deconvolution errors and normalization effects (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 4-5, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 18-19, lam2015phaseanalysisand pages 7-9).

## 4. Recommended Corrections

Based on the extensive NIST literature and the Goldstein et al. textbook framework, the following corrections are recommended, in order of decreasing rigor:

**(1) Standards-based k-ratio analysis with NIST DTSA-II.** This is the gold-standard recommendation from Newbury and Ritchie. Export the EDS spectra in .msa format and process them in NIST DTSA-II using measured reference spectra from pure element or compound standards (e.g., MgO for Mg). DTSA-II uses multiple linear least-squares (MLLS) peak fitting that handles the Mg Kα/Al Kα overlap far more robustly than typical vendor deconvolution, and its k-ratio/matrix correction protocol preserves the analytical total as a diagnostic of data quality. With this approach, 95% of major/minor constituent results fall within ±5% RDEV, and at low beam energy >98% were within ±5% RDEV (newbury2024testingtheaccuracy pages 1-3, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 26-27, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 11-13, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 23-26).

**(2) Measured Mg standard on the same instrument.** If DTSA-II is not available, using a physical Mg or MgO standard measured under identical conditions on the EDAX system and performing standards-based quantification within EDAX TEAM (rather than eZAF Smart Quant) eliminates the unknown systematic offset in the remote-standards database for Mg. The k-ratio cancels detector efficiency and many instrumental factors (goldstein2018quantitativeanalysisfrom pages 7-9, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 1-2).

**(3) WDS (wavelength-dispersive spectrometry) verification.** For the specific problem of 0.2–0.45 wt% Mg in an Al-Si matrix, WDS on an electron microprobe provides ~10× better energy resolution (~10 eV vs. ~130 eV), eliminating the Mg Kα/Al Kα overlap entirely. WDS with proper matrix corrections routinely achieves ±2% relative or better for this concentration range (newbury1995"standardless"quantitativeelectron pages 1-2, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 11-13).

**(4) Careful sample preparation and measurement protocol.** Regardless of quantification method, the specimen must be flat-polished to <50 nm surface roughness (ideally <20 nm for sub-keV lines) to minimize absorption path-length anomalies (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 11-13, newbury*2013isscanningelectron pages 23-26, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 6-7). Beam current should be measured with a Faraday cup, dead time kept below ~10% for SDD-EDS, and spectra accumulated to high total counts (≥10⁶) to improve counting statistics for dilute Mg.

**(5) Area analysis rather than spot analysis.** Given the severe microstructural heterogeneity of AlSi10Mg (Mg₂Si precipitates vs. α-Al matrix), bulk composition estimates require either large-area raster analysis covering many microstructural units or multiple widely spaced point analyses averaged together. Single-spot analyses on this alloy cannot represent bulk composition (lam2015phaseanalysisand pages 7-9).

**(6) Analytical total as a diagnostic.** When using standards-based analysis, the raw (unnormalized) analytical total should fall within 0.98–1.02 for a well-prepared specimen. Totals significantly below or above this range indicate problems with the analysis—a diagnostic that is lost when standardless normalization forces the total to unity (newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 4-5, newbury2015performingelementalmicroanalysis pages 23-25).

In summary, a standardless eZAF result of 1.4–1.6 wt% Mg on an AlSi10Mg sample with 0.3 wt% true bulk Mg is most likely dominated by microstructural sampling bias (beam hitting Mg₂Si precipitates) compounded by peak deconvolution errors in the Mg Kα/Al Kα overlap region and normalization artifacts. The recommended remedy is standards-based k-ratio analysis (ideally with NIST DTSA-II) on a properly prepared flat-polished specimen using large-area raster measurements, with WDS verification if the highest accuracy is required.
