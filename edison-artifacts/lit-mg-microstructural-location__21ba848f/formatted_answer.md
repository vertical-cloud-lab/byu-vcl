## Where Does Mg Reside in As-Built LPBF AlSi10Mg Microstructure?

### 1. Mg Distribution: Bulk vs. Grain-Boundary vs. Intercellular vs. Melt-Pool-Boundary

The as-built LPBF AlSi10Mg microstructure consists of fine α-Al cellular dendrites (~500 nm cell width) surrounded by an interconnected eutectic Si-rich network at cell boundaries. High-resolution characterization consistently demonstrates that **Mg is not uniformly distributed** but partitions strongly to the intercellular eutectic network.

**STEM-EDX mapping** by Cauwenbergh et al. (2021) directly imaged the Al, Si, and Mg distributions within single primary α-Al cells at the melt-pool centre (Figure 8 in their work). The Si and Mg maps clearly co-localize at cell boundaries, with quantitative STEM-EDX measuring ~1.7 wt% Mg in the eutectic network versus ~0.5 wt% Mg retained in the primary α-Al cell interiors (cauwenbergh2021unravellingthemultiscale pages 9-10, cauwenbergh2021unravellingthemultiscale media f66ff10d). This enrichment is consistent with the low equilibrium partition coefficient for Mg in Al (k₀ ≈ 0.195), which causes Mg rejection into the last liquid to solidify (cauwenbergh2021unravellingthemultiscale pages 9-10). The rapid LPBF solidification, however, produces significant solute trapping, so the α-Al cells retain more Mg in solid solution than equilibrium or even Scheil models would predict (cauwenbergh2021unravellingthemultiscale pages 9-10).

**EDX elemental mapping** by Qin et al. (2020) confirmed that Mg is co-located with Si along cell boundaries in both fine-cell and coarse-cell zones of the melt pool, forming Si/Mg-rich intercellular networks (qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 3-5). They also identified a high number density of sub-micron oxygen-rich particles at cell boundaries and near eutectic lamellae (qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 3-5).

**Atom probe tomography (APT)** by Giovagnoli (2021) revealed nanoscale Mg clustering within the as-built microstructure. Using a 3 at.% Mg iso-concentration surface, the APT reconstructions showed nanometric Mg-based precipitates/clusters, often co-associated with Si atoms (giovagnoli2021ontheprocessmicrostructureproperties pages 65-69, giovagnoli2021ontheprocessmicrostructurepropertiesa pages 65-69). These clusters are considered precursors (nuclei) for subsequent Mg₂Si precipitation during in-situ thermal cycling from adjacent laser passes or during post-build heat treatments (giovagnoli2021ontheprocessmicrostructurepropertiesa pages 65-69). Whether clustering occurs or is suppressed depends on local thermal history: strong heat flow from neighbouring melt pools can dissolve clusters and instead promote nanometric Si precipitation within the α-Al cells (giovagnoli2021ontheprocessmicrostructurepropertiesa pages 65-69).

**Mg₂Si precipitates:** In the as-built condition, Mg₂Si is not generally observed as a prominent equilibrium precipitate because the rapid solidification produces a supersaturated α-Al matrix. However, DSC analysis reveals an exothermic peak near ~317 °C attributed to Mg₂Si precipitation from the metastable supersaturated state (cattano2019feasibilitystudieson pages 96-100). XRD studies have also detected α-Al, Si, and Mg₂Si phases in some SLM AlSi10Mg samples (wang2020selectivelasermelting pages 29-32). APT data suggest that the nanometric Mg+Si clusters act as nuclei for accelerated Mg₂Si precipitation during subsequent reheating events (giovagnoli2021ontheprocessmicrostructurepropertiesa pages 65-69).

**MgAl₂O₄ spinel inclusions and MgO surface films:** Raza et al. (2021) performed detailed XPS and TEM/STEM-EDX characterization of AlSi10Mg powder surfaces over 30 months of reuse. XPS identified MgAl₂O₄ spinel forming within an Al₂O₃ matrix on powder surfaces, with average oxide thickness growing from ~4 nm (virgin) to ~38 nm (reused 30 months) (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 8-10, raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 10-11, raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 1-2). Spatter particles exhibited particularly thick, nodular oxide scales (50–125 nm) composed of MgAl₂O₄ and Al₂O₃ (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 4-6). Thermodynamic calculations (Thermo-Calc, Ellingham diagrams) confirmed that MgAl₂O₄ and Al₂O₃ are the only oxide phases with sufficiently negative ΔG to form directly from the AlSi10Mg liquid at processing temperatures, with a stability hierarchy of SiO₂ < Al₂O₃ < MgAl₂O₄ < MgO (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 8-10, raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 10-11).

**Mg-rich nanoscale oxides at melt-pool boundaries:** Qin et al. (2020) documented sub-micron oxygen-rich particles of various chemistries (Si-, Mg-, and/or Al-rich oxides) at cell boundaries and melt-pool boundaries. These oxide particles originated from native powder surface oxide films that were disrupted by the laser, floated in the melt pool, and were pushed by the solidification front to the last-to-solidify regions (qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 3-5). Definitive phase identification (e.g., distinguishing MgO from MgAl₂O₄ from Al₂O₃) within the built parts was noted as incomplete and suggested as future work (qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 3-5). Oxide films have also been reported to preferentially localize between laser scan traces rather than between layers (liu2020areviewof pages 19-22).

**Mg segregation in the cellular eutectic Si network:** As detailed in Section 1, this is the primary location where Mg concentrates in the as-built microstructure, reaching approximately 1.7 wt% in the eutectic network by STEM-EDX measurement (cauwenbergh2021unravellingthemultiscale pages 9-10).

### 3. SEM-EDS Bias for Mg Relative to ICP-OES Bulk Values

There is strong evidence that SEM-EDS measurements on polished LPBF AlSi10Mg cross-sections can significantly overestimate Mg content relative to bulk ICP-OES values. Lynch (2019) demonstrated dramatic voltage-dependent variability: at 5 kV (shallow interaction volume), Mg was measured at 4.35 wt%, whereas at 20 kV (deeper interaction volume) on the same sample it was only 1.01 wt% (lynch2019improvedselectivelaser pages 226-230). Given that the nominal bulk Mg content of AlSi10Mg is typically 0.3–0.5 wt%, a 5 kV reading of 4.35 wt% represents an overestimation of roughly **8–14× relative to nominal bulk**. Even the 20 kV reading of 1.01 wt% represents a ~2–3× overestimation. Lynch attributed this to thick surface oxide films, spatter/globule residues with oxide shells on the measured surface, surface topography creating shadows in EDS maps, and charging artifacts from spatter particles (lynch2019improvedselectivelaser pages 226-230).

Ho et al. (2022) similarly reported ~1.01 wt% Mg by SEM-EDS on AM AlSi10Mg surfaces, while XPS (which is far more surface-sensitive) found Mg below detection limits after etching (ho2022ultrascalablesurfacestructuring pages 4-5). This discrepancy illustrates the depth-dependent and technique-dependent nature of Mg quantification.

Several factors contribute to this bias: (i) **native and process-induced oxide films** enriched in Mg and Al oxides (MgAl₂O₄, Al₂O₃) on the metallographic surface; (ii) **microstructural heterogeneity**, where the EDS interaction volume may sample Mg-rich intercellular network regions disproportionately; (iii) **matrix effects and phase interference** in multiphase Al-Si-Mg microstructures (thomas2021slagrefiningof pages 118-124); and (iv) **colloidal silica polishing residue**, which can affect surface composition although strong evidence for direct Si contamination was not found in the documents reviewed (yakubov2022laserpowderbed pages 113-117). Thus, SEM-EDS can plausibly produce Mg values 3–7× or even higher relative to ICP-OES bulk values, depending primarily on accelerating voltage, surface condition, and whether surface oxides/spatter residues are present.

### 4. Mg Loss During LPBF Processing Due to Evaporation

Magnesium has a substantially higher vapor pressure than aluminum—by 5–8 orders of magnitude at relevant melt-pool temperatures—making preferential Mg vaporization during LPBF a significant concern (brice2018compositionalsensitivityin pages 80-84, brice2018compositionalsensitivityin pages 76-80). The Mg boiling point (~1091 °C) is far below typical LPBF melt-pool temperatures, which can exceed 1500–2000 °C depending on laser power and scanning speed (sharma2022advancementsinthe pages 4-6).

Quantitative Mg loss data come primarily from electron-beam additive deposition studies on Al alloy 2139. Brice (2018) measured **30–60% Mg loss** in deposited beads depending on Mg concentration and thermal conditions, with up to ~80% loss reported for higher-Mg Al-5356 (brice2018compositionalsensitivityin pages 166-170, brice2018compositionalsensitivityin pages 101-106). Bärtl et al. (2022) modeled evaporation using Langmuir's equation with Alcock's vapor pressure formulations and Fromm's shielding factor for inert gas, predicting rapid Mg evaporation from Scalmalloy® within ~45 seconds at 540 K superheat (bartl2022influenceofsurface pages 8-10). Importantly, the inert gas atmosphere in LPBF (argon or nitrogen at ~750 mbar) provides a shielding factor of only ~1.1 × 10⁻³, meaning evaporation is substantially suppressed relative to vacuum but not eliminated (bartl2022influenceofsurface pages 8-10).

For AlSi10Mg specifically, quantitative Mg loss data from LPBF are limited. One study on AlSi7Mg0.6 (similar alloy) using XRF found no significant change in Mg content between powder and built specimens within analytical uncertainty (smolina2022influenceofthe pages 8-11). This suggests that under optimized LPBF parameters with inert-gas shielding, **Mg loss in AlSi10Mg may be modest (a few percent)** rather than the dramatic 30–80% losses observed in vacuum EB deposition. Nevertheless, higher energy densities, keyhole-mode processing, or insufficient shielding could increase losses substantially, and compositional monitoring (e.g., by ICP-OES) is advisable for critical applications (mukherjee2024integratedmodelingto pages 6-7, brice2018compositionalsensitivityin pages 80-84). Given that the nominal Mg content is already low (~0.3–0.5 wt%), even small absolute losses could be fractionally significant and affect age-hardening response through Mg₂Si precipitation (brice2018compositionalsensitivityin pages 80-84).

### 5. Small Spheres or Nodules on Polished LPBF AlSi10Mg Surfaces

Small spherical particles or nodules observed on polished LPBF AlSi10Mg cross-sections are most commonly identified as **spatter inclusions** rather than intermetallics or polishing artifacts. Spatter particles are melt-pool ejecta generated during LPBF by recoil pressure, Marangoni flow, keyhole instabilities, and vapor-jet-induced gas flows (rehman2021spatterformationand pages 12-15, rehman2021spatterformationand pages 10-12). These ejected molten ligaments solidify in flight into spherical or near-spherical particles and can redeposit on the part surface or powder bed (bartl2022influenceofsurface pages 3-5, raza2021powderdegradationduring pages 23-26).

Raza et al. (2021) characterized spatter particles from AlSi10Mg LPBF using TEM/EDX and found them to be **heavily oxidized spherical particles** carrying thick oxide shells composed of MgAl₂O₄ spinel and Al₂O₃, with oxide scale thicknesses of 50–125 nm (raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 3-4, raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 8-10). These spatter particles appear as bright, high-contrast features in SEM due to their oxide shells. Additionally, Bärtl et al. (2022) noted that spatter particles can adhere to already-solidified surfaces and resist removal by the recoater, potentially becoming incorporated into subsequent layers and visible in cross-sections. They also reported visible MgO dust in the process chamber during LPBF (bartl2022influenceofsurface pages 3-5).

A second source of small spheres is **satellite particles**—fine particles generated during gas atomization that cling to larger powder particles and can detach during handling or processing (bosio2021laserpowderbeda pages 53-56, bosio2021laserpowderbed pages 53-56). Sub-micron oxide chip residues from disrupted native powder oxide films may also appear as nodular features in the microstructure (bosio2021laserpowderbeda pages 53-56, bosio2021laserpowderbed pages 53-56).

Polishing artifacts per se (e.g., embedded colloidal silica particles from OPS suspension) cannot be entirely excluded as a source of surface nodules, but the literature overwhelmingly identifies the spherical features as process-derived spatter and satellite particles. EDS point analysis on such features typically reveals elevated oxygen, confirming their oxide-rich nature and distinguishing them from intermetallic compounds or polishing media residues (lynch2019improvedselectivelaser pages 226-230). The morphology—spherical, often with rough or dendritic surface textures from rapid solidification—is characteristic of molten ejecta rather than polishing debris (revuru2021enhancementinfatigue pages 9-14, raza2021degradationofalsi10mg pages 4-6).

### Summary

In as-built LPBF AlSi10Mg, Mg resides in multiple distinct microstructural locations: (i) in supersaturated solid solution within α-Al cells (~0.5 wt%), (ii) enriched in the intercellular eutectic Si network at cell boundaries (~1.7 wt%), (iii) as nanoscale Mg+Si clusters detectable by APT, (iv) as MgAl₂O₄ spinel and Al₂O₃ oxide films on powder/spatter surfaces that become entrained as sub-micron oxide inclusions at cell and melt-pool boundaries, and (v) as latent Mg₂Si precipitates that nucleate upon subsequent heating. SEM-EDS measurements on polished surfaces can overestimate Mg by factors of 3× to >10× due to surface oxides, voltage-dependent interaction volume effects, and microstructural heterogeneity. Mg evaporation during LPBF under inert gas is likely modest for optimized parameters but can become significant at high energy densities. Spherical nodules on polished surfaces are predominantly oxidized spatter inclusions rather than intermetallics or polishing artifacts.
