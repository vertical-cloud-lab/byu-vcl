# Surface Features Observed as "Small Spheres" on Metallographically-Polished LPBF/SLM AlSi10Mg Cross-Sections Under SEM

## 1. Polishing-Introduced Artifacts

Metallographic polishing of aluminum alloys is a three-body tribological system in which the specimen, polishing pad, and abrasive particles interact to produce not only a mirror-finished surface but also a range of artifacts (remond2002implicationsofpolishing pages 6-7). The following artifacts could appear as small spheres under SEM at moderate-to-high magnification:

**Colloidal silica residue** is the most likely candidate for "small spheres covering the surface." The final polishing step for LPBF AlSi10Mg typically employs 0.05 µm colloidal silica on a soft cloth (voort2006metallographicpreparationfor pages 2-3, voort2011metallographicspecimenpreparation pages 2-4). These ~20 nm primary SiO₂ particles can agglomerate and crystallize on the surface if rinsing is insufficient, forming uniform nanosphere clusters visible at 10,000–50,000× magnification (remond2002implicationsofpolishing pages 7-9, remond2002implicationsofpolishing pages 19-21). EDX will show Si + O with little or no Al signal, distinguishing them from matrix-derived features. Rémond et al. documented that colloidal silica polishing can leave residues and that "crystallization of the colloidal silica" can occur on finished surfaces (remond2002implicationsofpolishing pages 7-9).

**Embedded abrasive particles** (SiC, diamond, alumina) from earlier grinding or polishing steps can become trapped in the softer aluminum matrix. On Al-NiP substrates, embedded alumina particles with protrusion heights of ~19 nm and embedment depths of ~30 nm have been documented as "thermal asperity" defects (salleh2016effectsofnonspherical pages 9-10, salleh2016effectsofnonspherical pages 1-2). These appear as discrete protruding particles in SE imaging, often associated with scratches or comet tails (remond2002implicationsofpolishing pages 6-7). Polishing pads can trap wear debris in grooves, which subsequently transfers to the specimen surface (remond2002implicationsofpolishing pages 6-7).

**Pulled-out eutectic Si particles and pinhole defects** are well-documented in Al-Si alloys. Mueller et al. demonstrated that polishing and etching can extract small Si particles from the matrix, leaving pinholes, and that submicron Fe- or Ti-rich intermetallics at the root of these pinholes can also be dislodged (mueller2017siliconparticlepinhole pages 9-10, mueller2017siliconparticlepinhole pages 4-5, mueller2017siliconparticlepinhole pages 5-7). In LPBF AlSi10Mg, where the eutectic Si network is extremely fine (~60–80 nm lamellar spacing), mechanical polishing may preferentially remove Si from cell boundaries, producing apparent voids or re-deposited particles (qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 3-5).

**Smeared Al and comet tails** arise from the softness of the α-Al matrix. Metal flow can cap over harder phases or produce directional tails behind particles, creating rounded mound-like features that may superficially resemble particles in SE imaging (voort2006metallographicpreparationfor pages 1-2).

## 2. Sample Preparation Contamination

Post-CMP cleaning studies have shown that residual particles from the polishing slurry (sub-500 nm SiO₂ or Al₂O₃) persist even after scrubbing, and ultrasonic cleaning can introduce metallic contamination from equipment wear (huang2009particlesdetectionand pages 5-5). Drying residues from ethanol, water, or other solvents can leave coffee-ring deposits or patchy films that appear as discrete spots. Environmental contamination can also introduce Si-O spheres from calibration aerosol or handling, as documented by Kandler et al. who identified "Si-O spheres" as instrumental contamination artifacts using SEM + EDX (kandler2015singleparticlecharacterizationof pages 6-7).

Mounting epoxy bleed-out (C + O-rich), Kapton tape residue, and fingerprint contamination can all produce localized deposits near specimen edges or pores. These are typically carbon-rich in EDX and show no spatial correlation with the underlying LPBF microstructure.

## 3. Real Microstructural Features

The as-built LPBF AlSi10Mg microstructure consists of a fine cellular/dendritic α-Al matrix with Si-rich intercellular networks (pezzato2022influenceofsilicon pages 4-5, qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 3-5, wei2017thealsi10mgsamples pages 12-15). Key features that could appear as small particles or spheres include:

- **Nano-Si precipitates**: Qin et al. identified numerous nanoscale, predominantly Si-rich precipitates (spheres, ellipsoids, plates, and needles, ~5–100 nm) nucleated in eutectic-free regions within α-Al cells. These are fully or semi-coherent with the Al matrix and arise from the supersaturated solid solution produced by rapid solidification (qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 1-2, qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 2-3).

- **Mg₂Si precipitates**: Mg segregates to cell boundaries with Si and can form Mg₂Si at cellular boundaries (wei2017thealsi10mgsamples pages 12-15). In the as-built condition, these are typically very fine (tens of nm) but may coarsen in the heat-affected zone (HAZ) regions between melt pools (qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 1-2).

- **Eutectic Si network nodes**: The interconnected Si network at α-Al cell boundaries consists of lamellar to fibrous eutectic structures with spacings of ~67–80 nm (qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 3-5). In BSE imaging, these appear as bright continuous networks rather than isolated spheres.

- **Oxide inclusions**: Sub-micron oxygen-rich particles have been observed at cell boundaries and near eutectic features (qin2020rapidsolidificationand pages 3-5).

- **Spatter particles and partially melted powder (PMP)**: These are typically much larger (1–40+ µm) and would appear as near-spherical features with oxide shells at the intersection of the polished plane (rosenthal2014microstructureandmechanical pages 1-3).

- **Gas porosity**: Round voids (0.5–20 µm) appear dark in both SE and BSE and can be confused with particles when out of focus (wei2017thealsi10mgsamples pages 12-15).

## 4. Why the Fishscale Melt-Pool Pattern is Not Visible in SEM on Unetched Polished Surfaces

This observation is fully explained by the fundamental difference in contrast mechanisms between optical microscopy and SEM on unetched, flat-polished aluminum surfaces.

**Optical microscopy** relies on reflected light, which is sensitive to subtle differences in reflectivity, thin-film interference effects, and minor compositional/structural variations that produce differential light scattering. Even on a mirror-polished, unetched LPBF AlSi10Mg surface, the melt-pool boundaries create slight differences in reflectivity due to local compositional gradients (Si enrichment at boundaries) and microstructural coarsening in the HAZ, producing visible fishscale patterns.

**SEM imaging**, by contrast, depends on either topographic contrast (secondary electrons, SE) or atomic-number contrast (backscattered electrons, BSE). On a properly polished, unetched surface that is deliberately flat, SE topographic contrast is minimal because there is no surface relief (torres2023microscopytechniquesto pages 12-13, torres2023microscopytechniquesto pages 1-2). BSE contrast requires sufficient atomic-number (Z) differences between adjacent regions; in LPBF AlSi10Mg, the melt-pool boundary is primarily a transition from fine-to-coarse cellular morphology of the same Al-Si composition, producing insufficient Z-contrast in BSE to delineate boundaries (torres2023microscopytechniquesto pages 12-13).

Okoro explicitly documented that "the unetched microstructure...had a featureless surface with no pores or intermetallic particles present" when examining LPBF AlSi10Mg by SEM, attributing this to the extremely fine cellular structure produced by rapid cooling (okoro2021irradiationassistedcorrosion pages 60-67). The disappearance of melt-pool patterns in SEM on unetched surfaces is therefore primarily due to **insufficient etching (no etchant used)** rather than a contamination/charging layer. Chemical etching creates the selective topographic relief—through differential dissolution of phases and the native Al₂O₃ film—that is necessary for SEM-SE contrast at melt-pool boundaries (zejli2026assessingthesl pages 11-15). A residual polishing layer or charging/contamination could exacerbate the problem but is not the primary cause; the fundamental issue is that a flat, unetched LPBF AlSi10Mg cross-section simply does not provide the contrast mechanisms that SEM requires to resolve melt-pool morphology.

## 5. Recommended Etchants and Post-Polish Preparation for LPBF AlSi10Mg

The recommended metallographic preparation sequence, following Vander Voort's methodology and ASTM E407-07 guidelines, proceeds as follows:

**Polishing sequence**: SiC grinding (240-grit) → progressive diamond polishing (9 µm → 3 µm → 1 µm) → 0.05 µm colloidal silica final polish (3 min) → optional vibratory colloidal silica polish (≥20 min for EBSD-quality surfaces) → thorough rinse with DI water and ethanol → dry immediately (voort2006metallographicpreparationfor pages 2-3, voort2011metallographicspecimenpreparation pages 2-4). Vander Voort demonstrated that a 20-minute vibratory colloidal silica polish after the standard cycle improves EBSD band contrast by at least 10% and is "very helpful for scratch and damage removal" (voort2011metallographicspecimenpreparation pages 2-4).

**Etching for melt-pool revelation**: **Keller's reagent** (2 mL HF + 3 mL HCl + 5 mL HNO₃ + 190 mL H₂O) is the most widely validated etchant for LPBF AlSi10Mg, applied per ASTM E407-07 (zejli2026assessingthesl pages 8-11, zejli2026assessingthesl pages 11-15). Immersion for approximately 60 seconds provides optimal melt-pool boundary contrast; 40 seconds produces inadequate contrast while 80 seconds causes over-etching and pitting (zejli2026assessingthesl pages 11-15). The HF component dissolves the native Al₂O₃ film, HNO₃ acts as an oxidizing agent initiating surface dissolution, and HCl selectively attacks Si-rich phases and inclusions (zejli2026assessingthesl pages 11-15).

**For Mg₂Si and intermetallic characterization**: A dilute 0.5% HF aqueous etch facilitates the identification of Mg₂Si particles and AlFeSi intermetallics on polished aluminum surfaces (voort2013microstructureinvestigationsof pages 1-2). For enhanced grain boundary contrast, Mohammadtaheri's two-step method—pre-etching in 1 g NaCl in 50 mL H₃PO₄ (40%) at 70°C for 3 min, followed by Weck's reagent (100 mL H₂O + 1 g NaOH + 4 g KMnO₄)—provides superior grain boundary delineation without requiring polarized light (mohammadtaheri2012anewmetallographic pages 1-3). For orientation mapping, Barker's electrolytic anodize (5 mL HBF₄ + 200 mL H₂O) can be used under polarized light (tamadon2017developmentofmetallographic pages 1-3).

It should be noted that conventional etchants including Keller's and Kroll's reagents do not always satisfactorily reveal microstructure in all aluminum alloys, and optimization of concentration and immersion time may be required (tamadon2017developmentofmetallographic pages 1-3, tamadon2017developmentofmetallographic pages 5-7).

In summary, "small spheres covering the surface" on a metallographically-polished, unetched LPBF AlSi10Mg cross-section are most likely **colloidal silica polishing residue**, with embedded abrasive particles as a secondary possibility. EDX analysis (Si + O signature without Al for colloidal silica; presence of Al for matrix features) combined with a re-cleaning or re-polishing test is the most efficient diagnostic approach. The absence of melt-pool patterns in SEM confirms that the surface has not been etched, and chemical etching with Keller's reagent (~60 s immersion) is the standard procedure to reveal the characteristic fishscale melt-pool morphology in LPBF AlSi10Mg.
